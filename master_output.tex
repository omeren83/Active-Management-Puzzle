\noindent\textbf{Comparison with \textcite{FamaFrench2010}.} The FF (2010) sample
spans Jan~1984--Sep~2006 (270 months), of which the present subperiod covers
only the second half (141 months). Differences in point estimates between this
appendix and Tables~I--III of FF~(2010) should therefore not be attributed to
methodological discrepancies but to (i) the absence of the 1984--1994 window,
during which the cross-section of U.S. equity mutual funds exhibited a more
favourable mean alpha than in the post-1995 era, and (ii) sample-construction
differences---this study uses LSEG share-class data with the
\textcite{Evans2010} incubation correction, whereas FF~(2010) draw on the CRSP
Mutual Fund Database aggregated to fund-level. The qualitative conclusions
of FF~(2010)---that the cross-sectional distribution of $t(\hat{\alpha})$
lies materially to the left of the bootstrap zero-skill null, that
$\hat{\pi}_0$ is high, and that VW net alphas are negative and statistically
significant---are expected to be reproduced; precise magnitudes need not be.

\clearpage
\section*{Appendix D: Sub-Period Analysis}
\addcontentsline{toc}{section}{Appendix D: Sub-Period Analysis}
\label{app:subperiod}
\setcounter{table}{0}
\renewcommand{\thetable}{D.\arabic{table}}
\setcounter{figure}{0}
\renewcommand{\thefigure}{D.\arabic{figure}}

The rolling alpha series in Figure~\ref{fig:rolling_alphas} exhibits pronounced
non-stationarity across the full December~1994--February~2026 sample. To
partition the sample into
economically coherent sub-periods without imposing thresholds \textit{a priori},
we apply the multiple structural break test of \textcite{Bai1998} and
\textcite{Bai2003} to the monthly cross-sectional equal-weighted mean of the
36-month rolling \textcite{Carhart1997} gross alpha for active funds --- the same
underlying series plotted (after 12-month smoothing) in
Figure~\ref{fig:rolling_alphas}. The test is applied to the raw, unsmoothed
series; smoothing the input would introduce additional moving-average serial
correlation on top of the overlap-induced autocorrelation already present in
rolling-window estimates, overstating the precision of detected breaks.

The model is a pure mean-shift specification, $y_t = \mu_i + \varepsilon_t$
for $t$ in segment $i$, with a minimum segment length of 36 months (matching
the rolling-window width) and up to five breaks tested. The optimal number of
breaks is selected by the Bayesian Information Criterion (BIC). Confidence
intervals for break dates use Newey--West heteroskedasticity- and
autocorrelation-consistent standard errors with a data-driven lag, following
the bandwidth rule of \textcite{Andrews1991}.

\subsection*{D.1 \ Structural Break Test Results}

Figure~\ref{fig:breaktest} plots the smoothed active-fund rolling alpha series
together with the Bai--Perron break-date estimates (dashed red verticals),
95\,\% HAC confidence intervals (shaded bands), and regime-mean step function
(solid red). Table~D.1 reports break dates, confidence intervals, and mean
alpha per segment.

\noindent The five-break solution selected by BIC identifies four tight,
economically interpretable breaks (Breaks~1--4, CIs spanning at most ten
months each) and one wider break at Jan~2023 (Break~5, CI spanning
31 months). Breaks~1--3 partition the pre-GFC era into its components:
the late-1990s tail (Period~1, $\bar{\alpha} = -0.59\%$), the dot-com peak
(Period~2, $\bar{\alpha} = +1.67\%$), and the post-crash recovery phase
(Period~3, $\bar{\alpha} = -0.80\%$). These oscillations cluster tightly
around zero on net and each regime spans 36--37 months --- below the
60-month minimum recommended by \textcite{BarrasScailletWermers2010} for
reliable bootstrap inference --- and are therefore collapsed into a single
pre-2006 regime (P1) in the sub-period analysis. Break~4 (Nov~2011,
CI: Jun~2011--Feb~2012) marks the onset of persistent alpha compression
and produces the largest regime-mean shift in the sample
($+1.02\%$ to $-1.13\%$). Break~5 (Jan~2023, CI: Dec~2021--Jul~2024) marks
a further intensification of compression; its 36-month post-break segment
is insufficient for separate bootstrap inference and is consequently pooled
with Period~5 in the sub-period tables, though the break itself is
retained in the figure and this table as part of the formal test output.
Accordingly, the sub-period analysis in Section~D.2 uses \textbf{two
thresholds}: \textbf{Dec~2005} (separating the volatile pre-GFC era from
the clean positive-alpha era) and \textbf{Nov~2011} (marking the structural
break in the alpha-generating process).

This appendix presents two robustness checks for each behavioral hypothesis
estimated in Section~5. The primary specification reported in the main body
uses sentiment and state variables lagged one period

estimation sample as the primary specification, so coefficient differences
across tables reflect only the spec change (timing or fixed-effect
structure), not sample composition.

\subsection*{F.1 \ Timing Robustness: Lagged State Variables}

The primary specification measures sentiment and margin-debt regime indicators contemporaneously with the dependent flow variable, consistent with \textcite{BakerWurgler2007}. \textcite{HuangEtal2015} use one-period lags in their return-predictability tests. This subsection re-estimates each behavioral hypothesis with all state variables measured at $t-1$ rather than at $t$. Treating fund flows as decisions taken in response to information available during the month, contemporaneous measurement is the more defensible default; the lagged specification is reported here to confirm that primary results are not artefacts of the timing assumption.

\clearpage
\section*{Appendix G: Activeness / Lottery Proxy Validation}
\addcontentsline{toc}{section}{Appendix G: Activeness / Lottery Proxy Validation}
\label{app:proxy_validation}
\setcounter{table}{0}
\renewcommand{\thetable}{G.\arabic{table}}

The proposal frames H3 around the ``distinctiveness'' or ``lottery''
attribute of an actively managed fund --- the dimension along which
investors irrationally substitute psychological for financial utility. The
ideal regressor in the proposal's Equation~11 is the holdings-based Active
Share of \textcite{CremersPetajisto2009}, which the LSEG data tier
available for this study cannot construct. This appendix documents the
diagnostic procedure used to select an alternative proxy from four
candidate measures.

The four candidates differ in which behavioural channel they isolate.
\textit{ActR2}, defined as $1 - R^2$ from the rolling 36-month Carhart
regression underlying the alpha estimation in Chapter~4, captures
\textit{statistical activeness}: the fraction of return variation
unexplained by the systematic factor benchmark. \textcite{AmihudGoyenko2013}
establish ActR2 as the standard fund-flow-literature proxy when holdings
data are unavailable and document a positive ActR2--alpha relationship
that this section replicates as a validation check. \textit{ActSkew},
the sample skewness of the trailing 36~monthly gross returns, captures
realised return asymmetry along the entire distribution; positive
ActSkew can reflect either a fat right tail (the lottery property of
interest) or a thin left tail (downside protection), and the measure
does not distinguish these. \textit{MAX12}, the maximum monthly gross
return over the trailing 12~months, isolates the magnitude of the
single largest realised gain --- the most direct right-tail measure
constructible from monthly fund return data. The original
\textcite{ChengEtal2025} construction uses daily MAX over a one-month
window, but their robustness specifications confirm that lower-frequency
analogues preserve the lottery-demand signal. \textit{UpSkew}, the
sample skewness restricted to returns above the rolling in-window mean
and scaled by upside semi-deviation, is a custom right-tail-only
measure included to test whether the asymmetry signal in ActSkew is
driven specifically by the right tail (which is what cumulative
prospect theory predicts investors should care about) rather than by
the full third moment.

Table~\ref{tab:activeness_proxy} reports the diagnostic horse race.
Sample correlations (Test~A) reveal that the four measures capture
distinct dimensions of fund-level heterogeneity: pairwise correlations
range from $-0.21$ to $+0.22$ within fund, ruling out trivial
substitution. Coverage (Test~B) is comparable across measures, with
$81$--$93\%$ of fund-months populated. The univariate flow regressions
(Test~C, both fixed-effect structures) show that ActR2 has no
detectable relationship with subsequent flows ($t = 0.47$ under
cross-fund identification, $t = -1.01$ under within-fund
identification), while ActSkew, MAX12, and UpSkew each predict flows
with $t$-statistics exceeding conventional thresholds. The horse race
(Test~D), in which all four measures are estimated jointly, identifies
ActSkew ($t = 3.70$) and MAX12 ($t = 2.58$) as carrying distinctive
flow-predictive content; UpSkew is statistically subsumed by these two
($t = 0.62$), and ActR2 is mechanically subsumed (point estimate
$2.3 \times 10^{-6}$). The future-alpha test (Test~E) confirms that
ActR2 retains its \textcite{AmihudGoyenko2013} role as a positive
predictor of $12$-month-ahead Carhart alpha ($t = 7.16$), validating
the construction of the variable; ActSkew, by contrast, predicts
\textit{lower} future alpha ($t = -2.64$), indicating that the skewness
signal investors chase is uncorrelated with risk-adjusted skill.

The H3 test specifications in Section~5.4 retain three of the four
candidates: ActR2 (the statistical-activeness baseline whose null in
flow regressions itself constitutes a behavioural finding), ActSkew
(the strongest flow-predictor that is also negatively associated with
future alpha), and MAX12 (an alternative right-tail proxy with
