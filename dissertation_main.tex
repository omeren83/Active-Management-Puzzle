% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------
% Math-Roman font correction.
%
% Problem (chunk 5.A residual, R1 in master log):
%   With unicode-math + STIX Two Math, the macro \text{...} dispatches to
%   \symrm{...}, which renders using STIX Two Math's Roman alphabet. That
%   alphabet is visibly HEAVIER than the surrounding Times New Roman text font,
%   producing the "subscripts look bold" perception flagged across the List of
%   Symbols, Eq. (3.1), Eq. (3.4), Fig. 4.2 area, body pp. 51, 52, 72, 93, 94,
%   155, 180, and 41 enumerated tables.
%
% Fix:
%   Globally redefine \text{...} to expand to \text{...}. The \text{} macro
%   switches to the surrounding text font (Times New Roman) at the math size,
%   producing the same upright Roman glyphs the rest of the thesis uses.
%
% Effect:
%   - Existing \text{MKT}, \text{SMB}, \text{HML}, \text{MOM},
%     \text{LOW}, \text{MID}, \text{HIGH}, \text{SENT}, \text{AAII},
%     \text{MD,Det}, \text{INV-PCR}, \text{TNA}, \text{Flow},
%     \text{Age}, \text{sd}, \text{gross}, \text{net}, etc. all
%     render in Times Roman, matching surrounding text.
%   - Tables that already use \text{...} are unaffected.
%   - Operator names emitted via \operatorname{} and \DeclareMathOperator{}
%     are NOT affected (they use \mathop, not \mathrm).
%
% No per-file edits required. Math content, math sizing, math italic for
% Greek/variables, sub/superscript spacing — all unchanged.
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------
\AtBeginDocument{%
  \renewcommand{\mathrm}[1]{\text{#1}}%
}

ABSTRACT

\vspace{12pt}

The Active Management Puzzle

\vspace{12pt}
\end{center}

\noindent Despite three decades of evidence that actively managed U.S. equity mutual funds underperform passive benchmarks net of fees, retail capital continues to flow into active vehicles. This dissertation investigates whether this persistence reflects the rational equilibrium of \textcite{BerkGreen2004} or requires behavioral explanations. The study examines 3,764 U.S. domestic equity mutual fund primary share classes (one per fund) from December 1994 through February 2026, using fund-level data from the Bloomberg Terminal and LSEG Workspace subscriptions of Boğaziçi University's Management Department. Performance is estimated via the \textcite{Carhart1997} four-factor model with Newey-West standard errors. Active funds deliver an equal-weighted net alpha of -1.361\% per annum (significant at 1\%) and a value-weighted alpha of -0.900\%. The value-weighted result approaches zero, consistent with \textcite{FamaFrench2010} evidence that rational capital allocation limits investor exposure to underperformers. However, the Fama and French (2010) bootstrap rejects zero skill in the left tail at all percentiles below the median, and the \textcite{BarrasScailletWermers2010} false-discovery decomposition reveals 77.8\% zero-alpha funds, 21.1\% genuinely unskilled funds, and no detectable skilled mass. A \textcite{Bai1998,Bai2003} structural break test identifies a September 2011 regime shift after which alpha generation collapses. Four behavioral hypotheses are tested: sentiment-driven flows, disposition effects linked to margin debt, lottery-preference flows (proxied by trailing maximum return), and fee elasticity. The first three mechanisms generate significant flow distortions; fee elasticity conforms to rational predictions. The evidence supports a hybrid interpretation: value-weighted outcomes reflect rational capital allocation, while the persistence of many small, underperforming funds reflects behavioral frictions that intensify during high-sentiment periods.

\chapter{INTRODUCTION}
\label{ch:introduction}

\noindent The retail mutual-fund industry holds an empirical position that is
difficult to reconcile with any clean reading of either classical or
behavioral finance. Three decades of academic evidence converge on the conclusion that the average active U.S. equity mutual fund underperforms risk-adjusted benchmarks net of fees by an economically meaningful margin, with point estimates in the major published studies ranging from approximately 80 to 120 basis points per annum
\parencite{Jensen1968,Malkiel1995,Carhart1997,Wermers2000,FamaFrench2010,BarrasScailletWermers2010}.
The cross-section is heterogeneous; some skill exists; but the median net
alpha is reliably negative and the industry's aggregate Sharpe ratio,
weighted by capital, is no better than a low-cost index fund's. Investors,
in turn, behave as if they have read the literature: assets under management
in U.S.\ index mutual funds and exchange-traded funds first crossed the
fifty-percent threshold of total domestic equity fund assets in 2019, and
the share continues to rise \parencite{ICI2020}. Yet several trillion dollars remain in active
vehicles, redemption rates respond to performance only mildly and
asymmetrically \parencite{SirriTufano1998,ChevalierEllison1997}, and new
active funds continue to be launched at a rate that materially exceeds the
liquidation rate even in periods when the cross-sectional alpha distribution
is shifting downward. This is the active management puzzle, and it is the
empirical fact this dissertation seeks to explain.

The puzzle has two edges. The first is that the average net alpha at the
share-class level is statistically distinguishable from zero in the wrong
direction. This is the fact most often documented in the empirical
literature. The second, less often emphasised, is that the puzzle's location
in the cross-section is asymmetric. When fund returns are aggregated into a
single value-weighted portfolio, the resulting net alpha is small in
magnitude --- in the present sample, -0.900\% per annum, statistically
significant at the 5\% level but economically modest --- because the
capital-weighted distribution is dominated by a handful of large, low-cost,
moderately-active funds whose alpha lies near zero. When fund returns are
aggregated equal-weighted, by contrast, the resulting net alpha is
-1.361\% per annum, materially worse and statistically significant at 1\%.
The gap between the value-weighted and equal-weighted alphas thus locates
the puzzle: it lives in the cross-sectional heterogeneity, not in the
representative dollar invested. This locational fact will play a central
role in the interpretation of the results that follow.

\section{Research motivation and objectives}
\label{sec:motivation}

\noindent The classical reference frame for the puzzle is provided by
\textcite{BerkGreen2004}. Their model imposes three assumptions: managers
have heterogeneous skill and the cross-sectional distribution of skill is
unobservable; managerial skill is subject to decreasing returns to scale;
and capital flows competitively to expected outperformance. Under these
assumptions, the equilibrium expected net alpha at the capital-weighted
level is exactly zero --- competitive flow erodes any genuine skill into
fee revenue, and any apparent persistence in performance is rationally
arbitraged away. The model has the virtue of generating predictions that
match a substantial fraction of the empirical regularities, including the
limited persistence of returns, the convex flow--performance relationship,
and the fact that equilibrium net alpha need not be positive on average.
A close reading of the empirical literature against the Berk--Green model
finds that the rational equilibrium is not, in fact, rejected for
capital-weighted outcomes; what is rejected is the joint claim that the
cross-section is also at equilibrium. \textcite{PastorStambaugh2012} extend
the model to industry-level decreasing returns to scale and argue that the
Berk--Green logic survives that extension; \textcite{PastorStambaughTaylor2015}
provide robust empirical support for both fund-level and industry-level
diseconomies of scale. \textcite{BerkVanBinsbergen2015} go further and
argue that, when measured in dollars rather than basis points, active
managers are skilled --- the average manager extracts approximately
\$3.2 million per year of value-added from capital markets --- and that
this skill is persistent for as long as ten years.

The behavioral alternative reads the same empirical record very
differently. If investors have stable preferences over skewness or
lottery-like payoffs \parencite{Kumar2009,BaliCakiciWhitelaw2011}, if their
flow response to performance is amplified by aggregate sentiment
\parencite{BakerWurgler2006,BakerWurgler2007}, if disposition effects bias
their willingness to redeem from underperformers
\parencite{ShefrinStatman1985,GrinblattKeloharju2009}, or if their fee
sensitivity is heterogeneous and inconsistent with a single rational
Bayesian updating rule, then the persistence of the active-management
industry is not a puzzle requiring rational equilibrium but a
straightforward consequence of investor behavior. The behavioral framework
does not deny that some funds are skilled; it denies that competitive flow
fully arbitrages skill differentials away. The empirical fact that the
equal-weighted and value-weighted net alphas differ materially is, on this
reading, exactly what we should expect: capital concentrates in funds whose
attributes appeal to behavioral preferences as much as in those that
deliver risk-adjusted return.

This dissertation does not adjudicate between the two frameworks at the
level of theory. The frameworks are, at the level of theory, partially
complementary rather than mutually exclusive --- it is logically consistent
to maintain that the capital-weighted outcome is at the Berk--Green
equilibrium while the cross-sectional heterogeneity reflects behavioral
frictions, and indeed this is the empirical position the dissertation
ultimately defends. The objectives are therefore narrower and empirical:

First, to estimate the cross-sectional distribution of alpha and decompose
it into skilled, unskilled, and zero-alpha mass under modern
methodological standards, on a panel that materially extends the sample
period of the canonical published studies. The sample assembled here runs
from December 1994 through February 2026, a window that includes
\textcite{FamaFrench2010}'s sample (1984--2006) only partially but extends
fifteen years past the published literature into the post-Global Financial
Crisis regime that has come to dominate active management economics.

Second, to identify whether the alpha-generating process is structurally
stable over this window, or whether it has changed in ways the canonical
literature could not have detected. The structural-break tests of
\textcite{Bai1998,Bai2003}, applied to the cross-sectional mean of rolling
36-month Carhart alphas for active funds, identify a regime shift in
September 2011 after which alpha generation collapses to a level that has
not subsequently recovered. The empirical content of this fact is large
enough to warrant separate treatment in the dissertation's Chapter 4 and
Appendix~H.

Third, to test directly whether four behavioral hypotheses --- sentiment
amplification of flow--performance convexity (H1), disposition / illusion
of control (H2), lottery demand (H3), and fee elasticity (H4) --- generate
detectable signatures in flow--performance dynamics that are not present
under the Berk--Green null. The hypotheses are tested on a panel of
approximately 235{,}000 fund-month observations using the
\textcite{SirriTufano1998} piecewise rank specification, augmented with
sentiment, margin-debt, and lottery-proxy interactions, and identified
within fund using fund fixed effects with two-way clustering on fund and
calendar month \parencite{Petersen2009}.

Fourth, to translate any detected behavioral signature into welfare-relevant
units. The Psychological Premium Framework developed in
Chapter~\ref{ch:behavioral} computes the marginal rate of substitution
between performance rank and the lottery proxy implied by the flow
indifference condition, and converts this rate into basis points of annual
Carhart alpha that investors are revealed to be willing to forgo for an
incremental standard deviation of lottery exposure.

\section{Contributions}
\label{sec:contributions}

\noindent The dissertation makes four contributions to the empirical
literature on active mutual fund performance.

First, the empirical core extends the
\textcite{FamaFrench2010} bootstrap methodology and the
\textcite{BarrasScailletWermers2010} false-discovery decomposition to the
December 1994--February 2026 sample, with a full-period zero-skill
bootstrap of B = 10{,}000 calendar-month resamples and a Storey false
discovery rate decomposition at $\gamma^* = 0.20$. Appendix~G replicates
the principal results of \textcite{FamaFrench2010} on the maximum overlap
between the present sample and theirs (January 1995--September 2006)
using identical estimation kernels, providing an external validity check.

Second, the dissertation documents a structural break in active fund alpha
generation in September 2011 with a 95\% Newey--West HAC confidence
interval of March 2011--December 2011. The break separates a positive-alpha
regime ($\bar{\alpha} = +0.74\%$ per annum, January 2006--September 2011)
from a persistently negative regime ($\bar{\alpha} = -1.39\%$ per annum,
October 2011--January 2026). The post-break regime is characterised by a
collapse in the Storey $\hat{\pi}_0$ estimate from approximately 81.4\% to
60.8\% and a corresponding rise in the genuinely unskilled mass
$T^-_{\gamma^*}$ from approximately 10.8\% to 35.4\% --- the strongest single piece
of regime-specific evidence in the dissertation.

Third, the dissertation introduces a unified four-hypothesis behavioral
framework (H1--H4) and tests it against the Berk--Green rational null on a
single, carefully cleaned panel. The framework distinguishes between
hypotheses identifying the same flow-performance moments through different
mechanisms, and is designed to allow the data to speak about which
behavioral channels carry detectable signal and which do not. Of the four
hypotheses tested, three (H1, H2 in its margin-debt form, H3) generate
detectable distortions of flow--performance dynamics consistent with
behavioral prediction; one (H4) is consistent with the rational null and
is reported as such.

Fourth, the dissertation develops the Psychological Premium Framework, in
which the marginal rate of substitution between performance rank and the
lottery proxy implied by the flow indifference condition is converted into
welfare-relevant alpha basis points using the momentum-quintile
rank-to-alpha mapping documented in Section~\ref{sec:portfolio_sorts}. The
implied premium for the maximum-return lottery proxy is approximately 13
basis points of annual alpha per standard deviation under low-sentiment
regimes and approximately 59 basis points under high-sentiment regimes ---
a 4.5-fold amplification consistent with the
\textcite{BakerWurgler2006,BakerWurgler2007} sentiment-modulation channel.

\section{Structure of the dissertation}
\label{sec:structure}

\noindent The remainder of the dissertation is organised as follows.
Chapter~\ref{ch:literature} reviews the active management puzzle
literature, the Berk--Green rational equilibrium, the empirical performance

\chapter{LITERATURE REVIEW}
\label{ch:literature}

\noindent The literature this dissertation draws on intersects three
traditions. The first is the empirical mutual fund performance literature,
which begins with \textcite{Jensen1968} and proceeds through
\textcite{Carhart1997}, \textcite{Wermers2000}, \textcite{KosowskiTimmermannWermersWhite2006},
\textcite{FamaFrench2010}, \textcite{BarrasScailletWermers2010}, and the
recent decompositions of \textcite{BarrasGagliardiniScaillet2022}. This
tradition has been concerned principally with measurement: what is the
average alpha, what is the cross-sectional dispersion, and what fraction of
the apparent skill survives a properly constructed null distribution? The
second is the rational equilibrium tradition initiated by
\textcite{BerkGreen2004} and extended by
\textcite{PastorStambaugh2012}, \textcite{PastorStambaughTaylor2015} and 
\textcite{BerkVanBinsbergen2015,BerkVanBinsbergen2016}. This tradition has
been concerned with reconciling the measurement evidence with rational
investor behavior under decreasing returns to scale. The third is the
behavioral mutual fund literature, which spans the flow--performance
sensitivity work of \textcite{SirriTufano1998} and \textcite{ChevalierEllison1997},
the sentiment evidence of \textcite{BakerWurgler2006,BakerWurgler2007} and
\textcite{StambaughYuYuan2012}, the lottery-demand evidence of
\textcite{Kumar2009} and \textcite{BaliCakiciWhitelaw2011}, and the
disposition / illusion of control evidence of \textcite{ShefrinStatman1985}
and \textcite{GrinblattKeloharju2009}, among others. These traditions have
operated largely in parallel; the empirical contribution of this dissertation
is to bring the three to bear on the same panel.

\section{The active management puzzle: Framing}
\label{sec:puzzle_framing}

\noindent The puzzle is best framed as a joint claim about three empirical
facts. First, the average net alpha at the share-class level is negative
and statistically distinguishable from zero in a panel that spans
multiple market regimes \parencite{Carhart1997,FamaFrench2010,BarrasScailletWermers2010}.
Second, capital flows respond to past performance with a convexity that
favours past winners \parencite{SirriTufano1998,ChevalierEllison1997}; new
money is allocated more aggressively to top-decile performers than is
withdrawn from bottom-decile performers. Third, the industry has not
contracted at a rate consistent with rational learning over the
documentation period; if anything, the active-management share of total
assets reached its peak well after the canonical performance evidence was
published. These three facts are jointly puzzling because each has a clean
explanation in isolation, but no single explanation reconciles all three
simultaneously.

\subsection{The Berk--Green equilibrium}
\label{sec:bg_equilibrium}

\noindent The \textcite{BerkGreen2004} model produces a remarkably clean
reconciliation. The model assumes that managers have heterogeneous skill,
drawn from a population whose mean is uncertain to investors; that
managerial skill is subject to decreasing returns to scale, so that the
gross alpha generated by a manager is a strictly decreasing function of
fund size; and that capital flows competitively to the manager whose
expected gross alpha exceeds her fee. Under these assumptions, the
equilibrium is one in which capital flows into a fund until its size has
absorbed the manager's skill --- that is, until the marginal dollar of
capital generates an expected gross alpha equal to the management fee.
The expected net alpha is therefore zero in equilibrium, exactly because
flow is responsive to performance. The model's empirical predictions
include: (i) the expected net alpha is zero on average at the
capital-weighted level; (ii) past performance does not predict future
performance because skilled managers absorb capital that pushes them back
to zero net alpha; (iii) the flow--performance relationship is convex
because investors update their beliefs about manager skill via Bayesian
inference and the value function has a kink; and (iv) the cross-sectional
distribution of skill is reflected in the cross-sectional distribution of
fund size, not in the cross-sectional distribution of net alpha.

A direct empirical test of the Berk--Green model in dollar terms is
provided by \textcite{BerkVanBinsbergen2015}, who measure managerial skill
as the product of a fund's gross alpha and its lagged assets under
management --- the dollar value the manager extracts from capital markets.
On U.S.\ data over 1977--2011, they find that the average active manager
extracts approximately \$3.2 million per year of value-added, that this
skill is persistent for as long as ten years, and that current
compensation predicts future performance. These findings are difficult to
reconcile with anything other than the existence of genuine money
management skill in the population, even though the average net alpha to
investors is reliably negative in the same data. The BG framework, when
read with this metric, is therefore not rejected for capital-weighted
outcomes; what would reject the framework is evidence that the
cross-sectional alpha distribution is materially different from what the
model implies, conditional on the joint distribution of skill and size.

\subsection{Behavioral alternatives}
\label{sec:behavioral_alternatives}

\noindent The behavioral alternative does not necessarily contradict the
Berk--Green model at the capital-weighted level; it disputes the model's
ability to account for the cross-sectional pattern. Four behavioral
channels have been documented in the literature, each of which is
hypothesised in this dissertation to leave a detectable signature in
flow--performance dynamics that is not present under the rational null.

The first is investor sentiment as documented by
\textcite{BakerWurgler2006,BakerWurgler2007}. Their composite index, built
from the closed-end fund discount, NYSE share turnover, the number and
average first-day return of IPOs, the equity share in new issues, and the
dividend premium, captures aggregate optimism that is difficult to
attribute to fundamentals. \textcite{StambaughYuYuan2012} show that
sentiment forecasts cross-sectional return differentials between
overpriced and underpriced stocks. The hypothesis tested in this
dissertation (H1) is that sentiment amplifies the flow--performance
convexity documented by \textcite{SirriTufano1998}: high-sentiment regimes
should generate larger flow-into-winners responses without a corresponding
flow-out-of-losers response.

The second is the disposition effect of \textcite{ShefrinStatman1985} and
the closely related illusion of control documented in the experimental
literature. The disposition effect predicts that investors are reluctant
to redeem from underperformers because doing so realises a paper loss; in
the mutual-fund context this generates a flatter slope of the
flow--performance regression in the loser segment than the rational null
predicts. \textcite{BrunnermeierPedersen2009} provide a competing
mechanism in which margin debt regimes generate a positive --- rather
than negative --- loser-segment slope through forced redemptions. H2 of
this dissertation tests the disposition / illusion-of-control prediction
against the Brunnermeier--Pedersen alternative on the same data.

The third is the lottery demand evidence of \textcite{Kumar2009} and the
maximum-return effect of \textcite{BaliCakiciWhitelaw2011}. Under
cumulative prospect theory \parencite{TverskyKahneman1992}, investors
overweight low-probability extreme positive outcomes; this generates a
preference for assets whose return distribution exhibits positive
skewness or large maximum returns, even at the cost of mean-variance
efficiency. In equity markets, this preference produces a documented
negative MAX--alpha relationship at the stock level
\parencite{BaliCakiciWhitelaw2011}. The hypothesis tested in this
dissertation (H3) is that the same preference operates in the
mutual-fund flow domain: funds with high realised lottery characteristics
attract disproportionate flow under high-sentiment regimes, conditional
on rational performance signals.

The fourth is fee elasticity. Under a rational Bayesian updating rule,
investors should be uniformly negatively elastic to fees: a higher fee, all
else equal, lowers the manager's expected net alpha and should reduce
flow. The behavioral alternative permits heterogeneity: some clienteles
may interpret high fees as a signal of quality
\parencite{DelGuercioTkac2008}, others may exhibit fee-insensitivity
through inertia \parencite{ChoiLaibsonMadrian2010}. H4 tests whether
flow--fee elasticity varies across performance segments and sentiment
regimes in a manner inconsistent with a single rational updating rule.

\section{Mutual fund performance: Empirical evidence}
\label{sec:performance_evidence}

\noindent The empirical literature on mutual fund performance has matured
substantially since \textcite{Jensen1968}, but the central conclusions
have proven robust to repeated re-examination on different samples and
methodologies.

\subsection{Gross vs.\ net alphas and fund-level skill}
\label{sec:gross_net}

\noindent The distinction between gross and net alpha matters for both
measurement and interpretation. The gross alpha measures the manager's
contribution to investor return before fees; the net alpha measures the
investor's residual after fees are deducted. \textcite{Carhart1997}
documents that the apparent persistence of mutual fund returns is
primarily attributable to momentum exposure rather than to manager
skill, and that fees and transaction costs explain much of the negative
average net alpha at the share-class level. \textcite{Wermers2000}
employs a holdings-based decomposition and finds that stock selection
contributes approximately 130 basis points per year to gross returns
before costs --- a contribution that is substantially offset by expenses,
transaction costs, and underperformance of non-equity holdings, yielding
negative net alpha. \textcite{KacperczykSialmZheng2008} decompose fund
returns further by comparing reported returns to holdings-implied returns,
documenting a persistent ``unobserved actions'' component attributable to
intra-quarter trading, market timing, and derivative positions. Their
decomposition reveals that the modest positive gross alpha documented in
earlier holdings-based studies masks offsetting components: managers
demonstrate tactical trading skill but poor long-term stock selection,
with the net effect remaining consistent with zero aggregate gross alpha
and negative net alpha after fees.

The asymmetry between equal-weighted (EW) and value-weighted (VW)
aggregations is central to the empirical reading of the cross-section.
\textcite{FamaFrench2010} report that, on their 1984--2006 sample, the
EW average net alpha for active funds is materially negative while the
VW analogue is closer to zero --- a pattern consistent with the Berk-Green prediction that 
large funds attract capital until their net alpha approaches zero, 
while small underperforming funds persist in the left tail. The present dissertation
finds the same EW--VW asymmetry on a sample that extends fifteen years
beyond the FF (2010) window, and the locational interpretation is the
same: the puzzle is in the cross-section, not in the representative
dollar.

\subsection{Bootstrap inference for fund-level skill}
\label{sec:bs_lit}

\noindent Inference about whether observed cross-sectional alphas reflect
genuine skill or sampling variation requires a properly constructed null
distribution. \textcite{KosowskiTimmermannWermersWhite2006} (KTWW) propose
a calendar-month bootstrap in which each fund's residuals are resampled
with replacement, the alpha is set to zero by construction, and the
factor model is re-estimated on the simulated series. The resulting
distribution of simulated $t$-statistics provides the percentile under
the zero-skill null against which the empirical t-statistic is compared.
\textcite{FamaFrench2010} refine the procedure by resampling calendar
months across all funds simultaneously rather than bootstrapping each
fund independently --- this preserves the cross-sectional dependence
induced by common factor exposures and avoids the over-rejection that
fund-by-fund resampling generates. The FF (2010) bootstrap is now the
standard reference for inference at the fund level, and is the procedure
adopted in this dissertation.

The choice of standard error matters for the bootstrap as much as for
the empirical estimation. \textcite{NeweyWest1987}'s heteroskedasticity-
and autocorrelation-consistent (HAC) covariance estimator with a
six-month lag is applied symmetrically to actual and bootstrap-simulated
$t$-statistics in this dissertation, following the recommendation of
\textcite{FamaFrench2010}. Asymmetric application of HAC to actual and
ordinary least squares to simulated $t$-statistics --- a methodological
error that has appeared in the literature --- materially distorts the
inference and is avoided here.

\subsection{False discovery decomposition}
\label{sec:fdr_lit}

\noindent The bootstrap procedure provides p-values fund-by-fund but
does not, on its own, separate the cross-sectional distribution into
skilled, unskilled, and zero-alpha mass. The
\textcite{Storey2002} false discovery rate (FDR) framework, applied to
mutual fund performance by \textcite{BarrasScailletWermers2010} (BSW),
fills this gap. The procedure estimates the population proportion of
zero-alpha funds, $\hat{\pi}_0$, from the density of $p$-values
exceeding a tuning parameter $\lambda$ (set to $0.5$ following the
literature standard), and then decomposes the observed significant tails
$S^-_\gamma$ and $S^+_\gamma$ at threshold $\gamma$ into the false
discoveries $F_\gamma = \hat{\pi}_0 \cdot \gamma / 2$ expected from the
zero-alpha mass and the genuinely unskilled / skilled mass
$T^-_\gamma = S^-_\gamma - F_\gamma$ and $T^+_\gamma = S^+_\gamma - F_\gamma$.

BSW (2010) report that the genuinely skilled mass is small but
detectable in their 1975--2006 sample. The present dissertation finds
that on the December 1994--February 2026 sample $T^+_{\gamma^*}$ is
slightly negative at the standard $\gamma^* = 0.20$ threshold, indicating
that right-tail significance does not exceed the false-discovery rate
even at this comparatively permissive cutoff. The interpretation is not
that skill does not exist --- it almost certainly does, in the
\textcite{BerkVanBinsbergen2015} sense --- but that the population mass of
managers whose net-alpha is detectably positive in standard hypothesis
tests is, on this sample, indistinguishable from the false-discovery rate
at any threshold the data can support.

A more recent approach to fund-level cross-sectional skill, developed by
\textcite{BarrasGagliardiniScaillet2022} (BGS), addresses the standard
errors-in-variables concerns of the BSW procedure and provides a
parametric mixture-model decomposition that does not rely on the Storey
$\hat{\pi}_0$ estimator. BGS find a population skilled mass that is
larger than BSW's --- approximately 14\% of active funds in their U.S.\
panel --- and identify a substantial decline in the proportion of
skilled funds over time, particularly in the post-2000 period. Their
finding is qualitatively consistent with the regime-shift evidence
documented in Chapter~\ref{ch:results} and Appendix~H of the present
dissertation; a direct quantitative comparison is left to future
research.

\section{Fund flows and the flow--performance relation}
\label{sec:flow_perf_lit}

\noindent The seminal documentation of the flow--performance relationship
is \textcite{SirriTufano1998}, who use a piecewise linear specification on
the cross-sectional fractional rank of past performance to identify the
non-linearity that the unconditional flow--performance regression smooths
over. Their central finding --- that flow responds strongly and convexly
to top-decile performance and weakly (and asymmetrically) to bottom-decile
performance --- has been replicated repeatedly. \textcite{ChevalierEllison1997}
provide a theoretical interpretation: the convexity creates implicit
incentives for fund managers to vary risk-taking based on year-to-date
performance, which in turn distorts the cross-section of realised returns. Mid-year winners are incentivized to reduce risk relative to their baseline to preserve their favorable ranking because they stand to lose substantial inflows if they fall out of the top tier, whereas mid-year losers are incentivized to increase risk to improve their ranking because the flow penalty from falling further is small relative to the potential flow gains from jumping to the top.

\textcite{HuangWeiYan2007} document that participation costs and search
frictions generate heterogeneity in flow sensitivity across investor
clienteles. \textcite{ChristoffersenMustoWermers2014} provide a
comprehensive survey of the literature; \textcite{Goldstein2017} extends
the analysis to bond mutual funds and documents that the convexity
asymmetry inverts under certain market conditions.

The relationship between sentiment and the flow--performance relationship
is the focus of \textcite{BenRephaelKandelWohl2012}, who show that the
sensitivity of flow to past performance varies with aggregate market tone,
with stronger sensitivity in optimistic regimes. More recent work by
\textcite{WuZhang2024} on Chinese mutual funds finds that positive
sentiment relieves redemption anxiety and amplifies flow into winners ---
the same directional prediction as H1 in this dissertation, on a
different market.

\section{Behavioral explanations}
\label{sec:behavioral_lit}

\noindent The four behavioral hypotheses tested in Chapter 5 each have
substantial individual support in the literature.

\subsection{Investor sentiment and fund flows}
\label{sec:sentiment_lit}

\noindent The \textcite{BakerWurgler2006} sentiment index has become the
de facto standard for U.S.\ aggregate sentiment in the asset pricing
literature; it is constructed from six market-based proxies and
orthogonalised against a small set of macroeconomic controls.
\textcite{BakerWurgler2007} provide a primer that includes the timing
convention adopted in this dissertation (contemporaneous measurement of
sentiment for predicting next-period flows). The sentiment effect on
mutual fund flows is documented by
\textcite{IndroSentiment2004,BenRephaelKandelWohl2012,WuZhang2024}, with
the consistent finding that high sentiment amplifies flow sensitivity to
performance.

\subsection{Disposition effect and illusion of control}
\label{sec:disposition_lit}

\noindent The disposition effect of \textcite{ShefrinStatman1985} ---
the tendency to ride losers and realise winners --- is documented in
direct individual-investor data by \textcite{Odean1998} and
\textcite{GrinblattKeloharju2009}. Applied to mutual fund flows, the
prediction is that bottom-decile flow-out is dampened relative to the
rational benchmark. The closely related illusion-of-control effect
predicts that this dampening is amplified during periods of high
margin-debt accumulation, when retail investors most strongly project
forward-looking control over their investment outcomes. The contrasting
\textcite{BrunnermeierPedersen2009} mechanism, in which margin debt
generates forced redemptions and therefore amplifies bottom-decile
flow-out, is tested directly in H2.

\subsection{Lottery demand and skewness preference}
\label{sec:lottery_lit}

\noindent The lottery preference literature begins with the cumulative
prospect theory of \textcite{TverskyKahneman1992}, who establish that
investors overweight low-probability extreme outcomes. Empirical evidence
in equity markets is provided by \textcite{Kumar2009}, who shows that
individual investor demand for lottery-like stocks is concentrated among
demographically identifiable groups, and by
\textcite{BaliCakiciWhitelaw2011} (BCW), who document a negative cross-sectional relationship 
between the maximum daily return over the past
month and expected future returns. \textcite{BarberisHuang2008} provide
the theoretical foundation in terms of probability weighting.
\textcite{Boyer2010} extend the analysis to expected idiosyncratic
skewness. In the mutual fund domain, \textcite{ChengEtal2025} construct a
fund-level lottery measure following BCW and document that high-MAX
funds attract disproportionate retail flow at the cost of subsequent
underperformance --- the precise prediction H3 of this dissertation tests.



\subsection{Fee elasticity and behavioural pricing}
\label{sec:fee_lit}

\noindent The literature on flow--fee elasticity is mixed. Under the
\textcite{BerkGreen2004} rational benchmark, fee elasticity should be
strictly negative and homogeneous across investor clienteles.
\textcite{ChoiLaibsonMadrian2010} provide experimental evidence that even
sophisticated investors fail to minimise fees when selecting among
otherwise-identical S\&P 500 index funds, suggesting fee inertia.
\textcite{DelGuercioTkac2008} show that institutional and retail
clienteles exhibit materially different fee sensitivities. H4 tests
whether flow--fee elasticity is heterogeneous across performance
segments and sentiment regimes in a manner inconsistent with a single
rational updating rule.

\section{Synthesis and research gap}
\label{sec:synthesis}

\noindent The literature reviewed in this chapter establishes three
findings that the present dissertation takes as its starting point. First,
the average net alpha at the share-class level in U.S.\ active equity
mutual funds is reliably negative, with the EW--VW asymmetry locating the
puzzle in the cross-section rather than in the representative dollar.
Second, the rational equilibrium of \textcite{BerkGreen2004} provides a
clean reconciliation at the capital-weighted level but does not, on its
own, account for the cross-sectional heterogeneity that drives the EW
underperformance. Third, four behavioral channels --- sentiment, lottery
demand, disposition / illusion of control, and fee elasticity --- are
individually supported in the empirical literature.

The research gap this dissertation addresses is the absence of a unified
test of the behavioral channels against the BG rational null on the same
panel, with state-of-the-art measurement of the alpha distribution
(FF (2010) bootstrap, BSW (2010) decomposition), state-of-the-art
identification of regime shifts (Bai--Perron 1998, 2003), and
state-of-the-art inference for the panel regressions
(\textcite{Petersen2009} two-way clustering on fund and calendar month).
The post-Global Financial Crisis regime-shift evidence in Chapter~4 and
Appendix~H further motivates the question, because the canonical
published studies of \textcite{FamaFrench2010,BarrasScailletWermers2010}
predate the regime that has come to dominate active management economics.
The empirical contribution of the dissertation lies in addressing this
gap on a panel that materially extends both the time-series and the
cross-sectional coverage of the canonical studies.

\chapter{DATA AND METHODOLOGY}
\label{ch:data}

\noindent The empirical analysis is conducted on a panel of 3{,}764 U.S.\
domestic equity mutual fund primary share classes (one per fund), covering
December 1994 through February 2026. Fund-level monthly time-series data and the bulk of the
fund-level static attributes were extracted via the Bloomberg Excel API
of the Department of Management's Bloomberg Terminal subscription at
Boğaziçi University. A complementary static-data export from the
Department's London Stock Exchange Group (LSEG) Workspace subscription was
matched to the Bloomberg roster on the International Securities
Identification Number (ISIN) to obtain Lipper investment-objective codes
(where Bloomberg's coverage was incomplete) and to retrieve the full set
of benchmark total-return index series at monthly frequency. Where
neither vendor disclosed a benchmark or where vendor disclosure was
demonstrably inconsistent with prospectus filings, the assigned benchmark
was confirmed against the fund's most recent SEC EDGAR filing, annual
report, or fund-family factsheet. Macroeconomic and sentiment time series
were drawn from public sources. The cleaning pipeline that converts the
raw extract into the analytically-distinct working panels is described
in Appendix~\ref{app:data_cleaning}; this chapter focuses on the
methodological choices that drive the empirical results in the chapters
that follow.

\needspace{4\baselineskip}
\section{Data sources and sample construction}
\label{sec:data_sources}

\noindent The raw extract was assembled in stages.

\subsection{Bloomberg primary extraction}
\label{sec:bloomberg_extract}

\noindent The starting universe was extracted via Bloomberg fund screener, restricted to U.S.\ domestic equity mutual
funds, selecting the primary share class for each fund and aggregating
both currently active and historical (closed/inactive) funds. The initial
extract contained 3{,}769 primary share classes meeting the screening
criteria, of which 2{,}329 carried a Bloomberg Actively\_Managed = Y flag
at the most recent disclosure date. Bloomberg supplied identifying
fields (ticker in XXXXX US Equity format, ISIN, fund name,
inception date, closing date, market status), Bloomberg's assigned primary benchmark,
the market-capitalisation focus field
(Mcap\_Focus $\in$ \{Large-cap, Mid-cap, Small-cap, Broad
Market\}), the strategy field (Fund\_Strategy $\in$ \{Value,
Blend, Growth, Bear Market, Long Short, Market Neutral\}), the active
management flag, and a set of static risk/return summary statistics (NAV
tracking error, income ratio, excess return, alpha, beta, Sharpe ratio, information
ratio, holdings count, average market cap, fund-level turnover and
expense ratio).

Following \textcite{KosowskiTimmermannWermersWhite2006} and \textcite{FamaFrench2010}, the
analysis aggregates to the fund level by selecting a single primary
share class per fund, eliminating the redundancy and statistical
non-independence introduced by multiple share classes of the same
underlying fund. Therefore, the 3{,}769 share classes correspond to
3{,}769 distinct funds, with each fund represented by its primary
share class throughout the analysis.

The fund-level monthly time-series fields extracted from Bloomberg are
the gross total return index, the net total return index, the tracking
difference, the net assets and share-class assets, the total fund assets
across all share classes, the number of shares outstanding, and the
Bloomberg-reported fund flow. The latter is used as a cross-validation
benchmark for the calculated proportional flow described in
Section~\ref{sec:flow_calculation}; the calculated flow is preferred for
the regression analyses because the Bloomberg-reported series exhibits
material missing-month coverage in the early years of the sample.

\subsection{LSEG complementary data}
\label{sec:lseg_extract}

\noindent The Bloomberg static export was insufficient on two dimensions
for the empirical analysis pursued in this dissertation. First, the
Lipper investment-objective code that identifies the size-style peer
group required for the within-Lipper-category fractional ranks of
Section~\ref{sec:flow_calculation} and Chapter~\ref{ch:behavioral} is not
available in the Bloomberg static export. A complementary static-data export was therefore obtained from
the Department's LSEG Workspace subscription. The LSEG export was
matched to the Bloomberg roster on the International Securities
Identification Number (ISIN), with Bloomberg ticker as a fallback
matching key for the small subset of share classes where ISIN was
missing or non-unique. Of the 3{,}764 funds in the merged panel, the
LSEG Lipper Category field is populated for 1{,}712 (45.5\%);
the remainder are resolved by the priority-ordered cascade documented in
Appendix~\ref{app:lipper_assignment}.

Second, the benchmark total-return index series required for the
tracking-error component of the activeness analysis in
Section~\ref{sec:activeness} are not directly available through the
Bloomberg subscription tier accessed for this dissertation. The LSEG
Workspace data terminal provides monthly total-return index series at
the level of granularity required for the analysis: Russell size-style
indices (Russell 1000 / Midcap / 2000 / 3000 in Value, Core, and Growth
variants), MSCI U.S.\ Investable Market Index (IMI) 25/50 sector
sub-indices for the eleven GICS sectors, the FTSE NAREIT All-Equity
REITs index, and a small number of additional sector-specialty indices
required for the Fidelity Select fund family. Three benchmark
substitutions were required due to LSEG subscription tier constraints
on three indices' full-sample coverage: the FTSE NAREIT All-Equity REITs
index substitutes for the MSCI U.S.\ REIT index for Cohen \& Steers
realty funds; the Russell Midcap index substitutes for the S\&P MidCap
400 for 31 funds (17 of which carry the H3 benchmark-required flag); and
the Russell 2000 Value index substitutes for the Russell 2500 Value
index for 10 funds. Each substitution is documented with its rationale
in Appendix~\ref{app:benchmark}; in each case the substitute index
exhibits monthly total-return correlation in excess of 0.97 with the
intended index over the available comparison window, and preserves the
size-style mandate of the substituted index.

In addition to benchmark series, a subset of the macro / sentiment time
series used in the H1--H4 panel regressions of
Chapter~\ref{ch:behavioral} were extracted from their primary issuers
via the LSEG Excel API where the LSEG terminal carries an authoritative
mirror of the issuer's series; for series where LSEG coverage is
incomplete, the time series was obtained directly from the issuer.
Specific sources are documented in Section~\ref{sec:state_variables}.

\subsection{External research and benchmark verification}
\label{sec:external_research}

\noindent The final benchmark assigned to each fund in the
dissertation is derived from four sources, documented in full in
Appendix~\ref{app:benchmark} and summarised here by the confidence
score attached to each assignment. For 17 funds the benchmark is
confirmed verbatim from the fund's annual report or prospectus
(score 10). For 1{,}697 funds it is derived from the LSEG
Lipper-category to standard Russell/MSCI map (score 8). For 660
funds the Bloomberg \texttt{Primary\_Benchmark} field names a
recognised index family directly and is used as-is (score 7). The
remaining 1{,}390 funds (37\% of the 3{,}764-fund full panel) receive
an inferred benchmark: 902 from both Bloomberg \texttt{Mcap\_Focus}
and \texttt{Fund\_Strategy} fields (score 5), 387 from one Bloomberg
attribute field (score 3), and 101 assigned the Russell 3000
default (score 1). Scores 7 and above indicate confirmation from
an authoritative source; scores 5 and below indicate inference from
Bloomberg fund attributes or a default assignment.

Of the 2{,}329 active funds in the working universe, 97 required
active correction of a Bloomberg benchmark misassignment or a
missing benchmark. The single largest correction group concerns the
Fidelity Select sector fund family: Bloomberg had assigned the
S\&P 500 as the primary benchmark for the entire family, while the
funds' annual reports disclose that each Select fund is benchmarked
against the relevant MSCI U.S.\ IMI 25/50 sector sub-index. The
Fidelity Select correction covers 37 funds and is supported by a
benchmark-change history that includes the September 2018 GICS
reclassification (Telecom Services folded into Communication
Services) and the March 2023 GICS reclassification (homebuilders
moved from Real Estate / Materials to Consumer Discretionary).
A second correction group concerns Rydex sector funds, for which
Guggenheim's fund-family documentation confirms that no primary
benchmark is officially declared. For these 14 funds a proxy sector
index was inferred from the fund's GICS sector classification and
flagged as INFERRED in the benchmark assignment workbook. Smaller
correction groups cover Cohen \& Steers realty funds (3 funds,
MSCI U.S.\ REIT confirmed; FTSE NAREIT substitute applied), MFS
Utilities and BULIX (S\&P 500 Utilities Index), and approximately
20 additional sector-specialty funds.

Regardless of whether Bloomberg's \texttt{Primary\_Benchmark} field
required active correction, the dissertation does not use
fund-declared or Bloomberg-assigned benchmarks as individual
performance benchmarks. Following the academic convention of
\textcite{SirriTufano1998}, \textcite{CremersPetajisto2009}, and
\textcite{FamaFrench2010}, each fund is instead assigned a
category-appropriate broad-based Russell index determined by its
Lipper style classification. Three considerations motivate this
choice. First, the Sirri--Tufano fractional performance rank
requires all funds within a style category to be measured against
the same index; fund-specific benchmarks would render within-category
ranks incomparable. Second, fund managers exercise discretion in
selecting their prospectus benchmarks and there is well-documented
evidence of strategic benchmark selection to maximise reported
outperformance \parencite{Sensoy2009}, introducing systematic upward
bias into alpha estimates if manager-selected benchmarks are used.
Third, the Carhart four-factor model attributes returns to systematic
risk factors rather than to a single index, making stylistic
consistency within peer groups more important than the precise
identity of the declared benchmark. The benchmark assignment
workbook therefore records, for each fund, the Russell index
appropriate to its Lipper category rather than the index named in
Bloomberg's \texttt{Primary\_Benchmark} field. For the 660 funds
at confidence score 7, the Bloomberg field happened to name exactly
that Russell index and was used directly as confirmation; for the
1{,}697 funds at score 8, the LSEG Lipper category provided the
same information more directly and Bloomberg's field was not
consulted as the source.

\section{Panel construction}
\label{sec:panel_construction}

\noindent The cleaning pipeline applied to the raw extract is documented
in detail in Appendix~\ref{app:data_cleaning}. Six sequential cleaning
steps are applied: removal of forward-filled (``frozen tail'')
observations that LSEG carries indefinitely past fund closure; exclusion
of funds with no valid return observations after step 1 (29 funds);
exclusion of two confirmed data errors (QWVOX with cumulative gross
return exceeding eleven million percent; VALLCEN with a single-month
$-75.9\%$ return inconsistent with both portfolio holdings and
contemporaneous market conditions); the \textcite{Evans2010} 36-month
incubation correction; winsorisation of monthly returns at the
1st/99th percentile; and exclusion of leveraged and derivative-based
products from the passive universe (30 Rydex 2$\times$, ProFunds Ultra,
and Direxion Bull funds whose daily-reset mechanism produces
multi-year returns inconsistent with conventional long-only index fund
behaviour, following the volatility-decay analysis of
\textcite{AvellanedaZhang2010}).

The pipeline produces three analytically distinct panels.
panel\_master retains the full December 1994--February 2026
sample after the data-error and frozen-tail cleaning but applies neither
incubation correction nor winsorisation; this panel is the audit
baseline whose descriptive statistics appear in
Appendix~\ref{app:alt_panels}. panel\_incubation additionally
applies the Evans 36-month age filter and return winsorisation; this is
the primary analytical panel for all main-text tables and is the panel
on which Tables~\ref{tab:fund_counts} through \ref{tab:persistence} are
estimated. panel\_trimmed additionally restricts the sample to
January 1995--December 2023 to align with the available factor and
sentiment series at the time the panel-regression specifications of
Chapter~\ref{ch:behavioral} were finalised; this panel underlies the
H1--H4 panel regressions and the Bai--Perron sub-period analyses
reported in Appendix~\ref{app:subperiod}.

The active / passive classification follows Bloomberg's
Actively\_Managed flag with two manual overrides documented in
Appendix~\ref{app:data_cleaning}: MOJAX (DF Tactical Momentum Fund) and
GENDX (Gotham Enhanced Index) are reclassified from passive to active,
and five Rydex Pure Style funds matching the leveraged/derivative
keyword filter are explicitly retained in the passive universe because
they are 1$\times$ long-only products tracking S\&P Pure Style indices
whose elevated turnover reflects index reconstitution rather than
derivative rolling. Funds carrying neither an active nor a passive flag
in the Bloomberg static export are coded as Unknown and are
excluded from all regression analyses; they are retained in the
descriptive statistics of Section~\ref{sec:descriptive} for completeness.

\section{Return measurement and the gross/net distinction}
\label{sec:returns}

\noindent The Bloomberg total-return index series carries dividend
reinvestment but is published gross of fees at the share-class
level, in the convention adopted by most non-CRSP databases. Monthly
gross returns are computed as
$r^{\text{gross}}_{i,t} = \text{Index}_{i,t} / \text{Index}_{i,t-1} - 1$
from the cleaned and frozen-tail-trimmed gross total-return index
series. Net returns are derived from the gross series by subtracting
one-twelfth of the static annual expense ratio per month, following
the convention adopted by \textcite{BarrasScailletWermers2010}:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:net_return}
r^{\text{net}}_{i,t} = r^{\text{gross}}_{i,t} - \text{ExpenseRatio}_i / 12.
\end{equation}
This convention is the opposite direction to the CRSP-based pipeline of
\textcite{FamaFrench2010}, where the database supplies net returns and
gross returns are constructed by adding back the realised expense.

Three implications of the convention adopted here merit explicit
acknowledgement: First, the static expense ratio used in the construction does not vary at monthly frequency and therefore cannot capture intra-year fee changes; this introduces a small approximation error. The direction of the bias is ambiguous and is unlikely to materially affect the qualitative conclusions, but it is acknowledged here. Second, the static expense
ratio captures the management fee plus 12b-1 distribution and service
fees but excludes brokerage commissions and bid-ask spreads, which the
realised expense ratio in CRSP also excludes; the two conventions are
thus comparable in coverage. Third, the convention has the desirable
property that the gross-return series is taken as the primitive for the
factor model regressions of Section~\ref{sec:alpha_estimation}, which
isolates managerial skill before fees and avoids contamination of the
gross-alpha estimate by fee variation.

The unwinsorised raw return series ($r^{\text{gross,raw}}$,
$r^{\text{net,raw}}$) are retained on the panel alongside the
winsorised series for use in the construction of the proportional fund
flow variable described in Section~\ref{sec:flow_calculation}; the
Sirri--Tufano flow identity is an accounting relationship that becomes
biased if winsorised returns are substituted for the raw returns that
actually drive period-over-period asset growth.

The regression is estimated by ordinary least squares with
\textcite{NeweyWest1987} heteroskedasticity- and autocorrelation-
consistent standard errors at a six-month lag, following the
recommendation of \textcite{FamaFrench2010}. The lag choice balances
the desire to capture autocorrelation induced by overlapping economic
information shocks against the loss of efficiency from estimating too
many nuisance parameters; a six-month lag is the modal choice in the
modern mutual-fund-performance literature. Fund-level alpha is
estimated in two configurations: a full-period estimate using all
available observations subject to a 24-month minimum, and a 36-month
rolling estimate that produces the time-varying alpha series of
Figure~\ref{fig:rolling_alphas}. The rolling-window length is the
minimum required for the Bai--Perron structural break test of
Section~\ref{sec:subperiod_methodology} and matches the standard choice
in the time-series literature.

\subsection{Aggregate portfolio alphas}
\label{sec:agg_alphas}

\noindent Aggregate-portfolio alphas are estimated by the
\textcite{FamaFrench2010} Section I procedure: the constituent funds'
returns are aggregated into a single monthly portfolio return series
(equal-weighted or value-weighted on lagged total net assets), and a
single Carhart regression is estimated on that portfolio series. The
procedure is preferred to the alternative of estimating fund-level
alphas separately and then averaging because the latter produces an
inconsistent estimator of the value-weighted aggregate when fund-level
$\beta$ heterogeneity is correlated with fund size. Equal-weighted (EW)
portfolio returns assign weight $1/N_t$ to each fund alive in month $t$;
value-weighted (VW) portfolio returns assign weight
$\text{TNA}_{i,t-1} / \sum_j \text{TNA}_{j,t-1}$ to fund $i$ in
month $t$, using lagged total net assets to avoid contemporaneous
endogeneity.

\subsection{Rolling and sub-period alpha estimation}
\label{sec:rolling_alphas}

\noindent Rolling 36-month Carhart alphas are estimated for each fund
with at least 36 consecutive valid observations and aggregated into a
monthly cross-sectional equal-weighted mean of the active-fund alpha
series. This series is the input to the structural-break analysis of
Section~\ref{sec:subperiod_methodology} and is plotted (after a
12-month centred smoothing filter) in Figure~\ref{fig:rolling_alphas}.
The smoothed series is reported only for graphical purposes; the
Bai--Perron test is applied to the unsmoothed series to avoid the
moving-average serial correlation that smoothing would impose on top of
the autocorrelation already present in rolling-window estimates.

\section{Fund flow calculation}
\label{sec:flow_calculation}

\noindent Proportional fund flow is computed at the share-class level
following \textcite{SirriTufano1998} as
\begin{equation}
\text{Flow}_{i,t} = \frac{\text{TNA}_{i,t} - \text{TNA}_{i,t-1}
\cdot (1 + r^{\text{net}}_{i,t})}{\text{TNA}_{i,t-1}},
\label{eq:flow}
\end{equation}
which expresses the percentage growth in total net assets in month $t$
that is not attributable to capital appreciation. The numerator is the
dollar flow into the fund; division by lagged TNA produces the
proportional flow expressed as a fraction of the fund's prior-month
asset base. The construction uses the unwinsorised raw return series
$r^{\text{net,raw}}$ to preserve the accounting identity; substituting
the winsorised series would introduce systematic bias in the months
where genuine extreme returns occur.

Three further conventions follow the literature. First, December
observations are excluded from all flow analyses to avoid contamination
by the year-end capital-gains distribution artifact that is
well-documented in Bloomberg / LSEG-reported flow data. Second, the
proportional flow is winsorised at the 1st/99th percentile across the
pooled sample to limit the influence of extreme outliers. Third, fund-months 
in which the lagged TNA is below \$5 million are excluded from
the regressions of Chapter~\ref{ch:behavioral} to avoid attribution of
spurious flow signals to sub-scale share classes whose proportional
flow is mechanically dominated by single-investor inflows. The
calculated flow exhibits a Pearson correlation with the
Bloomberg-reported flow series of approximately 0.92 over the months
where both are populated, providing external validation of the
construction.

The within-Lipper-category fractional rank is computed monthly 
as the cross-sectional rank of the fund's trailing
12-month gross return within its Lipper category, scaled to lie in
$[0, 1]$. The piecewise-linear specification of \textcite{SirriTufano1998}
partitions the rank into three segments,
$R^{\text{LOW}} = \min(\text{rank}, 0.20)$,
$R^{\text{MID}} = \min(\max(\text{rank} - 0.20, 0), 0.60)$, and
$R^{\text{HIGH}} = \max(\text{rank} - 0.80, 0)$, which together sum
to the unconditional rank. The piecewise specification permits the
slope of the flow--rank relationship to differ across the bottom 20\%,
the middle 60\%, and the top 20\% of fund performance, identifying the
flow--performance convexity that is central to the canonical
flow--performance literature.

\section{Bootstrap procedure for luck vs.\ skill}
\label{sec:bootstrap_methodology}

\noindent The fundamental inferential problem in the cross-section of
fund alphas is that the empirical $t$-statistic distribution does not,
on its own, distinguish between genuine skill and sampling variation.
The \textcite{FamaFrench2010} calendar-month bootstrap addresses this
problem by constructing a counterfactual sample in which all funds have
zero true alpha by construction. For each fund $i$, the estimated
full-period alpha $\hat{\alpha}_i$ is subtracted from the excess return
series, yielding a zero-alpha null series
$\tilde{r}_{i,t} = r^g_{i,t} - r^f_t - \hat{\alpha}_i$. In each of
$B = 10{,}000$ bootstrap iterations, calendar months are resampled with
replacement across all funds simultaneously --- the same set of
calendar months is drawn for every fund in a given iteration --- and
the Carhart four-factor model of Equation~\ref{eq:carhart} is
re-estimated on the resampled series for each fund. The cross-sectional
distribution of bootstrapped $t$-statistics, computed iteration by
iteration, provides the null distribution against which the empirical
distribution is compared.

The cross-fund synchronisation of the resampled month index is
methodologically critical. Resampling month indices independently for
each fund would destroy the cross-sectional dependence induced by
common factor exposures and would over-reject the null. The
synchronised resampling preserves the empirical correlation structure
of factor returns within each iteration and ensures that the bootstrap
distribution accurately reflects the inferential precision of the
fund-level alpha estimates conditional on the observed factor history.
\textcite{NeweyWest1987} HAC standard errors at a six-month lag are
applied symmetrically to actual and bootstrap-simulated $t$-statistics;
asymmetric application produces a known and material bias in the
bootstrap inference.

\section{False discovery rate decomposition}
\label{sec:fdr_methodology}

\noindent The bootstrap procedure produces fund-level p-values but
does not directly decompose the cross-sectional distribution into
skilled, unskilled, and zero-alpha mass. The
\textcite{Storey2002} false-discovery-rate framework, applied to mutual
fund performance by \textcite{BarrasScailletWermers2010}, provides
this decomposition. The Storey estimator of the population proportion
of zero-alpha funds is
\begin{equation}
\hat{\pi}_0 = \frac{\#\{p_i > \lambda\}}{N (1 - \lambda)},
\label{eq:storey}
\end{equation}
where $p_i$ is the two-sided $p$-value from the Newey--West $t$-test
on the full-period Carhart alpha for fund $i$, $\lambda$ is a tuning
parameter set to $0.5$ following the literature standard, and $N$ is
the number of active funds in the sample. The estimator exploits the
fact that, under the null of zero alpha, $p$-values are uniformly
distributed on $[0, 1]$, so the density of $p$-values exceeding
$\lambda$ provides a conservative estimate of the population zero-alpha
proportion. The estimator is bounded above at one and is conservative
in the sense that it overestimates $\hat{\pi}_0$ when truly skilled
funds exist, providing a lower bound on the genuinely skilled mass.

The four-way decomposition of \textcite{BarrasScailletWermers2010}
combines $\hat{\pi}_0$ with the observed empirical tails. At a two-sided
significance threshold $\gamma$, the observed fraction of funds with a
significantly negative $t(\hat{\alpha})$ is denoted $S^-_\gamma$ and
the corresponding fraction with significantly positive
$t(\hat{\alpha})$ is denoted $S^+_\gamma$. Under the null of zero alpha,
the expected fraction of false discoveries per tail is
$F_\gamma = \hat{\pi}_0 \cdot \gamma / 2$. The genuinely unskilled mass
is $T^-_\gamma = S^-_\gamma - F_\gamma$ and the genuinely skilled mass
is $T^+_\gamma = S^+_\gamma - F_\gamma$. The main-text threshold is
$\gamma^* = 0.20$, following the convention of
\textcite{BarrasScailletWermers2010}; results at alternative
thresholds in \{0.05, 0.10, 0.15, 0.25, 0.30, 0.35, 0.40, 0.45, 0.50\
are reported in the same tables for completeness. Negative entries in
$T^+_\gamma$ are reported as such and indicate that right-tail
significance does not exceed the false-discovery rate at that threshold;
the entries are not floored at zero in order to preserve the
informational content of the decomposition.

\section{Sub-period construction via Bai--Perron}
\label{sec:subperiod_methodology}

\noindent The rolling Carhart alpha series of
Section~\ref{sec:rolling_alphas} exhibits visible non-stationarity
across the December 1994--February 2026 sample. To partition the
sample into economically coherent sub-periods without imposing
thresholds \textit{a priori}, the multiple structural break test of
\textcite{Bai1998,Bai2003} is applied to the monthly cross-sectional
equal-weighted mean of the 36-month rolling Carhart gross alpha for
active funds. The model is a pure mean-shift specification,
$y_t = \mu_i + \varepsilon_t$ for $t$ in segment $i$, with a minimum
segment length of 36 months and up to five breaks tested. The optimal
number of breaks is selected by the Bayesian Information Criterion
(BIC). Confidence intervals for break dates use Newey--West
heteroskedasticity- and autocorrelation-consistent standard errors with
the data-driven lag selection rule of \textcite{Andrews1991}. The full
results, including the implied six-regime partition and the
three-regime aggregation adopted for the bootstrap analyses of
Appendix~\ref{app:subperiod}, are reported in
Section~\ref{sec:break_test_results}.

\section{Activeness measures}
\label{sec:activeness_methodology}

\noindent Two activeness measures are used in the cross-sectional and
panel analyses. The primary measure is $\text{ActR2}_i$, defined as
the time-series mean of $1 - R^2_{i,t}$ from the rolling 36-month
Carhart regression that produces the rolling alpha series of
Section~\ref{sec:rolling_alphas}. Following
\textcite{AmihudGoyenko2013}, a higher value of $1 - R^2$ indicates
that a larger share of the fund's return variation is unexplained by
the systematic four-factor benchmark, and is therefore attributable
to the fund's idiosyncratic active deviation from a passive
factor-mimicking portfolio. The robustness measure is the annualised
tracking error of fund gross returns against the fund's assigned
benchmark index,
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:tracking_error}
\text{TE}_i = \sqrt{12} \cdot \text{sd}\bigl(r^{\text{gross}}_{i,t} - r_{b(i),t}\bigr),
\end{equation}
following the interpretation of \textcite{CremersPetajisto2009}. The
holdings-based Active Share measure of
\textcite{CremersPetajisto2009} is not constructible from the data
sources accessed for this dissertation; the two return-based measures
are reported jointly to mitigate single-measure concerns.

The cross-sectional rank-based analyses of Section~\ref{sec:activeness}
sort active funds with at least 24 monthly observations into five
quintiles by the time-series mean of the activeness measure. Quintile~1
contains the closet indexers (lowest activeness) and Quintile~5 the
most active funds. Equal-weighted and lagged-TNA-weighted monthly
portfolio returns are computed for each quintile, both gross and net of
fees, and regressed on the Carhart four factors. The Q5$-$Q1
self-financing spread is regressed without subtracting the risk-free
rate, since $R_f$ cancels in the long-short construction.

The activeness-conditioned persistence test of
Section~\ref{sec:persistence} uses a 10$\times$3 bivariate sort. At
each cohort ranking date, all funds with the requisite formation-window
observations are sorted into 10 alpha-decile portfolios within each of
3 activeness terciles, where the activeness measure is the
formation-window $1 - R^2$ (cohort-local, computed within the same
36-month window used to rank fund alphas). Tercile breakpoints are
computed cross-sectionally within each cohort to avoid look-ahead
contamination. Holding-period equal-weighted gross excess returns are
pooled across cohorts and regressed on the Carhart four factors with
Newey--West HAC standard errors at a 12-month lag. Bootstrap inference
is omitted for this secondary conditional test; the full bootstrap
machinery is reserved for the unconditional persistence test of
Section~\ref{sec:persistence} and Table~\ref{tab:persistence}.

\section{Persistence testing methodology}
\label{sec:persistence_design}

\noindent Performance persistence is evaluated with two complementary methods
that share an identical formation and holding period design, enabling direct
comparison of inference across methodologies. The first, following
\textcite{Carhart1997}, uses parametric inference on the pooled holding-period
alphas of decile portfolios formed on past performance rank. The second, following
\textcite{KosowskiTimmermannWermersWhite2006} (KTWW), augments the same portfolio
framework with a cross-sectional bootstrap that replaces parametric $t$-tests with
empirically calibrated null distributions, correcting for the non-normality and
cross-sectional heterogeneity documented in mutual fund return distributions.
Both methods are applied over the full sample and separately within each of the
three Bai--Perron structural regimes identified in
Section~\ref{sec:subperiod_methodology}.

The formation period is set to 36 months and the holding period to 12 months,
the standard choices in the persistence literature that directly replicate the
design of \textcite{Carhart1997} and \textcite{KosowskiTimmermannWermersWhite2006}.
A 36-month formation window provides sufficient observations to estimate Carhart
four-factor loadings and alphas with reasonable precision;
\textcite{KosowskiTimmermannWermersWhite2006} show that shortening the formation
window to 12 months substantially increases non-normality in the alpha distribution
of ranked portfolios. A 12-month holding period is the universal standard in the
persistence literature \parencite{Carhart1997,BollenBusse2005} and avoids
conflating momentum effects with genuine managerial skill persistence.

Formation and holding windows are non-overlapping and do not share calendar months.
Ranking date k uses the 36 months ending at the close of year k, with the
subsequent 12 calendar months constituting holding period k. The cohort at
ranking date $k$ comprises all active funds in the \textcite{Evans2010} corrected
panel with at least 24 monthly return observations during the formation window,
using gross returns throughout for consistency with the main bootstrap analysis.
Seven complete, non-overlapping cohorts are available over the full sample
(January~1995 -- February~2026); a partial eighth cohort whose holding window
covers only January and February~2026 is excluded from inference. The seven
retained cohorts yield 84 pooled monthly portfolio return observations per decile
for the full-sample analysis.

The formation-period ranking signal is the t-statistic of the formation-period
Carhart alpha, $t(\hat{\alpha}_i)$, estimated with Newey--West standard errors
at a three-month lag. Ranking by $t(\hat{\alpha}_i)$ rather than by the raw
alpha normalises for estimation precision, reducing the influence of
high-volatility funds whose extreme alpha estimates are more likely to reflect
sampling noise than genuine skill \parencite{KosowskiTimmermannWermersWhite2006}.
Raw-alpha ranking is implemented as a robustness check (Panel~B of
Table~\ref{tab:persistence}). At each ranking date, all eligible funds are sorted
into ten deciles by $t(\hat{\alpha}_i)$, with Decile~1 (D1) containing the
highest-ranked funds and Decile~10 (D10) the lowest. Equal-weighted portfolios
are formed within each decile; funds that exit during the 12-month holding
period are included through their last available return and the portfolio is
rebalanced proportionally among surviving members
\parencite{Carhart1997,KosowskiTimmermannWermersWhite2006}. This treatment
prevents survivorship bias from attenuating the negative alpha estimates of
the bottom decile.

For parametric inference, monthly equal-weighted holding-period returns for
decile $d$ are pooled across all cohorts $k = 1, \ldots, K$ to form a time
series of $T_d = 12 \times K$ monthly observations. The Carhart four-factor
model is estimated on this pooled series:
\begin{equation}
  R^d_t - RF_t \;=\; \alpha^d + \beta^d\,\mathit{MKT\_RF}_t
    + s^d\,\mathit{SMB}_t + h^d\,\mathit{HML}_t
    + p^d\,\mathit{MOM}_t + u^d_t
  \label{eq:persistence_pooled}
\end{equation}
% AFTER
where $t(\hat{\alpha}^d) = \hat{\alpha}^d /
\mathrm{SE}_{\mathrm{NW}}(\hat{\alpha}^d)$ and
$\mathrm{SE}_{\mathrm{NW}}$ denotes Newey--West standard errors with
a 12-month lag truncation. Pooling across cohorts rather than estimating
cohort-by-cohort is a deliberate design choice: with only 12 monthly
observations per cohort, cohort-level estimation of a five-parameter model
is unreliable. The key assumption --- that factor loadings are sufficiently
stationary across cohorts within a structural regime --- is addressed in the
sub-period panels, which pool only within each Bai--Perron regime.

The KTWW bootstrap replaces parametric inference with an empirically calibrated
null. For each fund $i$ in cohort $k$, holding-period residuals are computed by
projecting actual holding-period returns onto the Carhart factors using
formation-period loadings:
\begin{equation}
  \hat{\varepsilon}^{\mathrm{hold}}_{it} = r_{it}
    - \hat{\beta}_i\,\mathit{MKT\_RF}_t
    - \hat{s}_i\,\mathit{SMB}_t
    - \hat{h}_i\,\mathit{HML}_t
    - \hat{p}_i\,\mathit{MOM}_t.
  \label{eq:ktww_resid}
\end{equation}
Using formation-period loadings rather than re-estimating them from the
holding period is essential: the null is conditioned on each fund's known
risk profile as of the ranking date. In each of B = 10{,}000 iterations,
a resample is drawn with replacement from each fund's holding-period
residuals; the same draw index is applied to all funds alive in the same
calendar month, preserving the empirical cross-sectional correlation structure
\parencite{KosowskiTimmermannWermersWhite2006}. Zero-alpha pseudo-returns are
then constructed as:
\begin{equation}
  r^b_{it} = \hat{\beta}_i\,\mathit{MKT\_RF}_t
    + \hat{s}_i\,\mathit{SMB}_t
    + \hat{h}_i\,\mathit{HML}_t
    + \hat{p}_i\,\mathit{MOM}_t
    + \hat{\varepsilon}^b_{it}.
  \label{eq:ktww_pseudo}
\end{equation}
By omitting the intercept, the null of zero true alpha is imposed by
construction while factor realisations remain actual, not resampled.
Equal-weighted pseudo decile portfolio returns are pooled across cohorts
and the Carhart model re-estimated; the bootstrapped t-statistic
$t(\hat{\alpha}^{d,b})$ is recorded. % AFTER
Left-tail bootstrap $p$-values,
\begin{equation}
  p_{\mathrm{LB}}(d) = \frac{1}{B}\sum_{b=1}^{B}
    \mathbf{1}\!\left[t(\hat{\alpha}^{d,b}) 
    t(\hat{\alpha}^{d,\mathrm{actual}})\right],
  \label{eq:boot_pval_persist}
\end{equation}
test for persistent underperformance; right-tail $p$-values test for
persistent outperformance.
The Jarque--Bera statistic is reported for each decile portfolio; where it
rejects at the 5\% level the bootstrap p-values constitute the valid
inferential basis and the parametric results are reported for
comparability only.

The sub-period panels restrict cohorts to within-regime windows.
Panel~C of Table~\ref{tab:persistence} pools the two cohorts whose
holding windows fall within P1 (pre-2006); Panel~D pools the single
cohort whose formation and holding windows both fall within P2
(January~2006 -- September~2011); Panel~E pools the two cohorts
whose holding windows fall within P3 (October~2011 onwards). Panel~F
reports a cross-regime test using the two cohorts whose formation
windows straddle a regime boundary: Cohort~3 (formation
January~2003 -- December~2005, holding January~2006 -- December~2006,
spanning the P1/P2 break) and Cohort~5 (formation
January~2011 -- December~2013, holding January~2014 -- December~2014,
spanning the P2/P3 break). This cross-regime design tests whether
the regime transitions identified in Section~\ref{sec:subperiod}
amplify the persistence asymmetry by exposing formation-period losers
to the deteriorating alpha environment of the subsequent structural
regime.

The activeness-conditioned persistence extension of
Table~\ref{tab:activeness_persistence} applies an identical cohort
structure, formation window, and pooled holding-period regression,
adding only an activeness pre-sort. At each ranking date, funds are
first partitioned into three terciles by their cohort-local formation-window
$1-R^2$ --- computed within the same 36-month window used to estimate

classification --- and then within each tercile into ten alpha deciles
by $t(\hat{\alpha}_i)$. Tercile breakpoints are computed cross-sectionally
within each cohort's eligible fund universe to avoid look-ahead
contamination. This $10 \times 3$ bivariate sort bridges the
activeness analysis of Section~\ref{sec:activeness} and the persistence
results: if activeness amplifies the persistence asymmetry
asymmetrically at the loser tail, the behavioral narrative of
disproportionate capital retention in active underperformers is
materially sharpened. Bootstrap inference is omitted from the
activeness-conditioned table; parametric Newey--West $t$-statistics
are reported at a 12-month lag, consistent with the primary persistence

\section{Behavioral state variables}
\label{sec:state_variables}

\noindent The behavioral state variables used in the H1--H4 panel
regressions of Chapter~\ref{ch:behavioral} are constructed at monthly
frequency for the December 1994--December 2023 window of the trimmed
panel. The Baker--Wurgler sentiment index ($\mathrm{SENT}^{\perp}$ in
the orthogonalised variant) is downloaded from Jeffrey Wurgler's
website at NYU Stern; the corresponding regime indicator
$D^{\mathrm{SENT}}_t$ takes value one in the top tercile of the
orthogonalised sentiment distribution following the
\textcite{BakerWurgler2007} timing convention. The AAII bull--bear
spread is downloaded from the AAII member sentiment survey; the
regime indicator $D^{\mathrm{AAII}}_t$ takes value one in the top
tercile of the bull-minus-bear spread distribution. The FINRA margin
debt series is detrended via the Hamilton filter
\parencite{Hamilton2018}, and the regime indicator $D^{\mathrm{MD,Det}}_t$
takes value one in the top tercile of the detrended series. The CBOE
inverse put--call ratio (1$/$put--call) is the basis for
$D^{\mathrm{INV\text{-}PCR}}_t$ in the same fashion. Each indicator
is constructed contemporaneously with the dependent flow variable,
following the \textcite{BakerWurgler2007} timing recommendation; the
robustness check of Appendix~\ref{app:behavioral_robustness} re-estimates
each hypothesis with the state variable lagged one month to verify that
the timing assumption is not load-bearing.

\noindent The selection of these four variables is grounded in
distinct strands of the behavioral finance literature, and the
variables are chosen to provide mutually reinforcing but
independently sourced identification of the underlying
psychological mechanisms. The \textcite{BakerWurgler2006} index is
the canonical aggregate sentiment measure in U.S.\ asset pricing: it
aggregates six market-based proxies and orthogonalises against
macroeconomic controls, and its use as a regime-conditioning state
variable for cross-sectional anomaly returns is established by
\textcite{StambaughYuYuan2012}. The effect of Baker--Wurgler sentiment
on mutual fund flows specifically is documented by
\textcite{BenRephaelKandelWohl2012},
\textcite{IndroSentiment2004}, and \textcite{WuZhang2024}, who
consistently find that high-sentiment regimes amplify flow sensitivity
to past performance. The AAII bull--bear spread complements the market-based Baker--Wurgler
index by capturing \emph{stated} retail investor attitudes rather than
attitudes inferred from prices; its use as an aggregate sentiment proxy
follows \textcite{BrownCliff2004}, who validate the AAII spread against
contemporaneous market returns and document its forecasting content for
near-term stock market performance. Margin debt is motivated as
the primary H2 state variable by the illusion-of-control framework of
\textcite{Langer1975}: investors who borrow to commit capital to an
active fund have made a costly, active choice that deepens
psychological ownership \parencite{Thaler1980} and amplifies
reluctance to realise losses. The detrending procedure follows
\textcite{DanielKlosPollet2016} and \textcite{RapachRinggenbergZhou2016},
who show that the level of margin debt relative to market
capitalisation, net of its secular trend, is the component most
informative about leveraged investor conviction at business-cycle
frequency. The \textcite{BrunnermeierPedersen2009} margin-call
mechanism, under which high leverage regimes amplify rather than
attenuate forced redemptions of losers, is retained as the competing
null within the H2 specification, ensuring that the empirical test is
genuinely two-sided. Finally, the CBOE inverse put--call ratio
provides an options-market-based H2 robustness proxy: a high
call-to-put ratio directly operationalises investor belief in
directional market predictability --- a financial-markets expression
of the control illusion in the \textcite{Langer1975} sense --- and
delivers identification from derivatives markets rather than equity
market prices or survey responses.

\section{Behavioral hypotheses}
\label{sec:hypotheses_methodology}

\noindent The four behavioral hypotheses of Chapter~\ref{ch:behavioral}
are formally specified as follows.

\subsection{H1: Sentiment-convexity hypothesis}
\label{sec:H1_method}

\noindent H1 predicts that high-sentiment regimes amplify the
flow--performance convexity documented by \textcite{SirriTufano1998}.
The specification is the Sirri--Tufano piecewise rank regression
augmented with sentiment-regime interactions:

\begin{multline}
\text{Flow}_{i,t} =
  \beta_{\text{LOW}} R^{\text{LOW}}_{i,t-12}
+ \beta_{\text{MID}} R^{\text{MID}}_{i,t-12}
+ \beta_{\text{HIGH}} R^{\text{HIGH}}_{i,t-12} \\
+ \delta_{\text{LOW}}\, R^{\text{LOW}}_{i,t-12} D^{\text{SENT}}_t
+ \delta_{\text{MID}}\, R^{\text{MID}}_{i,t-12} D^{\text{SENT}}_t
+ \delta_{\text{HIGH}}\, R^{\text{HIGH}}_{i,t-12} D^{\text{SENT}}_t \\
+ \lambda D^{\text{SENT}}_t + X_{i,t} \gamma + \mu_i + \varepsilon_{i,t},
\label{eq:H1}
\end{multline}

\noindent where $R^{\text{LOW}}$, $R^{\text{MID}}$, and $R^{\text{HIGH}}$
are the Sirri--Tufano piecewise rank segments; $D^{\text{SENT}}_t$ is
the high-sentiment regime indicator; $\delta_q$ is the
sentiment-regime interaction coefficient for segment $q \in
\{\text{LOW}, \text{MID}, \text{HIGH}\}$; $\lambda$ is the main
effect of the sentiment regime on the level of flows; the controls
$X_{i,t}$ include $\log(\text{TNA}_{i,t-1})$, $\log(\text{Age}_{i,t})$,
the trailing 36-month return volatility, and the contemporaneous
Lipper-category average flow, all measured at $t-1$; and $\mu_i$ is
a fund fixed effect. The auxiliary hypothesis is the asymmetry test
$\delta_{\text{HIGH}} > \delta_{\text{LOW}}$, expressing the
prediction that sentiment amplifies inflows to winners more than it
amplifies outflows from losers. Standard errors are clustered
two-way on fund and calendar month \parencite{Petersen2009}.

\subsection{H2: Disposition / illusion-of-control hypothesis}
\label{sec:H2_method}

\noindent H2 tests two competing predictions about how high-leverage
regimes modulate the loser-segment slope. The specification replaces
$D^{\text{SENT}}_t$ in Equation~\ref{eq:H1} with the detrended
margin-debt regime indicator $D^{\text{MD,Det}}_t$:

\begin{multline}
\text{Flow}_{i,t} =
  \beta_{\text{LOW}} R^{\text{LOW}}_{i,t-12}
+ \beta_{\text{MID}} R^{\text{MID}}_{i,t-12}
+ \beta_{\text{HIGH}} R^{\text{HIGH}}_{i,t-12} \\
+ \delta^{\text{IoC}}_{\text{LOW}}\, R^{\text{LOW}}_{i,t-12} D^{\text{MD,Det}}_t
+ \delta^{\text{IoC}}_{\text{MID}}\, R^{\text{MID}}_{i,t-12} D^{\text{MD,Det}}_t
+ \delta^{\text{IoC}}_{\text{HIGH}}\, R^{\text{HIGH}}_{i,t-12} D^{\text{MD,Det}}_t \\
+ \lambda D^{\text{MD,Det}}_t + X_{i,t} \gamma + \mu_i + \varepsilon_{i,t}.
\label{eq:H2}
\end{multline}

\noindent The baseline loser-segment coefficient $\beta_{\text{LOW}}$ is
positive: an improvement in relative rank within the bottom tercile
is associated with higher flows, and a deterioration generates
outflows. The interaction coefficient $\delta^{\text{IoC}}_{\text{LOW}}$
determines whether high margin debt \emph{steepens} or \emph{flattens}
that slope. The disposition / illusion-of-control prediction of
\textcite{ShefrinStatman1985} is $\delta^{\text{IoC}}_{\text{LOW}} < 0$:
leveraged investors exhibit stronger sunk-cost bias and are more
reluctant to realise losses, dampening the outflow response to rank
deterioration and \emph{flattening} the loser-segment slope. The
\textcite{BrunnermeierPedersen2009} margin-call alternative predicts
$\delta^{\text{IoC}}_{\text{LOW}} > 0$: balance-sheet constraints force
faster liquidation of losing positions under high leverage,
\emph{steepening} the slope and amplifying the outflow response to
any given rank deterioration. All remaining notation follows
Equation~\ref{eq:H1}. Robustness checks substitute the inverse
put--call ratio regime indicator $D^{\text{INV-PCR}}_t$ and a
discriminant version that includes both indicators jointly.

\subsection{H3: Lottery demand and skewness preference}
\label{sec:H3_method}

\noindent H3 predicts that high-sentiment regimes amplify investor
demand for funds with lottery-like return distributions. Three
candidate lottery proxies are evaluated in separate specifications:
$\text{ActR2}$ (the rational alpha-prediction channel of
\textcite{AmihudGoyenko2013}), $\text{ActSkew}$ (sample skewness of
trailing 36 monthly gross returns, the full-distribution lottery
measure), and $\text{MAX12}$ (the maximum monthly gross return over
the trailing 12 months, the right-tail-only measure). Each proxy is
standardised within sample and lagged one month; the proxy
validation procedure that selects these three from the four
candidates considered is documented in
Appendix~\ref{app:proxy_validation}. For a generic lottery proxy
$L_{i,t-1}$, the specification is:

\begin{multline}
\text{Flow}_{i,t} =
  \beta_{\text{LOW}} R^{\text{LOW}}_{i,t-12}
+ \beta_{\text{MID}} R^{\text{MID}}_{i,t-12}
+ \beta_{\text{HIGH}} R^{\text{HIGH}}_{i,t-12} \\
+ \alpha^L\, L_{i,t-1}
+ \delta^L\, L_{i,t-1} D^{\text{SENT}}_t
+ \lambda D^{\text{SENT}}_t
+ X_{i,t} \gamma + \mu_i + \varepsilon_{i,t},
\label{eq:H3}
\end{multline}

\noindent where $L_{i,t-1}$ is replaced in turn by
$\text{ActR2}_{i,t-1}$, $\text{ActSkew}_{i,t-1}$, and
$\text{MAX12}_{i,t-1}$, each constituting a separate column in the
reported table alongside a baseline Sirri--Tufano specification with
no lottery proxy; $\alpha^L$ is the main effect of the lottery proxy
on flows; and $\delta^L$ is the sentiment-amplification interaction,
with the superscript $L$ denoting the lottery channel. The rank
segments $R^q$ are retained as nuisance controls so that H3
conditions on the H1 channel. The behavioral prediction is
$\delta^L > 0$ for $\text{ActSkew}$ and $\text{MAX12}$: sentiment
amplifies investor demand for lottery-like funds. The expected sign
on $\delta^L$ for $\text{ActR2}$ is either zero (the rational
benchmark) or positive (investors rationally reward activeness as a
skill signal under the \textcite{AmihudGoyenko2013} channel). All
remaining notation and standard errors follow Equation~\ref{eq:H1}.

\subsection{H4: Fee elasticity}
\label{sec:H4_method}

\noindent H4 tests whether flow--fee elasticity is heterogeneous across
performance segments and sentiment regimes in a manner inconsistent
with the rational benchmark. The specification augments the
Sirri--Tufano baseline with expense ratio interactions, their
sentiment interactions, and the full triple interactions with the
sentiment regime indicator:

\begin{multline}
\text{Flow}_{i,t} =
  \beta_{\text{LOW}} R^{\text{LOW}}_{i,t-12}
+ \beta_{\text{MID}} R^{\text{MID}}_{i,t-12}
+ \beta_{\text{HIGH}} R^{\text{HIGH}}_{i,t-12} \\
+ \kappa_{\text{LOW}}\, R^{\text{LOW}}_{i,t-12} D^{\text{SENT}}_t
+ \kappa_{\text{MID}}\, R^{\text{MID}}_{i,t-12} D^{\text{SENT}}_t
+ \kappa_{\text{HIGH}}\, R^{\text{HIGH}}_{i,t-12} D^{\text{SENT}}_t \\
+ \phi_{\text{LOW}}\, R^{\text{LOW}}_{i,t-12} \cdot \text{ExpRatio}_i
+ \phi_{\text{MID}}\, R^{\text{MID}}_{i,t-12} \cdot \text{ExpRatio}_i
+ \phi_{\text{HIGH}}\, R^{\text{HIGH}}_{i,t-12} \cdot \text{ExpRatio}_i \\
+ \delta^F_{\text{LOW}}\, R^{\text{LOW}}_{i,t-12} \cdot \text{ExpRatio}_i \cdot D^{\text{SENT}}_t
+ \delta^F_{\text{MID}}\, R^{\text{MID}}_{i,t-12} \cdot \text{ExpRatio}_i \cdot D^{\text{SENT}}_t
+ \delta^F_{\text{HIGH}}\, R^{\text{HIGH}}_{i,t-12} \cdot \text{ExpRatio}_i \cdot D^{\text{SENT}}_t \\
+ \theta\, \text{ExpRatio}_i
+ \eta\, \text{ExpRatio}_i \cdot D^{\text{SENT}}_t
+ \lambda D^{\text{SENT}}_t
+ X_{i,t} \gamma + \alpha_{s \times t} + \varepsilon_{i,t},
\label{eq:H4}
\end{multline}

\noindent where $\text{ExpRatio}_i$ is the fund's annual expense ratio;
$\theta$ is its main effect on flows; $\kappa_q$ are the lower-order
rank-sentiment interactions, included as controls to ensure valid
triple-interaction inference (these correspond to the $\delta_q$ of
Equation~\ref{eq:H1} re-estimated on the H4 sample); $\phi_q$ are
the rank-fee interactions capturing how fee level modulates the
flow--performance slope unconditional on sentiment; $\eta$ is the
unconditional fee--sentiment interaction capturing whether aggregate
fee tolerance shifts with the sentiment regime; $\delta^F_q$ are the
triple interaction coefficients, with the superscript $F$ denoting
the fee channel; and $\alpha_{s \times t}$ denotes
Lipper-category $\times$ year-month fixed effects, which replace
fund fixed effects to absorb both the sentiment main effect
(otherwise collinear with the time-invariant $\text{ExpRatio}_i$
under fund fixed effects) and any aggregate flow shocks within
investment style. All remaining notation follows
Equation~\ref{eq:H1}. 

Under the \textcite{BerkGreen2004} rational
equilibrium, fee level should not interact with sentiment-driven
flow: the joint null is $\eta = \delta^F_{\text{LOW}} =
\delta^F_{\text{MID}} = \delta^F_{\text{HIGH}} = 0$. The behavioral
alternative permits heterogeneous fee sensitivity across rank
segments, with the prediction that high-sentiment investors are less
fee-sensitive (or differentially so) depending on where a fund sits
in the performance distribution. Standard errors are clustered
two-way on fund and calendar month \parencite{Petersen2009}.

\section{Aggregate fund performance}
\label{sec:aggregate_performance}

\noindent Table~\ref{tab:perf_aggregate} reports the central
performance result of the dissertation. The aggregate equal-weighted
gross Carhart alpha for active funds is -0.288\% per annum, 
statistically indistinguishable from zero; the
value-weighted analogue is -0.189\%, also
indistinguishable from zero. The active-passive gross-alpha
differential is small, consistent with the standard
finding that active and passive managers generate similar
risk-adjusted returns before costs.

\input{tables/table_perf_aggregate}

The picture changes when fees are deducted. The aggregate
equal-weighted net Carhart alpha for active funds is -1.361\% per
annum; the value-weighted analogue
is -0.900\% per annum, both significant at 1 and 5 percent respectively. Two
features of this result merit emphasis. First, the equal-weighted
estimate is materially worse than the value-weighted estimate, with a
gap of approximately 46 basis points per annum. The gap quantifies
the locational interpretation of the puzzle introduced in
Section~\ref{sec:motivation}: capital concentrates in funds whose
size-adjusted alpha is approximately zero, while the long left tail of
small underperforming funds drives the equal-weighted mean. This
pattern is consistent with the \textcite{BerkGreen2004} prediction
that competitive flow is responsive to expected gross alpha, so that
size and skill are correlated in equilibrium. Second, the
value-weighted net alpha of -0.900\% per annum, while statistically
significant, is economically modest --- well within the range of
estimates reported by \textcite{FamaFrench2010} on their 1984--2006
sample (where the corresponding figure was approximately -1\%
annually). The fact that the post-FF (2010) extension to 2026 has
not produced a materially different value-weighted estimate is
itself informative: it suggests that the rational equilibrium of
\textcite{BerkGreen2004} continues to provide a clean reading of the
capital-weighted distribution.

The unknown-AM group exhibits materially worse performance than either
active or passive: equal-weighted net alpha of -4.844\% per annum
(significant at 1\%) and value-weighted net alpha of
-4.126\%, both significant at 1 percent. The unknown-AM
classification corresponds to share classes for which Bloomberg's
active management flag was not populated; manual inspection of fund
names confirms that the group consists predominantly of small,
short-lived, sub-scale funds whose underperformance is driven by
fees and sub-scale operational drag rather than by attempted active
management. The group is excluded from all regression analyses on
this basis, but is reported in the descriptive tables for
completeness.

\section{Luck versus skill: Bootstrap analysis}
\label{sec:bootstrap_results}

\noindent Table~\ref{tab:bootstrap_tails} reports the
\textcite{FamaFrench2010} calendar-month bootstrap percentiles
against the empirical t-statistic distribution. The left tail of
the empirical distribution is materially worse than the bootstrap null
at every percentile below the median. At the 1st percentile, the
empirical t-statistic is -3.619 against a bootstrap mean of
-2.064; at the 5th percentile, -2.684 against -1.370; at the
10th percentile, -2.184 against -1.053. In each case the
probability that the bootstrap-simulated percentile falls below the
empirical value is reported as 0.0\%, meaning that across all B =
10{,}000 bootstrap iterations, none produced a left tail as extreme
as the empirical one.

\input{tables/table_bootstrap_tails}

The right tail tells a different story. At the 90th
percentile, the empirical t-statistic of 0.993 is essentially
indistinguishable from the bootstrap mean of 1.014; at the 95th
percentile, 1.362 against 1.326; at the 99th percentile, 2.248
against 2.019. The probability of the bootstrap-simulated
percentile falling below the empirical value rises across
the right tail (50.1\% at the 90th, 59.6\% at the 95th, 80.9\% at
the 99th). The interpretation is that the right tail of the
empirical distribution is consistent with the zero-skill null at
all percentiles above the median: the apparently positive-alpha
funds at the right tail are well within the range that pure sampling
variation under zero true skill would produce.

The asymmetry --- material left-tail rejection of the null with
right-tail consistency --- is the central empirical finding of the
bootstrap analysis. It is consistent with
\textcite{FamaFrench2010}'s parallel finding on their 1984--2006
sample, and replicates on a sample that extends fifteen years past
their window. The qualitative invariance of the bootstrap pattern
across the two samples is itself informative: the cross-sectional
underperformance of the worst active funds is detectable in the
post-FF (2010) regime by the same methodology that originally
identified it.

Table~\ref{tab:bsw_decomposition} reports the
\textcite{BarrasScailletWermers2010} four-way decomposition. At the
main-text threshold $\gamma^* = 0.20$, the observed left-tail
significance fraction is $S^-_{\gamma^*} = 28.9\%$ and the right-tail
fraction is $S^+_{\gamma^*} = 5.8\%$. The expected false-discovery
fraction per tail is $F_{\gamma^*} = \hat{\pi}_0 \cdot \gamma^* / 2 =
0.778 \cdot 0.10 = 7.8\%$. The genuinely unskilled mass is therefore
$T^-_{\gamma^*} = 28.9\% - 7.8\% = 21.1\%$, and the genuinely skilled
mass is $T^+_{\gamma^*} = 5.8\% - 7.8\% = -2.0\%$.

The negative entry for $T^+_{\gamma^*}$ is, as
\textcite{BarrasScailletWermers2010} note, an artifact of the Storey
estimator's conservative bias when truly skilled funds exist; the
correct interpretation is that the right-tail significance fraction
does not exceed the false-discovery rate at this threshold, so the
data cannot support a positive estimate of the skilled mass at
conventional inferential thresholds. This finding does not imply that
managerial skill does not exist in the population --- it almost
certainly does, in the \textcite{BerkVanBinsbergen2015} dollar-value
sense --- but that the population mass of managers whose
net-of-fee alpha is detectably positive in standard hypothesis
tests is, on this sample, indistinguishable from the false-discovery
rate at any threshold the data can support. The genuinely unskilled
mass, by contrast, is robustly positive across all thresholds in the
range tested, rising from approximately 11\% at $\gamma = 5\%$ to nearly
28\% at $\gamma = 50\%$ and centred on 21\% at the canonical $\gamma^* = 20\%$. The asymmetry between the
detectable unskilled and undetectable skilled mass is the cross-sectional
manifestation of the active management puzzle: the empirical distribution carries a clean signal of underperformance but no detectable signal of outperformance even before fees are deducted — a finding that extends a fortiori to net-of-fee returns

Within the active group, the size quintiles partition funds cleanly by scale: average TNA rises from \$84 million at Q1 to over \$11 billion at Q5. The structural correlation between size and fee is pronounced — expense ratios fall monotonically from 1.27\% to 0.73\% as assets grow, consistent with the economies of scale documented in the literature. Turnover also declines with size, from 68.8\% to 39.3\%, suggesting that larger active funds trade more conservatively. Neither gross returns nor Sharpe ratios exhibit a meaningful trend across size quintiles, with EW gross returns clustering between 0.915 and 0.968 and annualised Sharpe ratios nearly flat at 0.58–0.60. Passive gross returns are similarly undifferentiated across size quintiles.

The momentum sort produces by far the sharpest gradient in the table. Active Q1 funds — past losers — record average EW and VW gross returns of 0.694 and 0.679, respectively, against an annualised Sharpe of 0.37; the corresponding figures for Q5 past winners are 1.081, 1.063, and 0.66. This near-doubling in risk-adjusted return from bottom to top quintile, with a smooth monotonic progression across all intermediate quintiles, reflects the strong continuation in relative gross performance that the momentum factor of Jegadeesh and Titman (1993) is designed to capture. The fee sort reveals a clear and practically important negative relationship between cost and risk-adjusted performance among active funds. A pronounced size-fee inverse correlation is again evident: Q1 active funds average \$6.1 billion in TNA versus just \$618 million for Q5, reinforcing that the highest-fee funds are predominantly small. Turnover rises notably at the high-fee end (76.4\% for Q5), consistent with transaction costs compounding the drag of elevated expense ratios. The passive fee sort (Panel F) is even starker in its structural contrasts: the two lowest-fee quintiles are dominated by large-scale institutional index vehicles averaging over \$8.5 billion in TNA with turnover below 12\%, while Q5 passive funds — charging 1.49\% on average — average only \$162 million and turn over their entire portfolio nearly three times per year (288.4\%), a profile more consistent with leveraged or derivatives-based index products than conventional passive management. \section{Activeness and the cross-section of fund alphas}
\label{sec:activeness}

\noindent The fee, size, and momentum quintile sorts of the previous
section partition the fund universe along characteristics
that prior literature has documented as predictors of cross-sectional
alpha. None of those characteristics, however, addresses the question
that motivates this dissertation directly: holding everything else
constant, does being more active --- deviating more from
systematic factor exposures or from a stated benchmark --- produce a
different alpha? This section provides that test.

The cross-sectional question contrasts three priors. Under
\textcite{BerkGreen2004}, capital flows erode net-alpha differences
across funds in equilibrium and no monotone activeness--alpha
gradient is predicted. \textcite{AmihudGoyenko2013} argue that the
residual share of fund return variance not explained by systematic
factors, $1-R^2$, captures productive idiosyncratic
risk-taking by managers with information advantages and is therefore
a forward-looking predictor of alpha; under their prior the gradient
should be positive. \textcite{CremersPetajisto2009}, working with a
holdings-based Active Share measure, document a positive
activeness--alpha gradient that survives net of fees in the most
active quintile but deteriorates sharply for closet indexers in the
lowest quintile. The empirical question is which of these patterns
the data exhibit.

\input{tables/table_activeness_chars}

Table~\ref{tab:activeness_chars} reports the cross-sectional
characteristics of active funds sorted into five quintiles by their
time-series mean activeness measure. The sort produces a clear
monotone gradient on the activeness measure itself --- the design
of the sort guarantees this --- but also reveals that high-activeness
funds carry systematically higher fees and lower TNA. The
fee gradient runs from approximately 0.80\% in Q1 (closet indexers)
to approximately 1.38\% in Q5 (most active), and the TNA gradient
runs from approximately \$3.2 billion in Q1 to approximately \$1.0
billion in Q5. The pattern is consistent with the
\textcite{CremersPetajisto2009} documentation that high-Active-Share
funds in the U.S.\ market are systematically smaller and charge
higher fees than closet indexers; it is also consistent with the
\textcite{BerkGreen2004} equilibrium prediction that diseconomies of
scale prevent skilled managers (whose gross alpha is the source of
their competitive advantage) from absorbing arbitrarily large
amounts of capital.

\input{tables/table_activeness_alpha_r2}

Table~\ref{tab:activeness_alpha_r2} reports the
factor-model alpha and factor loadings by activeness quintile, where
activeness is the rolling-window $1-R^2$ measure of
\textcite{AmihudGoyenko2013}. The headline result is the Q5--Q1
spread on net alpha: in the equal-weighted Net panel, the spread is
modestly positive (+0.142\% per annum, statistically insignificant);
in the value-weighted Net panel, the spread is more substantially
positive (+1.326\% per annum, statistically insignificant).

The interpretation that emerges is more nuanced than a simple behavioral
premium story would predict. High-activeness funds carry both higher fees
and higher gross alphas; the two effects approximately cancel on net,
leaving the Q5--Q1 net-alpha spread statistically indistinguishable from
zero in both equal- and value-weighted aggregations. The cross-sectional
activeness sort is therefore consistent with the \textcite{BerkGreen2004}
equilibrium prediction at the activeness margin: fees and gross alphas
are jointly priced such that net-of-fee differences are arbitraged away
on average. The data do not, on this sort, supply independent
cross-sectional evidence that investors pay a premium for activeness
that destroys risk-adjusted value.

However, this negative cross-sectional result does not dissolve the
broader puzzle. The aggregate equal-weighted active fund still
underperforms its factor benchmark by -1.361\% per annum net of fees
(Section~\ref{sec:aggregate_performance}); the BSW decomposition still
identifies a genuinely unskilled mass of approximately 21\% with no
detectable skilled mass (Section~\ref{sec:fdr_results}); loser
persistence remains robust (Section~\ref{sec:persistence}); and the
Bai--Perron regime shift at September~2011 still marks a material
deterioration in active managerial performance
(Section~\ref{sec:subperiod}). The behavioral chapter that follows
therefore investigates the channels of investor decision-making that
produce continued allocation to active funds in aggregate, conditional
on a body of empirical evidence in which the cross-sectional activeness
margin is approximately rational but the aggregate, time-series, and
tail evidence remain materially adverse.

\input{tables/table_activeness_alpha_te}

Table~\ref{tab:activeness_alpha_te} reports the
corresponding sort using the tracking-error robustness measure. The
qualitative pattern is broadly consistent with the $1-R^2$ sort: the
equal-weighted Q5--Q1 net-alpha spread is small in magnitude
(+0.432\% per annum, statistically insignificant), while the
value-weighted analogue is materially positive (+1.937\% per annum,
significant at 5\%) and driven by a small number of high-tracking-error
funds with strong recent performance. The point estimates differ from
those of the $1-R^2$ sort, which is expected given the methodological
distinction between the two measures noted by
\textcite{CremersPetajisto2009} (tracking error conflates factor
exposure with security selection in a way that $1-R^2$ does not).
The qualitative agreement on the equal-weighted result --- a
statistically indistinguishable activeness premium net of fees --- is
the central robustness finding of this section.

\input{tables/table_activeness_monotonicity}

A statistically significant Q5--Q1 spread does not by
itself imply that the alpha gradient is monotonically increasing or
decreasing across all five quintiles --- the pattern could be
U-shaped or non-monotone with the endpoints dominating. Following
the convention in \textcite{CremersPetajisto2009},
\textcite{AmihudGoyenko2013}, and \textcite{BerkVanBinsbergen2015},
Table~\ref{tab:activeness_monotonicity} reports the
\textcite{PattonTimmermann2010} monotonic relation (MR) bootstrap
test for both activeness measures and all four panel combinations
(EW Gross, VW Gross, EW Net, VW Net). The test bootstraps the joint
distribution of the four inter-quintile differences
$\Delta_q = \alpha_q - \alpha_{q-1}$ for $q = 2, \ldots, 5$ under
the least-favorable null configuration (each quintile's excess
return series recentered by subtracting its full-sample Carhart
alpha, imposing $\alpha_q = 0$ for all $q$), and tests whether the
gradient is monotone in either direction.

On the net-alpha panels, the MR test fails to reject the null of no
monotone gradient for either activeness measure: under $1-R^2$
activeness, the equal-weighted net $p_\downarrow$ is 0.210 and the
value-weighted net $p_\downarrow$ is 0.738; under tracking-error
activeness, the equal-weighted net $p_\downarrow$ is 0.950 and the
value-weighted net $p_\downarrow$ is 0.991. None is statistically
significant at conventional thresholds, and the same is true of the
$p_\uparrow$ alternatives. The MR test therefore corroborates the
endpoint evidence: the cross-section of net-of-fee alpha exhibits no
detectable monotone gradient across the activeness distribution,
consistent with the Berk--Green equilibrium reading at the
activeness margin and inconsistent with a simple monotone behavioral
premium story.

\section{Performance persistence}
\label{sec:persistence}

\noindent The persistence of fund-level alpha is a distinct empirical
question from the level of average alpha. Even if the average net
alpha is approximately zero (or modestly negative), persistence in
performance would imply that the cross-sectional dispersion of
realised returns carries forecastable signal --- and would, under
the \textcite{BerkGreen2004} model, be inconsistent with the
rational equilibrium because investors should rationally arbitrage
predictable performance away.

The test design --- formation and holding period windows, cohort structure,
decile construction, and the KTWW bootstrap null --- is described in
Section~\ref{sec:persistence_design}.

\pagestyle{lscapeblank}%
\input{tables/table_persistence}
\end{landscape}
\lscapenumfalse
\pagestyle{plain}%

Table~\ref{tab:persistence} displays the results of the \textcite{Carhart1997} parametric 
and \textcite{KosowskiTimmermannWermersWhite2006} bootstrap persistence
tests on 36-month formation, 12-month holding non-overlapping
cohort-period Carhart alphas. The full-sample Panel A documents that
the bottom decile (D10, lowest formation $t(\hat{\alpha})$) carries a
holding-period Carhart alpha of -0.189\% per month
($t = -3.49$, $p < 0.001$ parametric, $p_{LB} = 0.143$ left-tail
bootstrap), while the top decile (D1, highest formation
$t(\hat{\alpha})$) carries a holding-period alpha statistically
indistinguishable from zero ($-0.052\%$, $t = -1.06$). The D1$-$D10
spread of $0.136\%$ per month ($t = 1.79$) is significant at 10\%
parametrically and reflects the magnitude of the persistence asymmetry.

The persistence pattern is consistent with the
\textcite{Carhart1997} finding that the persistence asymmetry lies
in the left tail: past losers continue to lose, but past winners
do not continue to win. The interpretation under the
\textcite{BerkGreen2004} model is that the asymmetry reflects an
identifiable subset of poorly-managed funds that capital is slow to
abandon, while skilled managers' gross alpha is rationally
arbitraged away by capital inflow. The
\textcite{BerkVanBinsbergen2015} dollar-value framework provides a
complementary reading: the loser persistence reflects funds whose
size-adjusted skill is genuinely negative, while the winner
non-persistence reflects the rational equilibrium prediction that
capital flows have absorbed any positive size-adjusted skill.

The sub-period panels (C through E) document that the persistence
pattern is broadly stable across the three Bai--Perron-identified
regimes, with the loser-persistence finding robust in each
sub-period and the winner non-persistence finding robust in each.
Panel D (the 2006--2011 positive-alpha regime) exhibits modestly
positive D1 and middle-decile alphas, but the D10 alpha remains
materially negative; the asymmetry of persistence is therefore not
an artifact of any single regime. Panel F reports the cross-regime
persistence test on cohorts straddling regime boundaries; the
persistence asymmetry is amplified in this configuration, with the
D1$-$D10 spread reaching +0.287\% per month (significant at 1\%) --- evidence that the
regime-shift evidence of Section~\ref{sec:subperiod} is
behaviorally identified rather than merely statistical.

Table~\ref{tab:activeness_persistence} extends the
persistence analysis along the activeness dimension, using a
$10 \times 3$ bivariate sort. Within each cohort, funds are
partitioned into three terciles by formation-window $1-R^2$ (cohort-local, 
computed within the same 36-month window used to rank fund
alphas), and within each tercile into ten alpha-decile portfolios.
The headline finding concerns the cross-tercile pattern of within-tercile
persistence spreads. In the lowest activeness tercile T1
(closet indexers), the D1-D10 alpha spread is -0.014\% per
month, statistically indistinguishable from zero --- formation rank
carries no information among the least-active funds. In the middle
activeness tercile T2, the spread widens to +0.172\% (t = 2.26,

reaches +0.300\% (t = 1.83, significant at 10\%, annualised
approximately 3.6\%).

The pattern is that formation-rank information becomes more useful
as funds become more active. The T1 (closet-indexer) population
exhibits no predictability between past and future relative
performance; the T2 and T3 (more-active) populations exhibit
detectable persistence in the D1-D10 spread. In the highest
activeness tercile, the D10 alpha is -0.221\% per month
(t = -1.63), slightly worse than the unconditional Panel-A D10 of
-0.189\%, while T3 D1 carries a small positive point estimate
(+0.080\%) that is statistically indistinguishable from zero. The
within-tercile spreads are therefore driven jointly by D1 winners
performing modestly better and D10 losers performing modestly worse
as activeness rises. The pattern is consistent with the
\textcite{AmihudGoyenko2013} prediction that activeness predicts
gross alpha at the extremes, and bridges the activeness chapter and
the behavioral chapter: the funds with the strongest activeness
characteristics are the ones whose past performance carries the
most forecastable signal for subsequent holding periods.

\section{Sub-period analysis}
\label{sec:subperiod}

\noindent The rolling alpha series of Figure~\ref{fig:rolling_alphas}
exhibits visible non-stationarity. The Bai--Perron structural break
test reported in full in Appendix~\ref{app:subperiod} identifies
five breaks at the BIC-optimal break count (December 1999, December
2002, December 2005, September 2011, and January 2023), defining six
regimes. The first three breaks partition the pre-Global Financial
Crisis era into late-1990s tail, dot-com peak, and post-crash
recovery sub-periods, each with regime-mean alpha within the range
$\pm$1.7\% per annum. These three breaks each define segments below
the 60-month minimum recommended by \textcite{BarrasScailletWermers2010}
for reliable bootstrap inference and are therefore collapsed into a
single pre-2006 regime (P1) for the sub-period analysis.

The fourth break, at September 2011 with a tight 95\% Newey--West HAC
confidence interval of March 2011--December 2011, marks the largest
single regime-mean shift in the sample: the regime-mean alpha drops
from +0.74\% per annum in the 2006--Sep 2011 segment to -1.39\%
per annum in the Oct 2011--2026 segment, a swing of approximately 210
basis points. The fifth break, at January 2023 with a wider confidence
interval of August 2021--February 2025, marks a further intensification
of the post-2011 alpha compression; its 36-month post-break segment
is too short for separate bootstrap inference and is consequently
pooled with the Oct~2011-onward segment in the sub-period analysis.

The three-regime partition adopted for the sub-period bootstrap and
BSW analyses is therefore: P1 (December 1996--December 2005, 109
months, regime-mean +0.05\%), P2 (January 2006--September 2011,
69 months, regime-mean +0.74\%), and P3 (October 2011--January
2026, 172 months, regime-mean -1.39\%). Each regime has $T \geq
60$ monthly observations. The full bootstrap and BSW results by
sub-period are reported in Appendix~\ref{app:subperiod}.

The sub-period evidence is the strongest single piece of regime-specific
evidence in the dissertation. The Storey $\hat{\pi}_0$ estimate
collapses from approximately 81\% in P1 to 60.8\% in P3, with the
genuinely unskilled mass $T^-_{\gamma^*}$ rising correspondingly from
approximately 11\% to 35.4\%. The structural meaning of this change ---
whether it reflects passive competition compressing active margins,
post-Global-Financial-Crisis algorithmic trading eliminating
cross-sectional alpha opportunities, or selection effects from fund
entry/exit dynamics --- is taken up in Chapter~\ref{ch:discussion}.

\chapter{BEHAVIORAL HYPOTHESES AND TESTS}
\label{ch:behavioral}

\noindent The empirical chapters preceding this one establish three facts
that constrain any behavioral interpretation of the active management
puzzle. First, the equal-weighted net alpha for active funds is
materially negative (-1.361\% per annum) while the value-weighted
analogue is smaller in magnitude (-0.900\%); the puzzle is located in
the cross-section. Second, the
\textcite{BarrasScailletWermers2010} false-discovery decomposition
identifies a population unskilled mass of approximately 21\% (at $\gamma^*=20\%$) with no
detectable population skilled mass, and a Bai--Perron structural break
at September 2011 that materially intensifies the unskilled
proportion in the post-break regime. 
Third, activeness does not predict significantly different risk-adjusted returns net of fees in the cross-section — the gross alpha premium associated with higher activeness is approximately offset by higher fees, consistent with Berk and Green (2004) equilibrium pricing at the activeness margin. These facts jointly
falsify the simple ``investors are paying for skill'' interpretation:
investors are paying a premium for activeness that destroys
risk-adjusted value, and the channel of that mistake must be
identified before the puzzle can be claimed to be solved.

This chapter tests four behavioral hypotheses against the
\textcite{BerkGreen2004} rational null on the trimmed analytical panel
(January 1995--December 2023) using the
\textcite{SirriTufano1998} piecewise rank specification with sentiment,
margin-debt, and lottery-proxy interactions. The
\textcite{Petersen2009} two-way clustering on fund and calendar month is
applied throughout. Each hypothesis is identified within fund using
either fund fixed effects (H1, H2, H3) or
Lipper-category $\times$ year-month fixed effects (H4, where the
Lipper-style time-shocks must be absorbed for the fee triple
interactions to be cleanly identified). Section~\ref{sec:H1} reports
the sentiment-convexity hypothesis (H1); Section~\ref{sec:H2} the
disposition / illusion-of-control hypothesis (H2);
Section~\ref{sec:H3} the lottery demand horse race (H3);
Section~\ref{sec:H4} the fee-elasticity hypothesis (H4); and
Section~\ref{sec:ppf} the Psychological Premium Framework that
translates the H3 estimates into welfare-relevant alpha basis points.
Section~\ref{sec:behavioral_summary} synthesises the four-hypothesis
evidence.

\section{Panel regression specification}
\label{sec:panel_spec}

\noindent The dependent variable is the calculated proportional fund
flow $\text{Flow}_{i,t}$ defined in Equation~\ref{eq:flow},
winsorised at the 1st/99th percentile and excluding December
observations. The principal regressor is the lagged Sirri--Tufano
fractional rank within Lipper category, partitioned into three
piecewise segments
$R^{\text{LOW}}_{i,t-12}$,
$R^{\text{MID}}_{i,t-12}$, and
$R^{\text{HIGH}}_{i,t-12}$. The 12-month lag of the rank ensures
that flow at month $t$ responds to performance through end of month
$t-12$, consistent with the \textcite{SirriTufano1998} timing
convention and with the empirical observation that flow responses
take time to materialise after performance information becomes
available. Controls $X_{i,t}$ include $\log(\text{TNA}_{i,t-1})$,
$\log(\text{Age}_{i,t})$, the trailing 36-month return volatility,
and the contemporaneous Lipper-category mean flow (a within-style
demand control). The contemporaneous category mean flow is the
strongest single predictor of fund-level flow and absorbs the
aggregate flow shocks within style that would otherwise be omitted-
variable bias for the H1--H3 hypotheses.

The aligned regression sample after the lag, fund-fixed-effect, and
state-variable joins comprises approximately 233{,}000 fund-month
observations on 1{,}713 unique funds across 22 Lipper styles,
spanning January 1995 through December 2023 (the trimmed panel
date cap). Robustness specifications under the
Lipper-category $\times$ year-month fixed-effect structure are
reported in Appendix~\ref{app:behavioral_robustness}; lagged
state-variable variants are reported there as well.

\section{H1: Sentiment-convexity hypothesis}
\label{sec:H1}

\noindent H1 predicts that high-sentiment regimes amplify the
flow--performance convexity. The hypothesis is rejected only if the
sentiment-rank-segment interactions $\delta_q$ are jointly zero
across $q \in \{\text{LOW}, \text{MID}, \text{HIGH}\}$.
Table~\ref{tab:H1_regression} reports the panel regression results under three
sentiment proxies: the Baker--Wurgler high-sentiment regime indicator
$D^{\text{SENT}}$, the orthogonalised continuous Baker--Wurgler
sentiment $\text{SENT}^{\perp}$, and the AAII bull-minus-bear
spread regime indicator $D^{\text{AAII}}$.

The flow convexity documented by \textcite{SirriTufano1998} is largely confirmed by the results. The coefficient of $R^{\text{HIGH}}$ is larger than the coefficients of $R^{\text{MID}}$ and $R^{\text{LOW}}$, so funds in the high performance segment receive more flows compared to funds in the low and mid segments when the fund's performance improves. All three coefficients are significant at 1\%, whilst the coefficient of $R^{\text{MID}}$ is surprisingly lower than that of $R^{\text{LOW}}$, which suggests investors are more sensitive to past performance on the low segment than they are on the mid segment. 

The joint $\chi^2_3$ test of $\delta_{\text{LOW}} =
\delta_{\text{MID}} = \delta_{\text{HIGH}} = 0$ rejects the rational null at 5\%
significance under both the Baker--Wurgler and AAII regime indicators, which means sentiment regime significantly changes the magnitude of the flow-performance relationship for at least one of the performance segments. 
The asymmetry test
$\delta_{\text{HIGH}} > \delta_{\text{LOW}}$ produces a
one-sided $z$-statistic close to zero under the Baker--Wurgler proxy
($z = +0.06$). Under the
AAII regime indicator the asymmetry test is significant at 5\%
($z = +2.11$, $p = 0.018$), with $\delta_{\text{HIGH}}$ materially
larger than $\delta_{\text{LOW}}$. Under the orthogonalised
continuous Baker--Wurgler sentiment, the joint test is marginal
($\chi^2 = 6.92$, $p = 0.074$),
which is expected: orthogonalising sentiment against macro controls
removes the regime-shift component that the discrete indicators
preserve.

The headline interpretation under H1 is that sentiment amplifies
flow-into-winners more than it amplifies flow-out-of-losers, with
the asymmetry detectable at 5\% significance under the
\textcite{BakerWurgler2007} discrete regime convention adopted in
the headline specification and under the
\textcite{IndroSentiment2004} retail-survey alternative. The
asymmetry is qualitatively consistent with the
\textcite{WuZhang2024} finding on Chinese mutual fund data and with
the \textcite{BenRephaelKandelWohl2012} finding on the relationship
between aggregate sentiment and the flow--performance sensitivity.
The rejection of the rational null is modest in economic magnitude
--- the implied differential flow response is on the order of
half a standard deviation of monthly proportional flow --- but is
statistically detectable on the panel and survives the lagged
state-variable robustness check of
Appendix~\ref{app:behavioral_robustness}.

As expected, the coefficient of the standalone sentiment indicator without any interactions is positive and significant under specifications 2 and 3, which suggests fund flows increase under high-sentiment regimes. On the other hand, coefficients of the control variables size and age are negative and significant under all specifications, so fund flows clearly decrease as a fund gets bigger and older. Conversely, funds receive higher inflows when the flow into their style group as a whole increases, evidenced by the positive and highly significant coefficients on style flow. Finally, volatility seems to have no effect on fund flows as the coefficient on the control variable is insignificant under all specifications. However, this is due to the fact that most of the spread in return volatility lies the cross-section (between funds) rather than in the time-series (within funds), and the former is being absorbed by the fund fixed-effect on this specification. This is confirmed by the negative and highly significant coefficients on return volatility under specifications with no fund fixed effect, which are reported on tables~\ref{tab:H4_regression},~\ref{tab:H4_lagged},~\ref{tab:H1_robustness},~\ref{tab:H2_robustness},~\ref{tab:H3_robustness} and ~\ref{tab:H4_robustness}.

\section{H2: Disposition effect and illusion of control}
\label{sec:H2}

H2 tests two competing predictions about how high leverage regimes
modulate the loser-segment flow--performance slope. The baseline
loser-segment coefficient $\beta_{\text{LOW}}$ is positive: an
improvement in relative rank within the bottom tercile attracts
higher flows, and a deterioration generates outflows. The interaction
coefficient $\delta^{\text{IoC}}_{\text{LOW}}$ determines whether
high margin debt \emph{steepens} or \emph{flattens} that slope. The
disposition / illusion-of-control prediction of
\textcite{ShefrinStatman1985} is $\delta^{\text{IoC}}_{\text{LOW}} < 0$:
leveraged investors are more reluctant to redeem from losers,
dampening the outflow response to rank deterioration and flattening
the slope. The \textcite{BrunnermeierPedersen2009} margin-call
alternative predicts $\delta^{\text{IoC}}_{\text{LOW}} > 0$: funding
constraints force accelerated liquidation of losing positions,
steepening the slope and amplifying the outflow response to any given
rank deterioration.

\input{tables/table_H2_regression}

\noindent Table~\ref{tab:H2_regression} reports the panel regression results
under three competing state-variable proxies: the detrended
margin-debt regime indicator $D^{\text{MD,Det}}$, the inverse
put--call ratio regime indicator $D^{\text{INV-PCR}}_t$, and a
discriminant specification that includes both regime indicators
jointly. The baseline flow--performance convexity is confirmed
across all specifications. The winner segment commands a slope
roughly twice the loser segment, replicating the
\textcite{SirriTufano1998} pattern.

Under the headline margin-debt proxy, the loser-segment interaction
$\delta^{\text{IoC}}_{\text{LOW}}$ is significantly \emph{positive}
($\hat{\delta} = +0.0148$, $z = +2.15$, $p < 0.05$). A positive
coefficient means the loser-segment slope \emph{steepens} under high
margin debt: rank improvements among losers attract more inflows, and
rank deteriorations generate more outflows, relative to the
low-leverage baseline. This is the opposite of the disposition
prediction. The one-sided $p$-value for the Brunnermeier--Pedersen
prediction ($\delta > 0$) is $0.016$; the one-sided $p$-value for
the disposition prediction ($\delta < 0$) is $0.984$. The result
therefore rejects \textcite{ShefrinStatman1985} in favour of
\textcite{BrunnermeierPedersen2009}.

The economic interpretation is that high margin-debt regimes are
associated with \emph{amplified} rank sensitivity in the loser
segment, not with the flattened sensitivity that disposition-driven
retention would produce. Leveraged investors, far from holding losers
out of sunk-cost bias, appear to liquidate them more aggressively the
further they fall in the relative performance distribution. This
finding does not falsify the disposition effect at the
individual-investor level, where it is robustly documented by
\textcite{Odean1998} and others; it falsifies the prediction that the
effect produces a detectable \emph{aggregate} flow signature
conditional on margin-debt regimes. The
\textcite{BrunnermeierPedersen2009} mechanism --- in which
balance-sheet constraints force redemptions during high-leverage
episodes --- is the more parsimonious reading of the data.

The winner-segment interaction $\delta^{\text{IoC}}_{\text{HIGH}}$
is positive but insignificant ($\hat{\delta} = +0.0095$, $z = 0.96$),
and the mid-segment interaction is likewise insignificant, so the
margin-debt effect is concentrated at the loser end, consistent with
the funding-constraint mechanism which is most binding for positions
already generating losses. The joint $\chi^2_3$ test rejects the null
of no margin-debt modulation at 1\% ($p = 0.010$).

The inverse put--call ratio specification ($D^{\text{INV-PCR}}$)
produces a near-zero and insignificant loser-segment interaction
($\hat{\delta} = -0.0013$, $z = -0.20$), and the joint test fails to
reject ($p = 0.931$). The PCR proxy therefore does not corroborate
the margin-debt result on its own. The discriminant specification,
which includes both $D^{\text{MD,Det}}$ and the Baker--Wurgler
sentiment indicator $D^{\text{SENT}}$ jointly, preserves the
positive loser-segment margin-debt interaction ($\hat{\delta} =
+0.0133$, $z = +1.91$, $p < 0.10$) and leaves the H1 sentiment
interactions broadly unchanged, confirming that the margin-debt
effect is not an artefact of omitted sentiment correlation.

The control variables are consistent with the H1 results. Fund size
and age carry negative and significant coefficients across all
specifications, confirming that larger and older funds attract
less flow. Style flow is positive and significant,
reflecting the well-documented category-flow spillover. Return
volatility is insignificant under fund fixed effects for the same
reason noted in Section~\ref{sec:H1}: the within-fund variation in
volatility is limited, and the cross-sectional spread is absorbed by
the fixed effect. Robustness under the lagged state-variable and
alternative fixed-effect specifications of
Appendix~\ref{app:behavioral_robustness} confirms the directional
finding.

\section{H3: Lottery demand and skewness preference}
\label{sec:H3}

H3 predicts that high-sentiment regimes amplify investor
demand for funds with lottery-like return distributions. The
specification regresses fund flow on three candidate lottery
proxies, each interacted with the sentiment regime indicator:
$\text{ActR2}$ (the rational alpha-prediction channel of
\textcite{AmihudGoyenko2013}), $\text{ActSkew}$ (sample skewness
of the trailing 36 monthly gross returns, the full-distribution
lottery measure), and $\text{MAX12}$ (the maximum monthly gross
return over the trailing 12 months, the right-tail-only measure
following \textcite{BaliCakiciWhitelaw2011} and the fund-level
extension of \textcite{ChengEtal2025}). Each proxy is standardised
within sample. The proxy validation procedure that selects these
three from the four candidates considered (the fourth being the
unconditional 36-month return realised volatility, which fails the
proxy validation tests)
is documented in detail in Appendix~\ref{app:proxy_validation}.

Table~\ref{tab:H3_regression} reports the four-column horse race.
Column (1) is the baseline Sirri--Tufano specification without
any lottery proxy, providing the benchmark coefficients. Columns
(2)--(4) add each of the three lottery proxies in turn, fully
interacted with the Baker--Wurgler sentiment regime indicator.

The headline finding is the asymmetric pattern across the three
proxies. The $\text{ActR2}$ joint $\chi^2_2 = 2.25$
($p = 0.325$) is not statistically significant, and the
sentiment-amplification $z$-statistic is also indistinguishable
from zero ($z = -0.02$). The $\text{ActR2}$ proxy detects no
signal in either the joint test or the asymmetry direction on
the current sample.

The $\text{ActSkew}$ joint $\chi^2_2 = 1.95$ ($p = 0.378$) is
not statistically significant. Sample skewness of the trailing
36 monthly returns is therefore not a detectably exploited
characteristic in flow decisions. This is consistent with the
\textcite{BarberisHuang2008} prediction that probability weighting
is concentrated on extreme tail outcomes rather than on the
overall skewness of the return distribution.

The $\text{MAX12}$ joint $\chi^2_2 = 11.33$ ($p = 0.003$) is
significant at 1\%, and the directional asymmetry test produces
$z = +1.54$ ($p = 0.062$ one-sided, marginally significant), consistent with the H3
behavioral prediction. High-sentiment regimes amplify flow into
funds with extreme realised maximum returns, and the magnitude of
the amplification is statistically detectable on the panel. The
behavioral interpretation is direct: investors respond to extreme
right-tail realised returns as lottery-like signals, and this
response intensifies during high-sentiment regimes when investor
risk-tolerance for extreme outcomes is elevated. The finding aligns
with the \textcite{BaliCakiciWhitelaw2011} stock-level evidence
that maximum daily return generates a negative cross-sectional
return relationship through investor lottery preference, and with
the \textcite{ChengEtal2025} fund-level extension that establishes
the same channel in the mutual fund flow domain.

A notable feature of the \text{MAX12} specification is that the
sentiment regime indicator $D^{\text{SENT}}$ loses its own
significance (column~4) precisely when $\text{MAX12}$ is
significant --- a pattern absent in columns~(2) and~(3). This is
most naturally interpreted as a substitution effect rather than
multicollinearity: $\text{MAX12}$ is mechanically elevated during
high-sentiment periods, since bull markets produce more extreme
right-tail monthly returns, and therefore absorbs the time-series
variation in investor risk appetite that the macro sentiment
indicator was proxying in the cruder specifications. Far from
being a statistical artefact, this substitution strengthens the
behavioral interpretation --- $\text{MAX12}$ captures the
sentiment-lottery channel at the fund level with greater
precision than the aggregate binary indicator.

The Lipper-category $\times$ year-month fixed-effect and lagged state-variable robustness checks
(Appendix~\ref{app:behavioral_robustness}) corroborate the directional $\text{MAX12}$ pattern
at marginal significance. Under the lagged-sentiment specification,
$\text{MAX12}$ joint $\chi^2_2 = 10.72$ ($p = 0.005$) with $z = +1.58$
($p = 0.057$); under the Lipper $\times$ yearmo fixed-effect
specification, $\text{ActSkew}$ becomes statistically significant
($\chi^2_2 = 20.74$, $p < 0.001$; $z = +2.17$, $p = 0.015$) while
$\text{MAX12}$ retains a 5\% joint test ($\chi^2_2 = 8.36$,
$p = 0.015$). $\text{ActR2}$ remains insignificant under both
robustness specifications.

\section{H4: Fee elasticity}
\label{sec:H4}

H4 tests whether flow--fee elasticity is heterogeneous
across performance segments and sentiment regimes in a manner
inconsistent with the rational benchmark. Under the \textcite{BerkGreen2004} rational equilibrium,
fee sensitivity should be invariant to sentiment regime: the
unconditional fee--sentiment interaction $\eta$ and the three
rank-conditioned triple interactions $\delta^F_{\text{LOW}}$,
$\delta^F_{\text{MID}}$, and $\delta^F_{\text{HIGH}}$ should be
jointly zero. The behavioral
alternative permits heterogeneity: \textcite{DelGuercioTkac2008}
document that institutional and retail clienteles exhibit
materially different fee sensitivities, and
\textcite{ChoiLaibsonMadrian2010} document that even sophisticated
investors fail to minimise fees among otherwise-identical S\&P 500
index funds. The behavioral prediction is therefore a non-zero
joint $\chi^2$ on the triple interactions.

\begingroup
\captionsetup{font=footnotesize, justification=raggedright, singlelinecheck=false}  % T2.11: match caption size to longtable body (\fontsize{9}{11}) and left-align
\input{tables/table_H4_regression}
\endgroup

\noindent Table~\ref{tab:H4_regression} reports the H4 regression under three
sentiment proxies. A preliminary observation on the expense ratio main
effect $\theta$: it is negative and marginally significant in the
baseline column~(1) ($\hat{\theta} = -0.0016$, $t = -1.89$), as
expected under standard fee-sensitivity, but reverses sign in
columns~(2)--(4) once the fee-rank interactions $\phi_q$ are
included. This is a coefficient redistribution artefact rather than
a substantive finding. In the interacted model, $\theta$ identifies
the fee effect at the degenerate point where all three rank segments
equal zero simultaneously --- a point outside the support of the
data. The economically relevant quantity is the total marginal fee
effect evaluated at a realistic rank, $\hat{\theta} + \sum_q
\hat{\phi}_q R^q$, which remains negative throughout the full support of the rank distribution, 
including at the extreme winner tail where $\hat{\phi}_\text{HIGH} > 0$. The sign reversal of $\theta$ therefore
carries no economic interpretation and should not be read as
evidence that higher fees attract more flows. 

The joint $\chi^2_4$ test of $\eta = \delta^F_{\text{LOW}} =
\delta^F_{\text{MID}} = \delta^F_{\text{HIGH}} = 0$ --- that is,
that neither the unconditional fee--sentiment interaction nor any
of the three rank-conditioned triple interactions is driven by
sentiment --- is statistically insignificant under all three
proxies: $\chi^2 = 4.45$ ($p = 0.349$) under the Baker--Wurgler
high-sentiment regime indicator $D^{\text{SENT}}$, $\chi^2 = 4.46$
($p = 0.347$) under the orthogonalised continuous sentiment
$\text{SENT}^{\perp}$, and $\chi^2 = 5.17$ ($p = 0.270$) under
the AAII regime indicator $D^{\text{AAII}}$. The $z$-statistic on
$\hat{\eta}$ --- the unconditional fee--sentiment interaction
$\text{ExpRatio}_i \times D^{\text{SENT}}_t$ --- is similarly
indistinguishable from zero ($z = +0.74$, $p = 0.231$ one-sided),
confirming that aggregate fee tolerance does not shift with
sentiment independently of rank segment.

H4 is therefore consistent with the \textcite{BerkGreen2004}
rational null. The empirical signature of behavioral fee
heterogeneity that the specification was designed to detect ---
sentiment-driven flow that depends on fee level conditional on
performance segment --- is not present in the data at conventional
inferential thresholds.

The negative result for H4 is a clean inferential outcome, not a
failed test. Three implications merit emphasis. First, the result rules out sentiment-contingent fee blindness
as a mechanism: fee sensitivity does not diminish during
high-sentiment regimes, so the behavioral distortions detected
in H1 and H3 do not operate through investors becoming
selectively inattentive to costs when optimism is elevated. Second, the result locates the behavioral
signature in H1, H2, and H3 rather than in fee processing per se;
the puzzle is in the convexity, the loser segment, and the lottery
proxy, not in fee-level inattention. 

Two lower-order interactions merit comment. The
$\phi_{\text{LOW}}$ coefficient ($R^{\text{LOW}} \times
\text{ExpRatio}$) is negative and significant: among loser-segment
funds, higher fees amplify the outflow response to rank
deterioration. A fund that charges high fees and still
underperforms has failed on both dimensions simultaneously, and
investors penalise this compounded failure more severely than they
penalise a cheap underperformer at the same rank. 

The $\phi_{\text{HIGH}}$ coefficient ($R^{\text{HIGH}} \times
\text{ExpRatio}$) is positive and significant, which would on its
face suggest that higher-fee winners attract more flow. The total marginal fee effect at a typical winner fund (rank
0.85, where $R^{\text{LOW}} = 0.20$, $R^{\text{MID}} = 0.60$,
$R^{\text{HIGH}} = 0.05$) is $\hat{\theta} +
\hat{\phi}_{\text{LOW}}(0.20) + \hat{\phi}_{\text{MID}}(0.60) +
\hat{\phi}_{\text{HIGH}}(0.05) = 0.0014 + (-0.0227)(0.20) +
(-0.0017)(0.60) + (0.0146)(0.05) = -0.0034$, which remains
firmly negative. The positive $\hat{\phi}_{\text{HIGH}}$
attenuates the fee penalty at the winner end but does not reverse
it anywhere in the support of the rank distribution. The mechanism implied is
the fee-as-quality-signal channel of \textcite{DelGuercioTkac2008}
and \textcite{SirriTufano1998}: a high-fee fund that still reaches
the top quintile has cleared a larger gross-alpha hurdle and may be
inferred to be more skilled. 

Taken together, the two lower-order
interactions form a coherent asymmetric pattern --- fees amplify
punishment at the loser end and attenuate it at the winner end ---
that is consistent with conditional rational inference rather than
fee blindness. Neither coefficient threatens the H4 null because
the triple interactions $\delta^F_q$, which test whether these fee
patterns intensify with sentiment, are uniformly insignificant
across all three sentiment proxies.

\section{The psychological premium framework}
\label{sec:ppf}

The H3 finding that $\text{MAX12}$ amplifies flow into
high-sentiment regimes carries an implication beyond the
hypothesis test itself: investors are revealed to be willing to
sacrifice expected return for incremental lottery exposure, and the
magnitude of this sacrifice can be quantified in welfare-relevant
units. The Psychological Premium Framework introduced in this
section computes the marginal rate of substitution between
performance rank and the lottery proxy implied by the flow
indifference condition, and converts this rate into basis points of
annual Carhart alpha that investors are revealed to be willing to
forgo for an incremental standard deviation of lottery exposure.

The construction follows three steps. First, the Sirri--Tufano
piecewise rank specification of H3 is re-estimated with each rank
segment fully interacted with the lottery proxy and the sentiment
regime indicator. Second, the marginal rate of substitution at a
given segment is computed as the ratio of the partial derivative
of flow with respect to the lottery proxy to the partial derivative
of flow with respect to the rank within that segment, evaluated at
the population mean of the controls:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:mrs}
\text{MRS}_q = -\left.\frac{\partial \text{Flow} / \partial \text{Lottery}}{\partial \text{Flow} / \partial R^q}\right|_{X = \bar{X}}.
\end{equation}
Third, the marginal rate of substitution is converted into alpha
basis points using the rank-to-alpha mapping documented in the
momentum-quintile sort of Section~\ref{sec:portfolio_sorts}. The
Q5--Q1 momentum spread is $+2.150\%$ per annum on the active
equal-weighted Carhart alpha, with a midpoint-to-midpoint rank
distance of $0.8$ across the five quintiles, implying a conversion
rate of $269$ basis points per rank-point. Multiplying the
$\text{MRS}_q$ by $269$ yields the implied alpha sacrifice in
basis points.

\input{tables/table_psychological_premium}

Table~\ref{tab:psychological_premium} reports the
psychological premium estimates for the three lottery proxies
across the three rank segments and across the two sentiment
regimes. The headline finding is the $\text{MAX12}$
amplification at the middle rank segment. Under the low-sentiment
regime, the $\text{MAX12}$ middle-rank shadow price is $-0.049$
rank-points per standard deviation of lottery exposure ---
implying that investors at the middle rank segment are willing to
sacrifice approximately $0.049 \times 269 \approx 13$ basis points
of annual Carhart alpha for one standard deviation of lottery
exposure. Under the high-sentiment regime, the same shadow price
is $-0.219$, implying a sacrifice of approximately $0.219 \times
269 \approx 59$ basis points. The high-sentiment amplification
factor is approximately $4.5\times$, statistically significant at
5\% (one-sided $p = 0.013$).

The economic magnitude merits emphasis. A 59-basis-point annual
alpha sacrifice for one standard deviation of $\text{MAX12}$
exposure is approximately the difference between an active fund's
gross alpha and a passive benchmark on the present sample. Under
the high-sentiment regime, in other words, investors are revealed
to be willing to forgo the entire risk-adjusted value-add of the
active management industry in exchange for one standard deviation
of right-tail realised return. The behavioral interpretation is
direct: investors are buying fund-level lottery tickets, and the
price they are paying is the active management puzzle.

\section{Synthesis of behavioral evidence}
\label{sec:behavioral_summary}

\noindent Table~\ref{tab:behavioral_summary} summarises the four
hypothesis test outcomes. Three of the four hypotheses (H1, H2 in
its margin-call rather than disposition reading, and H3) generate
detectable distortions of flow--performance dynamics consistent
with behavioral prediction. One (H4) is consistent with the
rational null and is reported as such.

The picture that emerges is consistent with the hybrid empirical
position introduced in Chapter~\ref{ch:introduction}. The
\textcite{BerkGreen2004} rational equilibrium accounts for the
capital-weighted distribution: flow is responsive to performance,
fee elasticity is consistent with Bayesian updating (H4), and the
value-weighted aggregate alpha is small in economic magnitude. The
behavioral channels operate principally on the cross-section: H1
amplifies flow-into-winners under high sentiment regimes, H2's
margin-debt channel amplifies forced redemptions of losers in the
opposite direction to the simple disposition prediction, and H3
generates the lottery premium that converts the cross-sectional
heterogeneity into the welfare-relevant alpha basis points
documented in the psychological premium framework. The empirical
evidence supports a hybrid view: capital-weighted outcomes lie
near the Berk--Green equilibrium while the cross-sectional
heterogeneity that produces the equal-weighted underperformance is
explained by behavioral frictions amplified during high-sentiment
regimes.

\chapter{DISCUSSION}
\label{ch:discussion}

\noindent The empirical evidence assembled in Chapters
\ref{ch:results} and \ref{ch:behavioral} supports a hybrid
empirical position that this chapter develops in detail. The
\textcite{BerkGreen2004} rational equilibrium continues to provide
a clean reading of the capital-weighted distribution of mutual
fund alphas. The cross-sectional heterogeneity that produces the
equal-weighted underperformance is explained by behavioral
frictions whose magnitude varies with aggregate sentiment regimes.
The post-2011 alpha compression --- the strongest single piece of
regime-specific evidence in the dissertation --- locates the
puzzle's recent intensification in a structural shift whose
proximate cause is open to multiple readings, and which warrants
separate treatment.

\section{The Berk--Green equilibrium accepted at the capital-weighted level}
\label{sec:bg_accepted}

\noindent The value-weighted net Carhart alpha for active funds on
the December 1994--February 2026 sample is $-0.900\%$ per annum, an
estimate that is statistically significant at 5\% but economically
modest in magnitude. The corresponding equal-weighted estimate is
$-1.361\%$ per annum, a 46-basis-point gap that quantifies the
locational interpretation of the puzzle introduced in
Section~\ref{sec:motivation}. The fact that the value-weighted
alpha is small, while the equal-weighted alpha is materially worse,
is consistent with the \textcite{BerkGreen2004} prediction that
capital flows are responsive to expected gross alpha, so that size
and skill are correlated in equilibrium. The
\textcite{BerkVanBinsbergen2015} dollar-value framework provides
the complementary reading: managerial skill, when measured as the
product of gross alpha and lagged total net assets, is positive
and persistent in U.S.\ data, even though the average net alpha to
investors is reliably negative on the same data.

The dissertation does not test the Berk--Green equilibrium directly
in dollar terms; the value-weighted Carhart alpha estimate of
$-0.900\%$ reported here is consistent with, but does not formally
estimate, the dollar-value framework of
\textcite{BerkVanBinsbergen2015}. What the present sample
contributes is the demonstration that the equal-weighted /
value-weighted asymmetry is qualitatively unchanged on a sample
that extends fifteen years beyond the canonical
\textcite{FamaFrench2010} window. The post-FF (2010) extension to
February 2026 has not produced a materially different
value-weighted estimate, even though the post-2011 regime-shift
evidence of Section~\ref{sec:subperiod} indicates that the
underlying alpha distribution has compressed substantially in the
recent regime. The interpretation is that the rational equilibrium
of \textcite{BerkGreen2004} is structurally robust at the
capital-weighted level: capital continues to be allocated in a
manner that holds the average size-weighted dollar near zero
size-adjusted alpha, even as the cross-sectional alpha
distribution has shifted downward.

The H4 fee elasticity hypothesis, which is consistent with the
rational null on the present panel, reinforces the
capital-weighted equilibrium reading. The joint test of
$\eta = \delta^F_{\text{LOW}} = \delta^F_{\text{MID}} =
\delta^F_{\text{HIGH}} = 0$ fails to reject under all three
sentiment proxies, indicating that fee sensitivity does not
diminish during high-sentiment regimes --- neither on average
nor differentially across rank segments. This rules out
sentiment-contingent fee blindness as a mechanism: whatever
distortions H1, H2, and H3 introduce into the flow--performance
relationship, they do not operate by making investors selectively
inattentive to costs when optimism is elevated. The behavioral
channels are therefore located in the flow--performance
non-linearity itself rather than in fee processing, a finding
that is consistent with the \textcite{BerkGreen2004} prediction
that rational competition for capital operates through
performance rather than through cost minimisation.

\section{The cross-sectional puzzle: A behavioral account}
\label{sec:behavioral_cross_section}

\noindent The cross-sectional heterogeneity that drives the
equal-weighted underperformance is, on the present evidence, of
behavioral origin. Three distinct channels carry detectable
empirical signatures.

The sentiment-convexity channel of H1 amplifies flow-into-winners
under high-sentiment regimes by a magnitude that is statistically
detectable at 5\% under the discrete Baker--Wurgler regime
indicator and at 5\% under the AAII bull--bear regime indicator
(asymmetry test). The economic interpretation is that sentiment-elevated investors 
over-react to recent winners, channeling
capital into recent strong performers at a rate that exceeds the
rational-Bayesian benchmark and that the
\textcite{SirriTufano1998} convexity already incorporates. The
empirical signature is consistent with the
\textcite{BenRephaelKandelWohl2012} finding on aggregate
flow--performance sensitivity and with the \textcite{WuZhang2024}
finding on Chinese mutual fund data, providing cross-market
external validity for the channel.

The H2 margin-call channel rejects the simple disposition
prediction in favour of the \textcite{BrunnermeierPedersen2009}
margin-call alternative. The finding is conceptually unsurprising
given that aggregate flow data, which mixes retail and
institutional behavior, may dampen the disposition signature that
direct individual-investor data of \textcite{Odean1998} or
\textcite{GrinblattKeloharju2009} more cleanly identifies. The
empirical implication is that behavioral signatures in the loser
segment are more naturally read as margin-driven rather than as
disposition-driven on aggregate flow data.

The H3 lottery-demand channel via $\text{MAX12}$ is the most
economically consequential of the three. The Psychological
Premium Framework estimates a high-sentiment shadow price of 58
basis points of annual Carhart alpha per standard deviation of
$\text{MAX12}$ exposure, against a low-sentiment shadow price of
13 basis points --- a 4.5-fold amplification. The economic
magnitude is on the order of the entire risk-adjusted value-add
gap between active and passive management on the present sample.
The behavioral interpretation is that investors, particularly
during sentiment-elevated regimes, are buying fund-level lottery
tickets, and the price they are paying is the active management
puzzle.

\section{The post-2011 regime: Three competing readings}
\label{sec:post_2011}

\noindent The Bai--Perron structural break test identifies September
2011 as the largest regime-mean shift in the rolling Carhart
gross alpha series for active funds. The pre-break regime
(2006--Sep 2011) carries regime-mean alpha $+0.74\%$ per annum; the
post-break regime (Oct 2011--2026) carries regime-mean alpha
$-1.39\%$ per annum, a 210-basis-point shift. The Storey
$\hat{\pi}_0$ estimate falls from approximately 81\% in the
pre-break regime to 60.8\% in the post-break regime, with the
genuinely unskilled mass $T^-_{\gamma^*}$ rising from approximately 11\%
to 35.4\%. This regime shift is the strongest single piece of
regime-specific evidence in the dissertation.

Three competing readings of the regime shift are consistent with
the empirical evidence, each of which the present dissertation
neither confirms nor rules out.

Reading 1: Passive competition compressing active margins.
The 2010s witnessed an unprecedented secular shift of retail capital
from active to passive vehicles, with index mutual funds and
exchange-traded funds crossing the fifty-percent threshold of total
domestic equity fund assets by 2019. Under this reading, the
passive shift generates the post-2011 alpha contraction through a
disequilibrium persistence mechanism rather than through a direct
compression of gross alpha.

The trigger is the cheapening of the passive outside option. As
low-cost index vehicles reduced their expense ratios to single
digits per annum, the competitive hurdle that an active fund must
clear to retain rational investors rose mechanically. In a
frictionless \textcite{BerkGreen2004} world, this shock would
equilibrate rapidly through two adjustment margins: active funds
would cut fees to narrow the cost gap, and outflows would shrink
active AUM until the remaining managers, operating on a smaller
asset base, could generate sufficient gross alpha to clear the new
hurdle. Either margin, operating quickly, would prevent a
sustained alpha contraction.

Both adjustment margins are impaired in the post-2011 data. On the
fee side, \textcite{PastorStambaughTaylor2015} document that active
expense ratios did not fall proportionally to passive fee
compression, blocking the cost-side adjustment. On the flow side,
the search frictions and performance-flow asymmetry documented by
\textcite{SirriTufano1998} --- and amplified by the behavioral
channels established in Chapter~5 of the present dissertation ---
imply that outflows from underperforming active funds are slow and
incomplete. The fund-flow evidence in Figure~4.1 is consistent with
this: active mean proportional flow turns only gradually negative
after approximately 2018, more than half a decade after the
structural break in alpha generation.

The consequence of both valves being partially blocked is that
active industry AUM remains larger than the new post-shock
equilibrium warrants for an extended period. This is where the
\textcite{PastorStambaughTaylor2015} industry-level decreasing
returns to scale result enters causally: their central finding is
that gross fund performance declines as active industry AUM grows
relative to total market capitalisation, because more active
capital competing for a finite pool of mispriced securities erodes
the per-dollar alpha available to each manager. An active industry
that is oversized relative to its new equilibrium therefore
generates persistently depressed gross alpha --- not because the
opportunity set has shrunk, but because too many dollars remain
allocated to harvesting it. Reading~1 thus accounts for the
direction and persistence of the post-2011 alpha contraction
through the combination of a higher competitive hurdle, blocked
fee and flow adjustment, and the PST crowding mechanism operating
on the excess AUM. The independent compression of the gross alpha
opportunity set itself --- through the maturation of quantitative
and factor-replicating strategies --- is the province of
Reading~2.

Reading 2: Algorithmic trading eliminating cross-sectional
alpha opportunities. The 2010s also witnessed the maturation of
high-frequency and quantitative trading strategies that exploit
cross-sectional return predictability at high frequency. Under
this reading, the post-2011 alpha compression reflects the
extraction of the cross-sectional alpha-generating signals that
active managers had previously been able to harvest at lower
frequency, by professionally-trained quantitative competitors.
The reading is consistent with the
\textcite{HarveyLiuZhu2016} multiple-testing critique of the
factor-investing literature, in which apparent cross-sectional
predictability has been substantially reduced by the maturation
of quantitative competition.

Reading 3: Selection effects from fund entry/exit. The
post-2011 regime shift could in principle reflect changes in the
composition of the active fund universe rather than changes in
average managerial skill. Under this reading, the post-2011
regime contains a different sample of active funds than the
pre-2011 regime, and the shift in regime-mean alpha reflects the
selection of funds whose ex-ante characteristics differ from
those of the pre-break sample. The reading is partially testable on the present panel by re-estimating regime-mean alpha on a balanced sub-sample of funds present in both regimes; this robustness check has not been performed on the present panel and is reserved for future work. The selection-effects reading is therefore neither confirmed nor ruled out by the present evidence, and the 210-basis-point regime-mean shift reported here should be interpreted as an upper bound on the component attributable to genuine skill deterioration

The three readings are not mutually exclusive. The present
dissertation does not adjudicate between them; the empirical
contribution is the documentation of the regime shift itself, and
the behavioral interpretation that the shift makes the
cross-sectional puzzle of the post-2011 regime substantially
worse than the pre-2011 puzzle. Future research that combines
the present panel with fund-level holdings data and with
high-frequency factor decomposition would be well-placed to
adjudicate among the three readings.

\section{Contribution to the active management puzzle literature}
\label{sec:contribution_lit}

\noindent The dissertation's contribution to the active management
puzzle literature is fourfold. First, the empirical core extends
the \textcite{FamaFrench2010} bootstrap and
\textcite{BarrasScailletWermers2010} false-discovery decomposition
to a sample that materially extends the canonical 1984--2006
window. The qualitative invariance of the bootstrap pattern across
the two samples --- left-tail rejection of the zero-skill null
with right-tail consistency --- is itself informative: the
cross-sectional underperformance of the worst active funds is
detectable in the post-FF (2010) regime by the same methodology
that originally identified it.

Second, the dissertation documents a structural break in active
fund alpha generation in September 2011 that has not been
previously reported in the published literature with the present
methodological apparatus. The break is large in magnitude
(210 basis points), narrow in confidence interval (95\% Newey--West
HAC: March 2011--December 2011), and is robust to the choice of
breakpoint test (BIC-optimal Bai--Perron, with a mode that selects
the same break point under alternative break-count specifications).
The post-break regime is characterised by a collapse in the Storey
$\hat{\pi}_0$ estimate and a corresponding rise in the genuinely
unskilled mass, indicating that the regime change is not merely a
shift in the mean of the alpha distribution but a structural
re-shaping of the cross-sectional skill distribution itself.

Third, the dissertation introduces a unified four-hypothesis
behavioral framework (H1--H4) and tests it against the
\textcite{BerkGreen2004} rational null on a single, carefully
cleaned panel. The framework distinguishes between hypotheses
identifying the same flow--performance moments through different
behavioral mechanisms, and is designed to allow the data to speak
about which behavioral channels carry detectable signal and which
do not. The empirical outcome --- three of four hypotheses
generate detectable signatures consistent with behavioral
prediction, while one (H4 fee elasticity) is consistent with the
rational null --- supports a hybrid position in which the rational
framework accounts for capital-weighted outcomes and the
behavioral channels operate principally on the cross-section.

Fourth, the Psychological Premium Framework converts the H3
empirical evidence into welfare-relevant alpha basis points, and
documents that high-sentiment regimes amplify the implied
psychological premium by a factor of approximately 4.2. The
high-sentiment estimate of 59 basis points per standard deviation
of $\text{MAX12}$ exposure is on the order of the entire
risk-adjusted value-add gap between active and passive management
on the present sample, and provides the most economically tractable
expression of the active management puzzle that the dissertation
develops.

\section{Limitations}
\label{sec:limitations}

\noindent Five limitations of the present analysis warrant explicit
acknowledgement.

First, the static expense ratio used in the construction of the
net-of-fee return series does not vary at monthly frequency. The implied approximation error for any given fund is bounded by the magnitude of that fund's intra-period fee changes. In practice, mutual fund expense ratios are sticky and change infrequently; when they do change, the revision is typically small. The
direction of the bias is ambiguous and is unlikely to materially
affect the qualitative conclusions, but it is acknowledged here.

Second, the fund-level holdings data required to construct the
\textcite{CremersPetajisto2009} Active Share measure is not
available within the data sources accessed for this dissertation.
The two return-based activeness measures ($1-R^2$ and tracking
error) are reported jointly to mitigate single-measure concerns,
but the reader who places primary weight on the holdings-based
Active Share measure should treat the activeness chapter results
as the best available approximation rather than as a direct
analogue.

Third, benchmark assignments for approximately 37\% of funds
are inferred from fund attribute fields rather than drawn from
a confirmed source. These benchmarks are used exclusively in
the tracking-error robustness sort, which is a secondary
check on the headline activeness findings; the primary
$1-R^2$ activeness proxy requires no fund-specific benchmark
and is therefore unaffected.

Fourth, the panel is unbalanced and exhibits material attrition.
Funds that exit the sample during the observation window are
included up to the date of their last valid return observation;
funds that never enter the sample (private separate accounts,
hedge funds, non-mutual-fund vehicles) are absent by construction.
The coverage of the public mutual-fund universe is, on internal
counts, comparable to the
\textcite{FamaFrench2010,BarrasScailletWermers2010} U.S.\ samples,
but the implications for representativeness of the broader
delegated-asset-management industry remain a limitation.

Fifth, the behavioral state variables (Baker--Wurgler sentiment,
detrended margin debt, AAII bull--bear, inverse put--call) are
constructed at monthly frequency and the regression specifications
adopt contemporaneous timing following the literature standard.
The lagged-state-variable robustness check of
Appendix~\ref{app:behavioral_robustness} confirms the qualitative
findings under a one-month lag, but a more demanding identification
strategy --- for example, instrumental variables for sentiment
based on weather or seasonal anomalies in the manner of
\textcite{HirshleiferShumway2003} --- is left to future research.

\chapter{CONCLUSION}
\label{ch:conclusion}

\noindent This dissertation has examined the active management
puzzle on a panel of 3{,}764 U.S.\ domestic equity mutual fund
primary share classes (one per fund) spanning December 1994 through
February 2026. The empirical evidence supports a hybrid empirical position. The
\textcite{BerkGreen2004} rational equilibrium continues to provide
a clean reading of the capital-weighted distribution of mutual
fund alphas: the value-weighted net Carhart alpha is small in
economic magnitude and the H4 fee elasticity hypothesis is
consistent with the rational null. The cross-sectional
heterogeneity that produces the equal-weighted underperformance is
explained by behavioral frictions whose magnitude varies with
aggregate sentiment regimes: H1 sentiment-convexity, H2 margin-debt
amplification of loser-segment outflows, and H3 lottery demand
each generate detectable empirical signatures consistent with
behavioral prediction. The Psychological Premium Framework
quantifies the implied behavioral price as approximately 13 basis
points of annual Carhart alpha per standard deviation of
$\text{MAX12}$ exposure under low-sentiment regimes and 59 basis
points under high-sentiment regimes, a 4.5-fold amplification that
locates the puzzle's economic magnitude in the lottery-demand
channel.

\section{Summary of findings}
\label{sec:summary_findings}

\noindent The principal empirical findings are as follows.

First, the equal-weighted net Carhart alpha for active funds is
$-1.361\%$ per annum (significant at 1\%); the value-weighted
analogue is $-0.900\%$ (significant at 5\%). The 46-basis-point
gap between the two estimates locates the active management puzzle
in the cross-sectional heterogeneity rather than in the
representative dollar.

Second, the \textcite{FamaFrench2010} calendar-month bootstrap
rejects the zero-skill null in the left tail of the empirical
$t$-statistic distribution at all percentiles below the median,
with right-tail consistency above the median. The
\textcite{BarrasScailletWermers2010} false-discovery decomposition
implies a population zero-alpha mass of approximately 77.8\%, a
genuinely unskilled mass of 21.1\%, and no detectable genuinely
skilled mass at the standard $\gamma^* = 0.20$ threshold.

Third, a Bai--Perron structural break test identifies a regime
shift in September 2011 (95\% Newey--West HAC confidence interval:
March 2011--December 2011) after which active fund alpha generation
collapses from a regime-mean of $+0.74\%$ per annum to $-1.39\%$
per annum. The post-break regime exhibits a Storey
$\hat{\pi}_0$ collapse from 81\% to 60.8\% and a corresponding rise
in the genuinely unskilled mass to 35.4\%.

Fourth, of the four behavioral hypotheses tested, three (H1, H2 in
its margin-call rather than disposition reading, and H3) generate
detectable signatures consistent with behavioral prediction, and
one (H4 fee elasticity) is consistent with the rational null. The
empirical outcome supports a hybrid empirical position in which
the rational framework accounts for capital-weighted outcomes and
the behavioral channels operate principally on the cross-section.

Fifth, the Psychological Premium Framework converts the H3
findings into welfare-relevant alpha basis points: a high-sentiment
shadow price of 59 basis points per standard deviation of
$\text{MAX12}$ exposure, against a low-sentiment shadow price of
13 basis points. The high-sentiment amplification factor is
approximately 4.5$\times$ and is statistically significant at 5\%.

\section{Policy and practical implications}
\label{sec:implications}

\noindent The findings carry implications for three audiences.

For the academic literature, the dissertation strengthens the
empirical case for a hybrid reading of the active management
puzzle. The rational equilibrium of \textcite{BerkGreen2004}
is preserved at the capital-weighted level, and the cross-sectional
behavioral evidence is consistent with the existing channel
literature without requiring abandonment of the rational baseline.
The post-2011 regime-shift evidence further suggests that future
empirical work on the puzzle should adopt a regime-aware
specification that allows for structural breaks in the underlying
alpha distribution, rather than treating the post-FF (2010)
sample as a homogeneous extension of the canonical window.

For investors, the dissertation reinforces the long-standing
practical recommendation that active management is, on average, a
poor purchase net of fees. The cross-sectional implication is
sharper: the most behaviorally appealing active funds --- those
with the most extreme realised maximum returns, the most lottery-like return distributions --- are the ones that exhibit the most
severe persistent underperformance in subsequent holding periods.
Investors who select funds on the basis of recent extreme positive
returns are, on the present evidence, paying a behavioral premium
of approximately 59 basis points of annual Carhart alpha per
standard deviation of $\text{MAX12}$ exposure during high-sentiment regimes, and that premium is unlikely to be recovered.

For regulators, the dissertation suggests that fee disclosure
alone --- the focus of much existing regulatory practice --- is
unlikely to fully address the cross-sectional behavioral
distortions documented here. The H4 finding that fee elasticity is
consistent with the rational null indicates that investors are not
systematically fee-blind, and that the behavioral channels driving
the cross-sectional puzzle operate through the flow--performance
non-linearity rather than through fee processing per se.
Regulatory interventions targeting the lottery-demand channel ---
for example, standardised disclosure of trailing-window maximum
returns alongside time-weighted average returns, or regime-aware
suitability standards for retail investors during high-sentiment
periods --- may be more effective than additional fee disclosure.

\section{Future research}
\label{sec:future_research}

\noindent Four extensions of the present work would advance the
understanding of the active management puzzle, ordered by their
proximity to the current dataset.

First, the most immediate extension is a balanced-panel
decomposition of the post-2011 alpha collapse using the present
return-based panel. The selection-effects reading of the regime
shift (Reading~3 of Section~\ref{sec:post_2011}) holds that the
210-basis-point decline in regime-mean alpha across the September
2011 break reflects changes in the composition of the active fund
universe rather than deterioration in average managerial skill.
This reading is testable on the current panel by restricting the
sub-period analysis to the set of funds present in both P2
(January 2006--September 2011) and P3 (October 2011--February
2026), and re-estimating the regime-mean Carhart alpha and the
Barras et al.\ (2010) skill decomposition on this balanced
subsample. If the regime-mean shift on the balanced subsample is
materially smaller than 194 basis points, selection effects account
for a non-trivial share of the aggregate collapse and the
upper-bound interpretation stated in Section~\ref{sec:post_2011}
is binding. If the shift is preserved, Readings~1 and~2 bear the
full explanatory burden. A natural complement is to characterise
the demographic differences between the balanced subsample and the
full unbalanced panel --- fund age, size, expense ratio, and
activeness at the break date --- which would identify whether the
entry cohort post-2011 is systematically less skilled or merely
differently positioned in characteristic space. This extension
requires no new data and is the first priority for post-defense
work.

Second, the broader post-2011 regime-shift evidence motivates a
fund-level holdings-based decomposition of the alpha collapse. The
three competing readings of the regime shift identified in
Section~\ref{sec:post_2011} --- passive competition compressing
active margins, algorithmic trading eliminating cross-sectional
alpha opportunities, and selection effects from fund entry/exit
--- generate distinguishable predictions for the cross-sectional
distribution of stock-level holdings concentration, of
informational asymmetry, and of fund attrition rates. A panel
that combines the present return-based methodology with quarterly
fund holdings data would be well-placed to adjudicate among the
three readings.

Third, the Psychological Premium Framework developed in
Section~\ref{sec:ppf} converts the H3 evidence into welfare-relevant
units, but does not estimate the cross-sectional dispersion of
the implied psychological premium across investor clienteles. A
panel that distinguishes between retail and institutional
ownership at the fund level --- using SEC 13F filings or the
share-class structure imposed by load conventions --- would be
well-placed to estimate clientele-specific shadow prices and to
test whether the psychological premium is concentrated in the
retail clientele in the manner suggested by the
\textcite{Kumar2009} individual-investor lottery evidence.

Fourth, the structural-break evidence raises the question of
whether the post-2011 regime is structurally different in ways
that the present return-based methodology cannot fully identify.
A natural extension would combine the present panel with
high-frequency factor decompositions and machine-learning-based
fund return prediction tools in the manner of
\textcite{DeMiguelGilBazoNogalesUppal2023}. The
\textcite{FauschTangScheek2025} correction to the
\textcite{DeMiguelGilBazoNogalesUppal2023} methodology, which
identifies a forward-looking bias coding error that eliminates
the original positive-alpha result, is a useful methodological
caution: the corrected replication preserves the loser-persistence
finding documented here, providing convergent evidence across
two methodological frameworks.

The active management puzzle, in summary, is not a single puzzle
but a cluster of related empirical regularities whose qualitative
shape has remained remarkably stable across three decades of
academic scrutiny. The contribution of this dissertation is to
extend the methodological apparatus to a sample that materially
extends the canonical window, to document a structural break that
intensifies the cross-sectional heterogeneity, and to identify
the behavioral channels that can rationalise that heterogeneity
without requiring abandonment of the
\textcite{BerkGreen2004} rational baseline. The hybrid
empirical position the dissertation defends is, in the author's
view, the most parsimonious reading of the joint evidence: the
representative dollar is at the rational equilibrium; the
cross-section is not.

\chapter{DATA CONSTRUCTION AND CLEANING}
\label{app:data_cleaning}

\noindent This appendix documents the full six-step cleaning pipeline that
converts the raw extract from the Department's Bloomberg Terminal and
LSEG Workspace subscriptions into the three analytical panels used in
the main text. The pipeline is implemented in R via the
data\_import\_and\_cleaning.R script and is reproducible from
the raw data sources.

\section{Raw extract structure}
\label{sec:raw_extract}

\noindent The raw fund-level data are stored as separate wide-format
sheets, with fund tickers on rows and calendar months on columns, in
the master Excel workbook. Static fund attributes (Ticker, ISIN, fund
name, inception date, market status, primary benchmark, market-cap
focus, fund strategy, active management flag, expense ratio) are
stored on a separate static sheet. Each time-series sheet is
imported and pivoted to long format --- one observation per fund-month
--- and the resulting long tables are joined on the composite key
(Ticker, date) using a full outer join. The static fund attributes are
merged in by ticker. Monthly macroeconomic and factor variables are
merged by date. The result is an unbalanced long-format panel in which
each row corresponds to a unique fund-month observation. Date column
headers in the raw Excel files are stored as Excel serial numbers and
converted using the standard Excel origin of December 30, 1899. LSEG
error strings (\#N/A N/A, \#N/A Field Not Applicable)
are coerced to missing values prior to numeric conversion.

\section{Step 1: Removal of post-closure forward-filled observations}
\label{sec:frozen_tail}

\noindent The LSEG Workspace data terminal does not truncate the return
series of liquidated or inactive funds at the point of closure. Instead,
the terminal carries the last valid total-return index value forward
indefinitely, generating spurious zero-return months in the panel that
would, if uncorrected, bias measured performance downward and
contaminate fund-flow regressions. The Close Date field in
LSEG static data is unreliable for filtering: all active funds receive
the data-pull date as their close date, liquidated funds receive
either the pull date or an error string, and inactive funds receive
only error strings.

A data-driven algorithm operates on the gross total-return index series
as the master signal. (1) Observations with a missing gross index are
removed. (2) A binary indicator is\_frozen is constructed,
equal to one if the current month's index value equals the prior
month's value. (3) The last date on which the index moved is identified
for each fund. (4) The length of the terminal frozen block --- the
count of is\_frozen observations strictly after the last
movement date --- is computed. (5) The terminal frozen block is removed
only if its length is at least two, which corresponds to at
least three consecutive identical index values at the tail; once this
threshold is met, deletion begins at the first repeated value.

The minimum run-length threshold of two protects against false
positives. A fund that happens to return exactly zero in its final
active month would produce a single terminal frozen observation,
which falls below the threshold and is therefore retained. Funds
subject to genuine LSEG carry --- which is indefinite and therefore
always produces a terminal run well exceeding the threshold --- are
correctly identified and trimmed. The known limitation of the
algorithm is that funds ceasing activity within two months of the
data-pull endpoint (February 2026) may retain one to two carried
observations, as the LSEG carry sequence is truncated by the data
pull before reaching the three-repeat threshold; this affects at most
two terminal observations per affected fund and cannot materially
influence panel-level regression estimates. As a byproduct, an
effective closure-date table is produced for each fund. All companion
series (net return index, tracking difference, net assets, share-class
assets, total assets, shares outstanding, fund flow) are synchronised
to the cleaned return timeline via a semi-join on (Ticker, date).

\section{Step 2: Exclusion of funds with no valid return observations}
\label{sec:empty_funds}

\noindent Following frozen-tail removal, 29 funds are eliminated for
having no remaining valid gross return observations. The working
sample falls to 3{,}735 share classes after this step.

\section{Step 3: Exclusion of confirmed data errors}
\label{sec:data_errors}

\noindent Two funds are excluded for confirmed data errors in the LSEG
return-index series. QWVOX US Equity: the gross total return
index for this fund reaches 113{,}446 by February 2026 from a base
value of 1 in December 1994, implying a cumulative gross return
exceeding 11{,}300{,}000\,\% --- impossible for any U.S.\ domestic
equity mutual fund. The series exhibits excess kurtosis of 91.6,
confirming the anomalous nature of the data. VALLCEN US
Equity: this fund records a single-month gross return of $-75.9\%$ in
April 2022, during which the S\&P 500 fell approximately 8.7\%. The
fund held approximately \$152 million in assets and had no documented
leverage or derivative overlay that could produce a return of this
magnitude; all other monthly returns for the fund are within plausible
ranges. Both funds are recorded in the exclusion registry under the
DATA\_ERROR flag and are excluded prior to panel assembly.
The working sample falls to 3{,}733 share classes after this step.

\section{Step 4: Evans (2010) incubation correction}
\label{sec:evans_correction}

\noindent Mutual fund incubation is the practice whereby fund families
privately seed multiple new funds and, after an evaluation period,
selectively open to the public only those that have performed well.
\textcite{Evans2010} documents that incubated funds outperform
non-incubated funds by approximately 3.5\% per annum on a risk-adjusted
basis during the incubation period, with the outperformance fully
reversing post-incubation. Inclusion of incubation-period returns in
the working panel imparts a systematic upward bias to measured average
fund performance. The TNA filter of \$25 million proposed in early
work removes only 47\% of incubated funds while excluding 24\% of
non-incubated funds, introducing its own bias.

\textcite{Evans2010} validates an alternative correction: removing the
first 36 months of return data for every fund. He shows that fewer
than 5\% of incubated funds remain in incubation longer than 36
months, so the threshold eliminates virtually the entire bias. For
each fund in the present panel, an Evans cutoff date is computed as
the inception date plus 36 calendar months. Where the inception date
is missing or contains an error string, the first observed return
date is used as a fallback. The Evans correction is applied to the
panel\_incubation and panel\_trimmed variants but
not to the panel\_master variant, which serves as the
audit baseline.

\section{Step 5: Winsorisation of monthly returns}
\label{sec:winsorisation}

\noindent Monthly percentage returns, computed as
$r_{i,t} = \text{Index}_{i,t} / \text{Index}_{i,t-1} - 1$ from the
gross and net total-return index levels, are winsorised at the
1st/99th percentiles across all fund-month observations. Winsorisation
is standard practice in the mutual fund literature to address extreme
values arising from leveraged fund blow-ups, illiquid pricing
anomalies, and residual data errors that survive earlier cleaning
steps. The 1st/99th percentile threshold is the modal choice in the
literature and is applied symmetrically to both tails.

The unwinsorised raw returns ($r^{\text{gross,raw}}$,
$r^{\text{net,raw}}$) are retained on the panel alongside the
winsorised series. This is necessary because the
\textcite{SirriTufano1998} flow identity --- $\text{Flow}_t =
\text{TNA}_t - \text{TNA}_{t-1} (1 + R_t)$ --- is an accounting
identity, not a statistical model. Substituting a winsorised return
into this identity would produce fictitious asset growth and
systematically biased flow estimates in months when genuine extreme
returns occur. Winsorisation is therefore applied only in the
performance-measurement and regression contexts, not in the construction
of fund-flow variables. Fund-flow variables are winsorised separately
at the same percentile threshold within the flow calculation script,
following \textcite{SirriTufano1998}. December observations are
excluded from winsorisation and from all flow analyses due to the
year-end capital-gains-distribution artifact in vendor-reported flow
data.

\section{Step 6: Exclusion of leveraged and derivative-based passive products}
\label{sec:leveraged_exclusion}

\noindent A diagnostic query identified 30 passive funds with annual
turnover exceeding 200\%, with values ranging from 205\% to 3{,}996\%.
Manual review confirmed all 30 to be leveraged or derivative-based
mutual funds: Rydex 2$\times$ Strategy funds, ProFunds UltraSector
products, and Direxion Bull products. These funds carry the LSEG
Actively\_Managed = N flag because they mechanically track an
index, but their economic character is categorically different from
conventional long-only index funds. The products deliver a constant
daily leverage multiple --- typically 1.25$\times$, 1.5$\times$, or
2$\times$ the underlying index return --- by holding equity swaps or
futures contracts and resetting the exposure every business day.
Daily resetting generates annual portfolio turnover of 200\% to
4{,}000\% (versus 5\% to 30\% for conventional index funds) and, more
importantly, introduces path dependency: for a $L$$\times$ fund, the
expected return over any horizon longer than one day is $L$ times the
index return minus a variance drag equal to the index variance scaled
by the leverage factor \parencite{AvellanedaZhang2010}. This
``volatility decay'' causes leveraged funds to persistently
underperform their stated multiple over multi-year horizons, even in
rising markets. Inclusion in the passive fee-quintile analysis
contaminated the high-fee passive quintile with anomalously low
returns and extreme turnover values, producing results that are
uninterpretable as a fee--performance comparison.

Three exclusion criteria are applied to the passive group only.
Keyword filter on fund name: funds whose name contains any of
2x, 3x, 1.5x, ultra, inverse,
bear market, bull 1 (capturing Direxion ``Bull 125'',
``Bull 150'', etc.), and profund (all ProFunds share classes
in this universe are leveraged) are excluded. Matching is
case-insensitive. Hard-coded misclassifications: two funds
are excluded as active strategies misclassified as passive by their
data vendor: MOJAX US Equity (DF Tactical Momentum Fund, a
tactical active fund) and GENDX US Equity (Gotham Enhanced
Index, a long/short quantitative strategy). Explicit retention
of Rydex Pure Style funds: five Rydex Pure Style funds (RYAVX,
RYZAX, RYAZX, RYWAX, RYAWX) match
the profund keyword pattern but are explicitly retained.
These are long-only, 1$\times$ products tracking S\&P Pure Style
indices, whose elevated reported turnover reflects the
reconstitution-heavy methodology of the underlying index rather than
derivative rolling.

\section{Output panels}
\label{sec:output_panels}

\noindent The pipeline produces three analytically distinct panels.
panel\_master is the full cleaned panel after frozen-tail
removal, empty-fund exclusion, data-error exclusion, active/passive
classification, and leveraged-fund exclusion --- no incubation
correction and no date restriction. Both raw and unwinsorised columns
are present but identical at this stage, since winsorisation is
applied at panel construction rather than at cleaning. This panel
serves as the audit baseline. panel\_incubation additionally
applies the Evans 36-month age filter and the return winsorisation,
with no date restriction; this is the primary main-text panel.
panel\_trimmed additionally restricts the sample to January
1995--December 2023 (December 1994 retained for lag computation); it
underlies the H1--H4 panel regressions where the date cap aligns with
the available factor and sentiment series.

\chapter{LIPPER CATEGORY ASSIGNMENT}
\label{app:lipper_assignment}

\noindent This appendix documents the priority-ordered cascade by
which Lipper investment-objective codes are assigned to the 3{,}764
share classes in the working panel. The cascade is implemented in
the \texttt{build\_lipper\_category.R} script and produces the
\texttt{lipper\_category\_output.xlsx} workbook from which the
within-Lipper-category fractional ranks of
Section~\ref{sec:panel_spec} are constructed.

\section{Source fields}
\label{sec:lipper_sources}

\noindent Three fields from the merged static-data file
(lipper.xlsx) are used in the assignment procedure.
LSEG Category --- the authoritative Lipper classification
sourced directly from the Department's LSEG Workspace export. Used
as-is where populated. New\_Benchmark --- the assigned
benchmark code from the benchmark-assignment exercise documented in
Appendix~\ref{app:benchmark}. Codes such as RLV, RUT, and RMCG
uniquely identify the size-style combination of the benchmark and
therefore the appropriate Lipper peer group. Mcap\_Focus and
Fund\_Strategy --- Bloomberg static fields capturing market-cap
focus (Large-cap, Mid-cap, Small-cap, Broad Market) and investment
style (Value, Blend, Growth). Used as a fallback when neither the
LSEG Category nor a specific-style benchmark code is available.

\section{The four-priority cascade}
\label{sec:lipper_cascade}

\noindent The following four priorities are applied in strict order;
once a fund is assigned at a given priority, lower priorities are
not evaluated.

Priority 1 --- LSEG Category (Direct). Where the LSEG
Category field is populated and contains a valid Lipper category
string, the value is used directly. This is the authoritative source
and covers 1{,}712 funds (45.5\% of the panel). Source tag:
LSEG\_Direct.

Priority 2 --- Benchmark Code Map. For the remaining 2{,}052
funds, the assigned benchmark code is checked against a deterministic
mapping table. Style-specific Russell and S\&P benchmark codes
unambiguously identify the size and style characteristics of the
benchmark mandate, and therefore the appropriate Lipper peer group.
This mapping covers 1{,}541 additional funds (40.9\%). The Russell
3000 broad index code (RUA) is intentionally excluded from the map:
RUA is the default catch-all benchmark assigned to funds where no
more specific information was available (confidence score 1 in the
benchmark assignment exercise), and carries no style information.
Funds assigned RUA fall through to Priority 3 or 4. Source tag:
Benchmark\_Map. The full mapping table is reported in
Appendix~\ref{app:benchmark}.

Priority 3 --- Bloomberg field derivation. For funds not
resolved by Priorities 1 or 2, the Bloomberg Mcap\_Focus and
Fund\_Strategy fields are combined to construct a Lipper
category label. Three sub-cases are applied in order. 3a:
both fields present (182 funds, source tag
Derived\_McapStrat). The Lipper category is constructed as
[Size~Prefix]~[Style~Suffix]~Funds, where the size prefix is derived
from Mcap\_Focus and the style suffix from
Fund\_Strategy. 3b: Mcap\_Focus present,
Fund\_Strategy missing or non-standard (49 funds, source tag
Derived\_McapOnly). The fund is assigned to the Core
category for the identified size tier; the rationale is that a
missing strategy field provides no style evidence, and Core (Blend)
is the modal category and the least biased default. 3c:
Fund\_Strategy present, Mcap\_Focus missing or
equal to Broad Market (175 funds, source tag
Derived\_StratOnly). The fund is assigned to the Multi-Cap
category for the identified style; Broad Market and missing Mcap
fields indicate no specific size constraint, and Multi-Cap is the
appropriate Lipper designation for unconstrained mandates.

Priority 4 --- Unresolvable (NA). Funds for which none of
the above priorities yields a valid assignment are coded as NA. These
105 funds (2.8\%) consist entirely of sector funds, long/short equity,
market-neutral, bear-market, and Unknown-AM funds, all of which are
excluded from the H1/H2/H4 regression sample by independent criteria.
The NA assignment for these funds entails no loss of analytical
sample. Source tag: Unresolvable.

\section{Classification outcome and circularity acknowledgement}
\label{sec:lipper_outcome}

\noindent The cascade classifies 3{,}659 of 3{,}764 funds (97.2\%).
The 105 unresolvable funds are all outside the analysis sample by
other criteria. The most consequential methodological feature of the
cascade is the Priority-2 derivation, in which 1{,}541 funds (40.9\%)
receive their Lipper category from their assigned benchmark code.
Because the within-Lipper-category fractional rank used in the
Sirri--Tufano regressions of Chapter~\ref{ch:behavioral} is computed
within these peer groups, a question of circularity arises: are the
peer groups partly defined by the same benchmark assignment that
indirectly determines the activeness measure?

The answer is that Priority-2 assignments are not formally circular
because the benchmark assignment was performed independently of the
Lipper assignment, using Bloomberg's primary-benchmark field, the
LSEG benchmark field, and prospectus research as inputs (see
Appendix~\ref{app:benchmark}). Of the 1{,}541 Priority-2 funds, the
overwhelming majority have a benchmark assignment confirmed at
confidence $\geq 7$ (Bloomberg primary benchmark direct map, LSEG
Lipper-category map, or prospectus confirmation). The Lipper
category these funds receive at Priority 2 is therefore grounded in
independently verified evidence rather than derived circularly from
the same data inputs that drive the activeness measure.

The robustness check supporting the cascade is documented in the
Lipper\_Source column of lipper\_category\_output.xlsx,
which records the priority tag for each fund and permits restriction
of any downstream analysis to the LSEG-Direct-only sub-sample
($n = 1{,}712$) or the LSEG-Direct$+$Benchmark-Map sub-sample
($n = 3{,}253$, all classified via objective evidence). The main
results in the dissertation are reported using all classified funds;
the LSEG-Direct-only sub-sample is available for interested readers
as a methodological cross-check.

\section{Additional notes}
\label{sec:lipper_notes}

\noindent Three further notes complete the documentation of the
cascade. First, the classification is applied at the fund level: because the
panel contains exactly one primary share class per fund (the
Bloomberg Lipper-designated primary flag), each fund carries a single
Lipper category entry in \texttt{lipper\_category\_output.xlsx} and
no within-fund consolidation is required. Second, Lipper
category labels that appear in the LSEG Category field but do not
correspond to the twelve standard size-style categories (for example,
Equity Income Funds, Real Estate Funds, Science \& Technology Funds)
are retained as-is. Sector-mandated and covered-call-overlay funds
in this residual group are excluded from H3 by the
\flag{SECTOR\_FUND} and \flag{COVERED\_CALL\_OVERLAY} flags
respectively in the sample-membership matrix
documented in Appendix~\ref{app:benchmark}; the remaining
non-standard-category funds (Equity Income, Specialty Diversified,
Specialty/Miscellaneous) are retained in H3 with their LSEG-supplied
label forming their peer group, and appear in H1, H2, and H4
analyses on the same basis. Third,
the script merges the Lipper\_Category column from the
output workbook into the static sheet of the master fund-data
workbook, making the classification available to all downstream R
scripts via the panel object.

\chapter{BENCHMARK ASSIGNMENT METHODOLOGY}
\label{app:benchmark}

\noindent This appendix documents the benchmark-assignment process
that produced the \texttt{Benchmark\_Assignment\_updated.xlsx}
workbook, the source data for the tracking-error robustness measure
of Section~\ref{sec:activeness}. Each fund record carries
a primary (sector- or strategy-specific) benchmark, a broad-based
comparator benchmark, the corresponding Refinitiv RIC codes for the
price-return and total-return variants, a confidence score, and a
flag indicating its membership in the various hypothesis sub-samples.

\section{Three-tier assessment process}
\label{sec:benchmark_tiers}

\noindent Each fund was assessed in three stages. Tier 1 ---
Bloomberg benchmark screening: Bloomberg's Primary\_Benchmark
field was reviewed for transparent misassignments. Sector funds
benchmarked to S\&P 500, small/mid-cap funds benchmarked to S\&P 500,
and funds with no benchmark were flagged for further research.
Tier 2 --- Active research: for flagged funds, primary-source
research established the correct declared benchmark. The principal
sources were SEC EDGAR (prospectuses, annual reports), fund
factsheets and family websites, Morningstar / U.S.\ News /
StockAnalysis (for cross-reference), and direct fund-family
documentation where the fund's prospectus did not explicitly state a
benchmark (most prominently, Guggenheim's confirmation that Rydex
sector funds declare no official primary benchmark).
Tier 3 --- Inference for undisclosed benchmarks: for funds
with no declared benchmark (primarily Rydex sector funds), a proxy
sector index was inferred from the fund's GICS sector classification,
cap focus, and strategy, and flagged as INFERRED.

The benchmark assignment workbook covers all 3{,}764 funds in the
full panel, including passive index trackers. Of the 2{,}329 active
funds, 97 required a manual correction to a Bloomberg benchmark
misassignment or a missing benchmark; the remaining 2{,}232 required
no active-research override. The final assigned benchmark is
nevertheless not Bloomberg-sourced for the majority of funds: for
1{,}697 funds it derives from the LSEG Lipper-category to
Russell/MSCI map (score 8), and Bloomberg's
\texttt{Primary\_Benchmark} field is the direct source only for the
660 funds at score 7.

\section{Special treatment: Sector and REIT funds}
\label{sec:special_treatment}

\noindent The largest single group of corrections concerns the
Fidelity Select sector fund family. Bloomberg had assigned the
S\&P 500 as the primary benchmark for the entire Fidelity Select
family. In practice, each Fidelity Select fund is benchmarked
against the relevant MSCI U.S.\ Investable Market Index (IMI) 25/50
sub-index, confirmed verbatim in each fund's annual report. The
S\&P 500 is retained as the broad-based comparator benchmark. The
correction covers 37 funds.

The MSCI U.S.\ IMI 25/50 sub-indices are the appropriate primary
benchmarks for Fidelity Select funds because the IMI coverage
universe encompasses large, mid, and small cap constituents within
each GICS sector, reflecting the full-market-cap mandate of the
Select funds. A large- and mid-cap-only sector index would
systematically exclude small-cap holdings and produce mechanically
elevated tracking error reflecting mandate breadth rather than
genuine active deviation. The 25/50 concentration constraint --- a
single-issuer cap of 25\% and an aggregated cap of 50\% on issuers
above 5\% --- ensures compatibility with the diversification
requirements imposed on regulated investment companies under the
U.S.\ Internal Revenue Code.

Five confirmed benchmark-change events are documented for Fidelity
Select funds. FSTCX/FWRLX (September--November
2018): GICS reclassification of Telecom Services into Communication
Services; benchmark changed from S\&P 500 Telecom Services Index to
MSCI U.S.\ IMI Communication Services 25/50. FIUIX
(September 2018): similar GICS reclassification; benchmark changed
to MSCI U.S.\ IMI Utilities 25/50. FBIOX/FBTAX
(January 2010): benchmark changed from MSCI U.S.\ IM Capital Goods
to MSCI U.S.\ IMI Biotechnology 25/50. FRESX/FRIFX
(December 2020): benchmark changed from Dow Jones U.S.\ Select Real
Estate Securities Index to MSCI U.S.\ IMI Real Estate 25/50.
FSHOX (March 2023): post-GICS reclassification of
homebuilders from Real Estate / Materials to Consumer Discretionary;
benchmark updated to MSCI U.S.\ IMI Consumer Discretionary 25/50.

A second correction group concerns Rydex sector funds, for which
Guggenheim's documentation confirms the absence of a declared
primary benchmark. For these 14 funds a proxy sector index is
inferred from the fund's GICS sector classification and labelled
INFERRED PROXY with confidence score 3. Smaller correction
groups cover Cohen \& Steers Realty funds (3 funds, MSCI U.S.\ REIT
confirmed), MFS Utilities and BULIX (S\&P 500 Utilities Index), and
approximately 20 additional sector funds.

\section{Confidence scoring framework}
\label{sec:confidence_scoring}

\noindent Each benchmark assignment carries a numeric confidence
score from 1 to 10 reflecting the reliability of the source used
to determine the final assigned benchmark. The distribution across
all 3{,}764 funds in the full panel (active and passive) is
concentrated in the high-confidence tiers: 17 funds at score 10
(benchmark confirmed verbatim in fund annual report or prospectus),
1{,}697 at score 8 (benchmark derived from the LSEG Lipper-category
to standard Russell/MSCI map), 660 at score 7 (Bloomberg
\texttt{Primary\_Benchmark} field maps directly to a recognised
index family), 902 at score 5 (benchmark inferred from both
Bloomberg \texttt{Mcap\_Focus} and \texttt{Fund\_Strategy} fields),
387 at score 3 (partial inference from one Bloomberg attribute
field), and 101 at score 1 (default Russell 3000 placeholder).
Approximately 37\% of assignments carry confidence $\leq 5$
(inferred). Sector-mandated funds are excluded from the H3
activeness analysis under the \flag{SECTOR\_FUND} flag documented
in Section~\ref{sec:sample_membership}. The primary activeness
measure ($1-R^2$ from the Carhart factor regression) uses no
fund-specific benchmark, so benchmark inference quality is not
load-bearing on the headline activeness findings.

\section{Data-availability benchmark substitutions}
\label{sec:substitutions}

\noindent Three benchmark substitutions were required due to LSEG
subscription tier constraints. Each is documented with its rationale
and the magnitude of its effect.

MSCI U.S.\ REIT Index $\rightarrow$ FTSE NAREIT All-Equity
REITs Index. The confirmed benchmark for Cohen \& Steers REIT funds
is the MSCI U.S.\ REIT Index (RIC: RMZTR). The total-return series
for this index is inaccessible within the LSEG subscription tier
used in this dissertation. The FTSE NAREIT All-Equity REITs Total
Return Index (RIC: .FTFNERUSDT) is adopted as a substitute.
The substitution is methodologically defensible on three grounds.
First, the FTSE NAREIT All-Equity REITs Index is the industry-standard
benchmark for U.S.\ REIT funds and is more widely cited in the
academic literature on real-estate investment trust performance than
the MSCI U.S.\ REIT Index. Second, both indices cover the universe of
U.S.-listed equity REITs using comparable construction methodologies,
yielding monthly total-return correlation in excess of 0.97 over the
available comparison window. Third, the All-Equity REITs sub-index
is specifically selected --- rather than the broader All REITs
variant, which includes mortgage REITs --- to preserve alignment
with the Cohen \& Steers mandate, which focuses exclusively on
equity REITs.

S\&P MidCap 400 $\rightarrow$ Russell Midcap Index.
Thirty-one funds in the benchmark dataset are assigned the S\&P
MidCap 400 as their primary benchmark, of which 17 carry the
benchmark-required flag. The S\&P MidCap 400 total-return series was
unavailable within the LSEG subscription tier for the full sample
period. The Russell Midcap Index total-return series (RIC:
.RUMTU) is adopted as a substitute. Both indices cover the
U.S.\ mid-capitalisation equity segment and their monthly total
returns exhibit correlation in excess of 0.97 over the full sample.
The methodological difference between the two indices is constituent
selection (S\&P committee-driven; Russell rules-based market cap
ranking); for the purposes of computing rolling Carhart $R^2$ and
tracking error this difference is immaterial.

Russell 2500 Value $\rightarrow$ Russell 2000 Value. Ten
funds carry Russell 2500 Value as their primary benchmark. The LSEG
total-return series for this index has insufficient sample coverage
within the available subscription tier. The Russell 2000 Value Index
total-return series (RIC: .RUJTRI) is adopted as a
substitute. The Russell 2500 is defined as the bottom 2{,}500
constituents of the Russell 3000 by market cap, encompassing the
entire Russell 2000 plus the smallest 500 stocks of the Russell
Midcap; the Russell 2500 Value and Russell 2000 Value share
approximately 80\% constituent overlap by count. The Russell 2000
Value preserves the value style tilt of the original benchmark,
which is the methodologically critical dimension; the substitution
affects only 10 funds and is immaterial to the aggregate results.

\section{Sample-membership flag matrix}
\label{sec:sample_membership}

\noindent The \texttt{flagged\_funds.xlsx} workbook organises sample
membership across three exclusion scopes that segregate funds by
the analytical context in which their inclusion is methodologically
defensible.

The \emph{Exclude from Entire Analysis} scope (125 fund records
across the full panel) covers funds whose strategy or mandate places 
them outside the domestic-equity active-management scope of the dissertation:
non-U.S.\ benchmarks (36 \flag{GLOBAL\_MANDATE} plus 2 combined
\flag{EM\_MANDATE}\slashbreak\flag{GLOBAL\_MANDATE} and 1 combined
\flag{GLOBAL\_MANDATE}\slashbreak\flag{LONG\_SHORT}), long-short
strategies (31 \flag{LONG\_SHORT} pure plus the combinations noted
above), market-neutral strategies (26 \flag{MARKET\_NEUTRAL}),
bear-market or inverse strategies (27 \flag{BEAR\_MARKET}), and
two confirmed LSEG data-error funds (\flag{DATA\_ERROR}).
Passive index trackers are retained in the panel: their inclusion
is necessary to construct the active-versus-passive performance
comparison that motivates the puzzle.

The \emph{Exclude from H3 Only} scope (155 fund records across
the full panel) covers
sector-mandated funds (149 \flag{SECTOR\_FUND}) and covered-call

variance, so the resulting high $1-R^2$ values reflect
mandate-driven factor orthogonality rather than active manager
discretion \parencite{KacperczykSialmZheng2008}. Covered-call

character to the lottery profile that H3 is designed to test.

funds are retained in H3: the
\textcite{AmihudGoyenko2013} $1-R^2$ proxy is computed from a
Carhart regression with no fund-specific benchmark, so the
benchmark-misassignment concern that motivates sector-fund
exclusion does not apply to these categories. Both sector-mandated
and covered-call funds retain validity for the H1, H2, and H4
panel regressions and therefore appear in those analytical samples.

This scope defines the subsample used for the portfolio-level
alpha estimates of Section~\ref{sec:aggregate_performance} and the

\chapter{ACTIVENESS AND LOTTERY PROXY VALIDATION}
\label{app:proxy_validation}

\noindent This appendix documents the construction and validation of
the four candidate activeness/lottery proxies considered for the H3
panel regressions of Chapter~\ref{ch:behavioral}: $\text{ActR2}$,
$\text{ActSkew}$, $\text{MAX12}$, and a right-tail conditional
skewness measure ($\text{UpSkew}$). The proxy validation
procedure selects three proxies for the H3 horse race and excludes
the fourth.

\section{Construction}
\label{sec:proxy_construction}

\noindent $\text{ActR2}_i$ is the time-series mean of $1 - R^2_{i,t}$
from the rolling 36-month Carhart regression that produces the rolling
alpha series of Section~\ref{sec:rolling_alphas}. Following
\textcite{AmihudGoyenko2013}, a higher value indicates that a larger
share of fund return variation is unexplained by the systematic four-factor benchmark and is therefore attributable to the fund's
idiosyncratic active deviation from a passive factor-mimicking
portfolio.

$\text{ActSkew}_i$ is the cross-sectional sample skewness of the
fund's trailing 36 monthly gross returns. The measure captures the
full-distribution lottery character: a positively skewed return
series reflects right-tail mass that lottery-preferring investors
should value, conditional on mean and variance.

$\text{MAX12}_i$ is the maximum monthly gross return over the
trailing 12 months, following the stock-level construction of
\textcite{BaliCakiciWhitelaw2011} and the fund-level extension of
\textcite{ChengEtal2025}. The measure captures the right-tail-only
lottery character: a high MAX value reflects a single extreme
positive return that lottery-preferring investors should value
disproportionately under cumulative prospect theory probability
weighting.

$\text{UpSkew}_i$ is the sample skewness of the fund's trailing 36
monthly gross returns restricted to observations above the in-window
mean, scaled by the upside semi-deviation. The measure tests whether
the asymmetry signal in $\text{ActSkew}$ is driven specifically by
the right tail — which cumulative prospect theory predicts investors
should overweight — rather than by the full third moment.

Each proxy is standardised within sample and lagged one month for the
H3 regressions to avoid contemporaneous endogeneity.

\section{Diagnostic statistics and proxy selection}
\label{sec:proxy_diagnostics}

\input{tables/table_activeness_proxy_diagnostic}

\noindent Table~\ref{tab:activeness_proxy} reports the diagnostic
statistics for each candidate proxy. Coverage (column 2) is the
fraction of fund-months in \texttt{panel\_reg} with the measure
non-missing; it ranges from 81.6\% to 93.3\%, confirming comparable
sample availability across candidates. Column 3 reports each measure's cross-sectional
correlation with $\text{ActR2}$. Columns 4 and 5 report univariate flow-prediction
$t$-statistics under two fixed-effect structures (Lipper $\times$
year-month and Ticker $+$ year-month). Column 6 reports the

future Carhart alpha, providing a sanity check on the rational-channel
interpretation of $\text{ActR2}$.

The diagnostic test that distinguishes among the four candidates is
the cross-sectional correlation with $\text{ActR2}$ (column 3). A
high correlation indicates that the candidate proxy is essentially a
re-statement of the rational-channel activeness signal of
\textcite{AmihudGoyenko2013} rather than a distinct behavioral
signal.  
$\text{UpSkew}$ is dropped on the basis of horse-race

\section{Limitations of the replication}
\label{sec:ff_limitations}

\noindent Differences in point estimates between this appendix and
Tables I--III of \textcite{FamaFrench2010} reflect three
methodological choices that diverge from the FF (2010) protocol and
should be borne in mind when comparing magnitudes. First, the
present sample uses LSEG total-return data rather than the CRSP
mutual fund database used by FF (2010); the two sources differ in
their treatment of share-class consolidation, fee-waiver
adjustments, and back-fill correction, and small differences in
fund-level returns can compound into 10--30 basis point differences
in aggregate alpha estimates over a decade-long window. Second,
incubation correction in this dissertation uses the
\textcite{Evans2010} 36-month age proxy, whereas FF (2010) use the
NASD ticker creation date; the two filters select different sets of
incubation-eligible funds. Third, net returns in the present sample
are computed by deducting one-twelfth of the static annual expense
ratio from each fund's monthly gross return (following Carhart 1997
and Wermers 2000), whereas CRSP-based studies typically use the
fund-reported net return series directly. The qualitative
invariance of the bootstrap pattern across the two samples ---
left-tail rejection of the zero-skill null with right-tail
consistency --- is the central robustness finding of this appendix
and is independent of these point-estimate differences.

\chapter{FACTOR MODEL ROBUSTNESS}
\label{app:robustness}

\noindent This appendix re-estimates the core performance results
under two alternative factor-model specifications: the
\textcite{FamaFrench2015} five-factor model augmented with momentum
(FF6: $\text{MKT} - \text{Rf}$, $\text{SMB}$, $\text{HML}$,
$\text{RMW}$, $\text{CMA}$, $\text{MOM}$) and the Carhart
four-factor model augmented with the
\textcite{PastorStambaugh2003} traded liquidity factor (Carhart$+$PSL:
$\text{MKT} - \text{Rf}$, $\text{SMB}$, $\text{HML}$,
$\text{MOM}$, $\text{LIQ}_V$). The robustness exercise is
confined to the full-period pooled alpha, bootstrap, and BSW
decomposition components; the rolling-alpha series, flow--performance
regressions, and sub-period analyses are not re-estimated under
alternative factor models. The defensive scope reflects the focus of
the robustness exercise: whether the puzzle survives factor-model
choice, not whether every downstream result is factor-invariant.

The motivation for the robustness exercise is the legitimate question
raised by the committee during preparation meetings: whether the
main-text Carhart four-factor alpha estimates are robust to factor
model choice, specifically referencing the
\textcite{FamaFrench2015} five-factor model and the
\textcite{PastorStambaugh2003} traded liquidity factor. A
defensive response is warranted: if pooled active-fund alpha proves
materially different under FF6 or Carhart$+$PSL, the main empirical
argument of the dissertation would require qualification.

The Fama--French SMB construction is held constant across all three
specifications (FF3/Carhart construction, single $2 \times 3$ size-BM
sort). The FF5 release provides a different SMB (averaged across
three $2 \times 3$ sorts), but the two series correlate above 0.97
over December 1994 through December 2024. Holding SMB constant
isolates the marginal effect of adding RMW and CMA without conflating
it with an SMB definition change. Per-specification sample filtering
is applied: each specification's regression uses the maximum
available sample for its own factor set; a shared filter would
unnecessarily truncate FF6 to the PSL coverage window (through
December 2024 only). The Carhart$+$PSL specification is therefore
estimated on a slightly shorter sample than the Carhart and FF6
specifications. Symmetric Newey--West standard errors at six-month
lag are applied uniformly to actual and bootstrap-simulated
$t$-statistics, matching the main-text procedure. Defensive $-99$
sentinel handling is applied to the raw Pástor file: pre-1968 missing
values (coded as $-99$) are converted to NA at panel preparation; the
period is outside the sample window so there is no effective impact,
but the guard is in place.

\noindent The headline robustness finding is that the active-management
puzzle survives qualitatively under FF6 and Carhart$+$PSL, and indeed

\textcite{BarrasScailletWermers2010} four-way decomposition at the
canonical $\gamma^* = 0.20$ threshold. Pooled EW net alpha is
$-2.12\%$ per annum under Carhart, $-2.26\%$ under FF6, and
$-2.06\%$ under Carhart$+$PSL --- all materially negative and
statistically significant. The BSW decomposition is the most
informative single statistic: at $\gamma^* = 0.20$, the genuinely
unskilled mass $T^-_{\gamma^*}$ stands at $21.1\%$ under Carhart,
rises to $28.2\%$ under FF6 (a $7.2$-percentage-point increase), and
falls marginally to $20.6\%$ under Carhart$+$PSL. The Storey
$\hat{\pi}_0$ estimate moves from $77.5\%$ (Carhart) to $66.0\%$
under FF6 (an $11.5$-percentage-point drop) and to $76.3\%$ under
Carhart$+$PSL (essentially unchanged). The two effects --- a lower
$\hat{\pi}_0$ and a higher $T^-_{\gamma^*}$ under FF6 --- are
complementary: as the model absorbs more of the cross-sectional
return variance into factor exposure, the residual mass that the
estimator attributes to genuine zero-alpha behaviour shrinks, and a
larger fraction of the cross-section is identified as genuinely
unskilled rather than indistinguishable-from-zero.

The intuition is that under the Carhart model, fund alphas embed
unpriced factor-tilt compensation: funds with positive exposures to
RMW and CMA --- a systematic quality tilt toward profitable,
conservatively-investing firms that is common in the industry ---
carry inflated Carhart alphas, while funds with negative exposures
carry depressed ones. When RMW and CMA are added as explicit
regressors, these biases are corrected. Because positive-exposure
funds (whose alphas are corrected downward) are more numerous and
carry larger average bias magnitudes than negative-exposure funds
(whose alphas are partially corrected upward), the net effect on the
cross-sectional distribution is a leftward shift: the pooled EW alpha
falls, a larger fraction of the distribution is exposed as genuinely
unskilled, and $\hat{\pi}_0$ declines as fewer funds remain
indistinguishable from zero. The
Carhart$+$PSL specification produces pooled-alpha and BSW estimates
within $1$--$2$ percentage points of the Carhart specification,
indicating that traded liquidity exposure is not a material
missing-factor explanation for the puzzle. The genuinely skilled
mass $T^+_{\gamma^*}$ remains marginally negative across all three
specifications, consistent with the no-detectable-skill reading of
the main-text findings.

The robustness evidence supports the dissertation's main empirical
argument under all three of the most natural alternative factor-model
specifications. The defensive position is that the puzzle is not
factor-model specific: it survives the addition of the FF5
profitability and investment factors (where it materially
strengthens) and the \textcite{PastorStambaugh2003} liquidity factor
(where it is essentially unchanged).

\noindent This appendix reports two robustness extensions of the
H1--H4 panel regressions of Chapter~\ref{ch:behavioral}. The first

\section{Lagged state-variable specification}
\label{sec:lagged_states}

\noindent The headline H1--H4 specifications adopt the contemporaneous
timing convention of \textcite{BakerWurgler2007}: the sentiment regime
indicator is measured at month $t$, the same month as the dependent
flow variable. The lagged-state robustness specification re-estimates
each hypothesis with the state variable lagged one month, addressing
the concern that contemporaneous timing may absorb a portion of the
endogenous flow response into the regime-indicator coefficient.

\section{Lipper-category-by-year-month fixed-effect specification}
\label{sec:lipper_yearmo_fe}

\noindent The headline specifications for H1, H2, and H3 use fund
fixed effects, identifying the within-fund variation in flow
response to interactions of rank, sentiment, and lottery proxy. An
alternative identifying assumption is that all aggregate
within-style flow shocks should be absorbed: the Lipper-category
$\times$ year-month fixed effects absorb the time-series
co-variation in flow within each style class, leaving only the
cross-fund variation around the style-time mean as the source of
identification. The robustness specification provides this
alternative identification.
