\noindent [First paragraph. The literature intersects three traditions:

\section{The active management puzzle: Framing}
\label{sec:puzzle_framing}

\noindent [First paragraph. Formally state the puzzle: if most active funds

\label{sec:fdr}

\section{Comparison with Fama and French (2010)}
\label{sec:ff_comparison_discussion}

\noindent The FF (2010) sample spans Jan 1984--Sep 2006 (270 months), of
which the present subperiod covers only the second half (141 months).
Differences in point estimates between this appendix and Tables I--III of
FF (2010) should therefore not be attributed to methodological discrepancies
but to (i) the absence of the 1984--1994 window, during which the
cross-section of U.S.\ equity mutual funds exhibited a more favourable mean
alpha than in the post-1995 era, and (ii) sample-construction
differences---this study uses LSEG share-class data with the
\textcite{Evans2010} incubation correction, whereas FF (2010) draw on the
CRSP Mutual Fund Database aggregated to fund-level. The qualitative
conclusions of FF (2010)---that the cross-sectional distribution of
$t(\hat{\alpha})$ lies materially to the left of the bootstrap zero-skill
null, that $\hat{\pi}_0$ is high, and that VW net alphas are negative and
statistically significant---are expected to be reproduced; precise
magnitudes need not be.

\noindent The rolling alpha series in Figure~\ref{fig:rolling_alphas}
exhibits pronounced non-stationarity across the full December
1994--February 2026 sample. To partition the sample into economically
coherent sub-periods without imposing thresholds \textit{a priori}, we
apply the multiple structural break test of \textcite{Bai1998} and
\textcite{Bai2003} to the monthly cross-sectional equal-weighted mean of
the 36-month rolling \textcite{Carhart1997} gross alpha for active funds
--- the same underlying series plotted (after 12-month smoothing) in
Figure~\ref{fig:rolling_alphas}. The test is applied to the raw, unsmoothed
series; smoothing the input would introduce additional moving-average serial
correlation on top of the overlap-induced autocorrelation already present
in rolling-window estimates, overstating the precision of detected breaks.

The model is a pure mean-shift specification,
$y_t = \mu_i + \varepsilon_t$ for $t$ in segment $i$, with a minimum segment
length of 36 months (matching the rolling-window width) and up to five
breaks tested. The optimal number of breaks is selected by the Bayesian
Information Criterion (BIC). Confidence intervals for break dates use
Newey--West heteroskedasticity- and autocorrelation-consistent standard
errors with a data-driven lag, following the bandwidth rule of
\textcite{Andrews1991}.

\noindent The five-break solution selected by BIC identifies four tight,
economically interpretable breaks (Breaks 1--4, CIs spanning at most ten
months each) and one wider break at Jan 2023 (Break 5, CI spanning
31 months). Breaks 1--3 partition the pre-GFC era into its components:
the late-1990s tail (Period 1, $\bar{\alpha} = -0.59\%$), the dot-com
peak (Period 2, $\bar{\alpha} = +1.67\%$), and the post-crash recovery
phase (Period 3, $\bar{\alpha} = -0.80\%$). These oscillations cluster
tightly around zero on net and each regime spans 36--37 months --- below
the 60-month minimum recommended by \textcite{BarrasScailletWermers2010}
for reliable bootstrap inference --- and are therefore collapsed into a
single pre-2006 regime (P1) in the sub-period analysis. Break~4
(Nov 2011, CI: Jun 2011--Feb 2012) marks the onset of persistent alpha
compression and produces the largest regime-mean shift in the sample
($+1.02\%$ to $-1.13\%$). Break~5 (Jan 2023, CI: Dec 2021--Jul 2024)
marks a further intensification of compression; its 36-month post-break
segment is insufficient for separate bootstrap inference and is
consequently pooled with Period~5 in the sub-period tables, though the
break itself is retained in the figure and this table as part of the
formal test output. Accordingly, the sub-period analysis in
Section~\ref{sec:subperiod_panels} uses \textbf{two thresholds}:
\textbf{Dec 2005} (separating the volatile pre-GFC era from the clean
positive-alpha era) and \textbf{Nov 2011} (marking the structural break
in the alpha-generating process).

\chapter{FACTOR MODEL ROBUSTNESS}
\label{app:robustness}

\noindent This appendix re-estimates the core performance results under
two alternative factor specifications to assess robustness of the active
management puzzle to factor model choice. The \textit{Fama--French
6-Factor} model (FF6) augments the \textcite{Carhart1997} four-factor
baseline with the profitability factor (RMW) and investment factor (CMA)
of \textcite{FamaFrench2015}. The \textit{Carhart + PSL} model (C+PSL)
augments the Carhart four-factor model with the traded liquidity factor
of \textcite{PastorStambaugh2003}. The estimation procedure --- full-period
OLS with Newey--West standard errors (6-month lag), the
\textcite{FamaFrench2010} calendar-month bootstrap ($B = 10{,}000$), and
the \textcite{BarrasScailletWermers2010} false-discovery decomposition at
$\gamma^{*} = 0.20$ --- is identical across all three specifications. SMB
is held at the \textcite{FamaFrench1993} construction (single
$2 \times 3$ size--BM sort) throughout; the two SMB definitions correlate
above 0.97 over the sample window. The Carhart + PSL specification is
constrained to December 1994--December 2024 by the current data release
of the Pastor liquidity factor series; the Carhart and FF6 specifications
use the full December 1994--February 2026 window.
